# Section 15: How to Form Your Own Stance on the SSPX
## The SSPX Compendium

> **How to use this section.** This is the final section - the synthesis. The previous fourteen sections have given you the materials: the history, the figures, the canon law, the documents, the doctrinal positions, the cases for and against, the dialectical confrontation, and the technical vocabulary. This section is about putting those materials together into a coherent personal stance. It cannot make the decision for you, and it should not. What it can do is offer a framework - a set of questions, a way of thinking about the evidence, a method for moving from the data to a position you can defend and live with. Read this section last. It assumes you have engaged seriously with §1–§14, particularly §10–§12, where the dialectical work has been done. Read this section slowly. It is short, but every paragraph is intended to do work.

---

## Part I: Why a Framework Matters

Most stances people hold about the SSPX are not formed from the evidence.
They are formed from social context, partisan identification, or initial emotional impressions.
A traditionalist Catholic in a parish where the SSPX is widely admired tends to admire the SSPX.
A mainstream Catholic in a parish where the SSPX is dismissed as schismatic tends to dismiss it.
A person who first encountered the SSPX through Bishop Williamson's Holocaust comments tends to associate the Society with that scandal.
A person who first encountered the SSPX through the Traditional Mass tends to associate it with beauty and reverence.

These contextual factors are powerful but they are not arguments.
A position formed by them is not a stance - it is a reflex.

The purpose of this compendium has been to give you what you need to move past reflex.
By now you have engaged with the actual evidence: what the SSPX claims, what Rome has said and not said, what canon law actually requires, what the relevant documents actually say, what the strongest arguments on each side actually are.
You have the materials to form a *stance* in the genuine sense - a position you have reasoned into, that you can defend with evidence, and that you can hold with intellectual integrity.

The framework that follows is not a method for *deriving* a single correct answer.
It is a method for *organizing the evidence into a coherent personal position*.
Different reasonable people, applying this framework rigorously, will reach different conclusions.
That is not a failure of the framework; it is an honest acknowledgment that the SSPX question is one where serious Catholic minds, looking at the same evidence, reasonably disagree.

What the framework can do is ensure that your conclusion - whatever it is - is *yours* in a meaningful sense: built from the evidence, internally consistent, aware of its own commitments, and accountable to the strongest counterarguments.

## Part II: The Three Levels of Question

The SSPX question is really three questions, and they need to be kept distinct.

### Level 1: Is the SSPX Right?

This is the *substantive* question - whether the SSPX's specific theological positions correspond to Catholic truth.
It includes the questions of §4 (liturgical), §5 (Vatican II), and §6 (doctrinal positions): Is the Traditional Mass theologically superior to the Novus Ordo?
Do specific Vatican II documents contradict prior definitive teaching?
Are the SSPX's doctrinal positions correct?

This is a question about *truth*.
The answer does not depend on the SSPX's institutional choices; it depends on whether their theological claims are accurate.

### Level 2: Was the SSPX's Institutional Response Appropriate?

This is the *prudential* question - whether the SSPX's specific actions (founding outside canonical regularity, consecrating bishops without papal mandate, maintaining the irregular structure) were the right response to the situation they faced.
It includes the questions of §7 (canon law), §10 (case for), §11 (case against), and §12 (debates 1, 2, 8): Was the 1988 act canonically defensible?
Did the state of necessity justify it?
Has the institutional irregularity produced net positive or negative spiritual fruit?
Could the same goals have been achieved within canonical communion?

This is a question about *judgment*.
The answer depends on whether you accept the SSPX's reading of the situation in 1988 and their evaluation of available alternatives.

### Level 3: What Should You Do?

This is the *personal-pastoral* question - whether you, in your specific circumstances, should attend the SSPX, support it, recommend it to others, or maintain distance from it.
It includes the questions of §9 (sacramental questions) and §12 (debate 9): What alternatives are available to you?
What is the state of your own spiritual formation?
What kind of Catholic community will best support your life of faith?

This is a question about *prudence in your own life*.
The answer depends not only on your judgments at Levels 1 and 2 but on your specific situation, vocation, and pastoral needs.

### Why These Levels Must Be Kept Distinct

A common error is to collapse these levels - to conclude from one that the others follow automatically.
They do not.

You can hold that the SSPX is right at Level 1 (correct about Vatican II, correct about the liturgy) while concluding they were wrong at Level 2 (the institutional response was disproportionate or self-defeating).
Many serious traditional Catholics in canonical communion hold exactly this position.

You can hold that the SSPX was wrong at Level 1 (Vatican II is reconcilable, the liturgical reform is defensible) and therefore wrong at Level 2 (their institutional response addressed problems that did not exist as they claimed).
Many mainstream Catholics hold this.

You can hold that the SSPX is mostly right at Level 1 and that their Level 2 response was justified at the time (state of necessity in 1988 was real), but conclude at Level 3 that you personally should attend a canonically regular community where available.
Many sympathetic Catholics hold this.

You can hold that all three answers favor the SSPX - and become a regular SSPX faithful.
You can hold that none of them do - and avoid the SSPX entirely.
And there are positions in between.

The framework that follows works through each level systematically.

## Part III: Working Through Level 1 - Is the SSPX Right Substantively?

A methodological note before the sub-questions.
The work below requires three things in sequence - a structure Fr. Daniel Themann (District Superior of the SSPX District of Australia and New Zealand) lays out as the answer to the lay Catholic who asks "how do I know?"

1. **Learn the faith first.** Without a competent grasp of what Catholicism authentically *is* - its doctrine, its liturgical patrimony, its moral tradition, its sacramental order - no assessment of the *nature* and *gravity* of the post-conciliar situation is possible.
   A reader who arrives at the questions below with only the implicit catechesis of a typical post-conciliar parish will tend to find the situation unremarkable simply because the reference frame for "what the Catholic Church has always taught" is missing.
   Stage 1 is prior even to the study of the crisis itself.

2. **Trace the origins of the crisis.** Once the reference frame is in place, the historical-doctrinal trajectory becomes traceable: Pius X's *Pascendi Dominici Gregis* (1907) identifying and condemning modernism as the synthesis of all heresies; modernism's underground survival between the World Wars (Cardinal Louis Billot, SJ warned Pius XI at the 1923 secret consistory not to call an ecumenical council on precisely the grounds that the modernist current would dominate the drafting); its resurgence as *nouvelle théologie* / neomodernism in the 1940s and 1950s; Pius XII's *Humani Generis* (1950) re-condemning the same currents; the dominance of the formerly suppressed theologians at Vatican II four years after Pius XII's death; the post-conciliar trajectory through the years of the present pontificate.

3. **Apply the principles with moral certainty.** The certainty available in any prudential judgment is *moral certainty* - certainty beyond reasonable doubt, exercised through the virtue of prudence (Aquinas, STh II-II q. 47).
   The reader who insists on *absolute* certainty before acting on what the prior two stages establish has, on the perennial Catholic moral tradition, refused the virtue God has given for the task.
   The image is Themann's: solving the moral question by ignoring the concrete circumstances is like solving a physics problem by assuming a frictionless surface - the answer is precise but it is the wrong answer.
   The companion image (the neighbor's living room): the prudent agent does not need to draw the property line exactly to know he has crossed it; he needs only to recognize that he is now inside the neighbor's house.

The Robinson framing of Level 2 (Part IV opening, below) and the Themann framing of the whole sequence are complementary: Robinson observes that the *principles* of the state of necessity are not what is disputed (they are uncontroversial in the Catholic moral tradition); the dispute is over the *facts*.
Themann observes that judging the facts requires the prudential maturity to act under moral certainty, and that the formation for that maturity has two prerequisites - knowing the faith, and tracing how the crisis has come to be what it is.
The questions in this section assume both prerequisites have been satisfied.

Five sub-questions:

### 1.1 Are the SSPX's liturgical claims about the Novus Ordo substantively correct?

The compendium's analysis (§4, §12 Debate 5) found this is one of the clearest cases of substantive vindication:

- The Traditional Mass was never juridically abrogated - confirmed by Benedict XVI in *Summorum Pontificum* (2007).
- The new Offertory does substitute "blessing" prayers for explicitly sacrificial prayers - textually demonstrable.
- The *pro multis* translation controversy was eventually resolved in the SSPX's favor by Benedict XVI's 2006 directive.
- Cardinal Ratzinger himself acknowledged the unprecedented scope of the post-conciliar reform.
- The decline in belief in the Real Presence (the 31% figure in 2019 Pew) correlates with the period of the reform's implementation.

**Your question:** Do you find the textual and historical evidence sufficient to conclude that the Traditional Mass more adequately expresses Catholic Eucharistic doctrine than the Novus Ordo?
Or do you find the Novus Ordo, properly celebrated, an adequate vehicle for the same doctrine?

### 1.2 Do specific Vatican II documents contradict prior definitive teaching?

The compendium's analysis (§5, §12 Debate 3) found this is the most genuinely contested doctrinal question:

- At the level of texts, *Dignitatis Humanae* §2 and Pius IX's Syllabus propositions 15, 77, 78 read in their plain sense as saying opposite things.
- No magisterial document in sixty years has produced a sustained textual reconciliation.
- The continuity-resolutions are possible but require sophisticated interpretive work.
- The CDF's 2007 *Responses* substantially addressed the *Lumen Gentium* "subsists in" concern but not the religious liberty question.

**Your question:** When you read the relevant texts side-by-side (Pius IX on civil order, *Dignitatis Humanae* on religious liberty), do you find them reconcilable?
If so, what is the reconciliation?
If not, which teaching prevails, and why?

This is the substantive theological question on which everything else turns.
A position on the SSPX that has not faced this specific textual question honestly is not a serious position.

### 1.3 Are the SSPX's doctrinal positions on the Mass, priesthood, and ecclesiology in continuity with the perennial Catholic tradition?

The compendium's analysis (§6) found that the SSPX's doctrinal positions are substantially grounded in identifiable magisterial sources - Trent, Vatican I, the pre-conciliar papal Magisterium.
Their positions on the Mass as propitiatory sacrifice, on the priesthood as *alter Christus*, on the Catholic Church as the one true Church, are not idiosyncratic.

**Your question:** When you examine the SSPX's specific doctrinal positions, do you find them in genuine continuity with the perennial Catholic tradition?
Or do you find that they have hardened or distorted that tradition in some way?

### 1.4 Is the post-conciliar crisis real, and is it connected to the Council?

The compendium's analysis (§6 Position 10, §10) found:

- The crisis is real - documented in every major sociological survey of the past sixty years.
- The crisis correlates with the post-conciliar period in ways that demand explanation.
- Multiple causes contribute (sexual revolution, secularization, catechetical failure, etc.).
- Whether the Council itself is a *cause* of the crisis or merely contemporaneous with it is contested.

**Your question:** Do you accept that the post-conciliar period has produced a genuine crisis in Catholic life?
If so, what is your account of the relationship between that crisis and the Council?

### 1.5 Is the hermeneutic of continuity adequately worked out as applied to the most contested Council passages?

The compendium's analysis (§5, §12 Debate 4) found that the principle is correct but its actual application has been less complete than its defenders sometimes claim.

**Your question:** Do you find the existing continuity-resolutions of *Dignitatis Humanae* sufficient?
Or do you find that the work has not yet been done at the level of texts?

### Provisional Level-1 Conclusion

After working through these five sub-questions, you should be able to characterize your provisional position:

- **Position A (SSPX-aligned on substance):** "The SSPX is substantially correct. Specific Vatican II passages do contradict prior teaching; the post-conciliar crisis is connected to the Council's ambiguities; the traditional Mass more adequately expresses Catholic doctrine; the SSPX's doctrinal positions are in genuine continuity with the perennial tradition."

- **Position B (Mainstream-aligned on substance):** "The SSPX is substantively wrong. The hermeneutic of continuity, properly applied, reconciles the Council with prior teaching; the crisis has causes external to the Council; the traditional Mass and the Novus Ordo both adequately express Catholic doctrine; the SSPX has hardened or distorted certain aspects of tradition."

- **Position C (Mixed):** "The SSPX is right about some things and wrong about others. The liturgical critique has substantive force; the textual concerns about *Dignitatis Humanae* are real; the post-conciliar crisis is genuine. But the SSPX overstates the case in some respects, treats some questions as more settled than they are, and tends toward a totalizing critique that the evidence does not support."

Most thoughtful Catholics who engage the evidence seriously end up in Position C in some form.
Whether you lean toward A or B from within Position C will shape your judgments at Level 2.

## Part IV: Working Through Level 2 - Was the Institutional Response Appropriate?

This is the prudential question.
It depends partly on your Level 1 conclusions but is not determined by them.

A methodological note before proceeding.
Most disagreement about the SSPX at this level is *not* disagreement about the principles of the state of necessity.
The classical doctrine - that human ecclesiastical law admits of exceptions where its observance would frustrate the common good and salvation of souls - is uncontroversial in the Catholic moral tradition (see §10 Part IV; §7 Canon 1323 §4).
The disagreement is over the *facts*: whether the situation of the post-conciliar Church is in fact a state of grave general necessity sufficient to justify the act of 1988 (and the act of July 1, 2026).
Fr. Paul Robinson's framing of this division is exact: "If we're going to disagree, we're going to disagree on whether or not there is a state of necessity, not on the principles that apply when there is a state of necessity."
The work of Level 2 is therefore not theoretical but evidentiary.
The questions below ask you to weigh the facts as you find them.

### 2.1 Was the state of necessity met in 1988?

The compendium's analysis (§7, §10, §11, §12 Debate 2) found this is genuinely contested.
The case in favor depends on accepting Lefebvre's reading of Roman intentions; the case against depends on the existence of alternatives (the May 5 Protocol, the FSSP-style path) that traditional Catholicism could and did take.

**Your question:** Looking at the situation in 1988, do you find that:
(a) Strict canonical necessity was met - there was no viable alternative for preserving the traditional Catholic priesthood and Mass?
(b) Genuine grave concern existed, but the means chosen (consecration without papal mandate) was not the only available means?
(c) The state of necessity was not met - Rome had offered a viable path that Lefebvre prematurely abandoned?

### 2.2 What does the FSSP and similar communities prove?

The compendium's analysis (§11) found that the FSSP, ICKSP, Campos, and similar canonical traditional communities demonstrate that traditional Catholicism *can* be preserved within canonical communion.
This is the strongest empirical counter to the SSPX's institutional argument.

**Your question:** Does the existence and flourishing of these communities demonstrate that the SSPX's institutional response was unnecessary?
Or do you find that the SSPX's distinctive doctrinal posture toward Vatican II makes their irregular position different in kind from the FSSP's canonical position?

### 2.3 What is the net spiritual fruit of the SSPX's irregular status?

The compendium's analysis (§11, §12 Debate 8) found that the SSPX has produced both genuine spiritual fruit (vocations, observant families, doctrinal formation) and genuine spiritual harm (sectarian patterns, the Williamson phenomenon, suspicion toward the visible Church).

**Your question:** When you weigh the fruit and the harm, do you find:
(a) The fruit substantially outweighs the harm; the SSPX has been, on balance, a blessing to the Church?
(b) The fruit and harm are comparable in weight; the SSPX is a complicated case?
(c) The harm substantially outweighs the fruit; the SSPX has, on balance, damaged the broader Catholic community?

### 2.4 Where does responsibility for the failed 2012 negotiations lie?

The compendium's analysis (§11, §12 Debate 10) found that Rome offered minimal terms (acceptance of Vatican II as legitimate Magisterium, with continued theological engagement on specific passages), which the SSPX refused.
The barrier to regularization is on the SSPX's side - though understandably, given the Society's self-understanding.

**Your question:** Was the SSPX right to refuse the 2012 terms?
Could the Society have accepted regularization without compromising its essential mission?
Was Rome's position unreasonable, or was the SSPX's refusal an institutional self-protection that prioritized identity over reconciliation?

### Provisional Level-2 Conclusion

After working through these sub-questions:

- **Position A (Defending the SSPX's institutional response):** "The 1988 consecrations were canonically defensible under the state of necessity; the FSSP path was real but compromised by accepting Council terms the SSPX could not in conscience accept; the spiritual fruit substantially outweighs the harm; the failed 2012 negotiations reflect Rome's inability to offer acceptable terms."

- **Position B (Criticizing the SSPX's institutional response):** "The 1988 consecrations were not strictly necessary; the FSSP demonstrated that tradition could be preserved without irregularity; the SSPX has produced real harm alongside real fruit; the failed 2012 negotiations show the barrier is on the SSPX's side."

- **Position C (Mixed):** "The 1988 act was a defensible judgment in the moment, but the continuing institutional irregularity has become harder to defend; the SSPX has done real good but also produced real problems; regularization should happen, and the SSPX's refusal to accept minimal terms is increasingly hard to justify."

It is possible - and common - to hold Position A at Level 1 (the SSPX is right substantively) and Position B or C at Level 2 (but their institutional response was nonetheless problematic).
The two levels are independent.

## Part V: Working Through Level 3 - What Should You Do?

This is the personal-pastoral question.
It depends on your Level 1 and Level 2 conclusions, but it also depends on factors specific to your own situation that no document can determine for you.

### 3.1 What alternatives are realistically available to you?

The compendium's analysis (§9) found that Rome's guidance has been to prefer fully canonical traditional communities where available - but that the SSPX provides valid sacraments and satisfies the Sunday obligation, and that post-*Traditionis Custodes* alternatives have become significantly reduced in many regions.

**Your question:** What is actually available where you live?
- A diocesan TLM community in good standing under post-*Traditionis Custodes* arrangements?
- An FSSP or ICKSP parish or chapel?
- An SSPX chapel?
- Only Novus Ordo parishes?
- Some combination of these at varying distances?

Your answer to this practical question shapes the answer to everything else.
A Catholic with an FSSP parish twenty minutes away is in a different situation from a Catholic whose only Sunday Mass option within fifty miles is the SSPX.

### 3.2 What is your own spiritual situation?

The compendium's analysis (§9, §11) noted that long-term SSPX engagement can form Catholics in particular ways - some good (rigorous formation, doctrinal seriousness) and some problematic (sectarian dispositions, suspicion of legitimate authority).

**Your question:** Honestly assessed, how would the SSPX form you, given who you are?
- Are you secure enough in your Catholic identity that you can attend the SSPX while maintaining communion with the broader Church and avoiding sectarian dispositions?
- Or are you at risk of forming, through habitual SSPX engagement, attitudes that would gradually distance you from the broader Catholic community in problematic ways?
- Are you the kind of Catholic for whom doctrinal precision is necessary, and is the SSPX's formation more likely to provide that than the alternatives available to you?

### 3.3 What does your vocation and state of life require?

Different states of life create different needs and different responsibilities.

- A young single Catholic considering vocation may have particular questions about where to receive priestly or religious formation.
- A parent has responsibilities for the formation of children that go beyond personal preference - what kind of Catholic community will your children be formed in?
- A married couple may have to navigate differences in liturgical preference and theological judgment with their spouse.
- A convert or returned Catholic has particular formation needs that may or may not be best served by the SSPX's particular intensity.

**Your question:** What does your specific vocation require?
Where will you and those for whom you are responsible be best served spiritually?

### 3.4 What are your obligations to the broader Catholic community?

The compendium's analysis (§11) noted that one of the costs of long-term SSPX engagement can be reduced engagement with the broader Catholic struggles of our age - pro-life work, religious liberty defense, catechetical renewal in dioceses, support for diocesan Catholic life.

**Your question:** What is your relationship to the broader Catholic community, and how would attendance at the SSPX affect it?
Are you in a position where engagement with diocesan or canonically regular Catholic life is important - for your own spiritual formation, for your family, for your apostolate, for your service?

### Provisional Level-3 Conclusion

Three broad pastoral postures are possible:

- **Posture A (Regular SSPX engagement):** "The SSPX is where my spiritual life is best served, where my formation has been deepened, where the sacraments are administered reverently, where my family is formed in the faith. The canonical irregularity is a real cross but does not deprive me of the means of grace. I attend the SSPX as my normal Sunday community while remaining aware of the broader Catholic Church."

- **Posture B (Occasional or supplementary SSPX engagement):** "I attend a canonical Catholic community as my normal Sunday community - diocesan, FSSP, or other - but I attend the SSPX when their offerings (a specific feast day, a retreat, a conference) are spiritually beneficial and when no canonical alternative serves the same need. The SSPX is part of my Catholic life but not my primary community."

- **Posture C (Distance from the SSPX):** "Whatever my judgments at Levels 1 and 2, my actual spiritual life is better served by remaining within canonically regular structures. I attend a diocesan parish, an FSSP community, or another canonical alternative. I respect the SSPX's preservation of tradition and pray for regularization, but I do not personally attend or support the Society institutionally."

All three postures are defensible.
The right posture for you depends on the specifics of your situation, which only you can fully assess.

## Part VI: The Most Common Personal Positions

Based on the framework above, several coherent positions emerge that thoughtful Catholics actually hold.
Recognizing them may help you locate your own.

### Position 1: The Convinced SSPX Catholic

**Level 1:** The SSPX is substantively correct.
Vatican II contains genuine errors; the new Mass is theologically problematic; the post-conciliar crisis is real and connected to the Council.

**Level 2:** The institutional response was justified.
The state of necessity was met in 1988 and continues today; the FSSP took a path that compromised doctrinal integrity; the spiritual fruit vindicates the institutional choices.

**Level 3:** Regular SSPX attendance is appropriate.
The SSPX is the place where Catholic life can be lived in integrity in the current crisis.

This position is internally coherent and is held by many faithful SSPX Catholics.
Its weakness is that it tends to underweight the empirical evidence that traditional Catholicism survives in canonical communion (the FSSP, ICKSP, Campos) and to dismiss too quickly the formation patterns that long-term SSPX engagement can produce.

### Position 2: The Critical Mainstream Catholic

**Level 1:** The SSPX is substantively wrong on the most contested questions.
The hermeneutic of continuity reconciles Vatican II with prior teaching; the new Mass adequately expresses Catholic doctrine when properly celebrated; the post-conciliar crisis has causes external to the Council.

**Level 2:** The institutional response was a serious error.
The state of necessity was not met in 1988; the FSSP demonstrated the alternative; the SSPX has produced harm alongside fruit; the 2012 refusal places the regularization barrier on the SSPX's side.

**Level 3:** Distance from the SSPX is appropriate.
Catholics should attend canonically regular communities; the SSPX should not be normalized as a regular part of Catholic life.

This position is internally coherent and is held by many mainstream Catholics, including many who are theologically conservative.
Its weakness is that it can underestimate the substantive force of the SSPX's specific textual concerns (particularly on *Dignitatis Humanae*) and overstate the adequacy of the existing magisterial reconciliations.

### Position 3: The Sympathetic Traditionalist in Canonical Communion

**Level 1:** The SSPX is substantively right on many things.
The liturgical critique is largely vindicated; the textual concerns about Vatican II are real; the post-conciliar crisis is genuine.

**Level 2:** The institutional response is harder to defend.
Whether or not 1988 was justified in the moment, the continuing institutional irregularity has become difficult to sustain; the FSSP path exists; the spiritual fruit could be obtained within canonical communion.

**Level 3:** Engagement with traditional Catholic life within canonical structures is best.
Attend FSSP, ICKSP, diocesan TLM where available; the SSPX is a complicated case to be respected but not regularly attended; pray for regularization.

This position is probably the most common among serious traditional Catholics in canonical communion.
It takes the SSPX's substantive concerns seriously while declining to follow them institutionally.

### Position 4: The Pragmatic SSPX Attendee

**Level 1:** I'm not certain.
The textual questions about Vatican II are genuinely difficult; I haven't fully worked out my position on the hermeneutic of continuity; I do believe the Traditional Mass is more beautiful and reverent than what's available locally.

**Level 2:** I don't fully judge.
The 1988 consecrations are above my pay grade theologically; I trust Lefebvre acted in good faith; I'm not sure I can adjudicate between him and the FSSP founders.

**Level 3:** Practically, the SSPX is where I can find the Mass and formation my soul needs.
Post-*Traditionis Custodes*, my diocese has no TLM; the nearest FSSP is 90 minutes away; my children are receiving serious Catholic formation at the SSPX school; my own spiritual life is genuinely deeper since I started attending.

This position is probably the most common among ordinary SSPX attendees who have not engaged in extensive theological study.
It is not unreasonable: it acknowledges uncertainty at the higher levels while making a pastoral judgment based on what is actually available and what actually serves the spiritual life.
Its weakness is that it can leave the higher-level questions unresolved indefinitely, which can become problematic over time.

### Position 5: The Cautious Observer

**Level 1:** The substantive questions are too contested for me to take a strong position.
I find the SSPX's textual concerns serious but the mainstream responses also serious.

**Level 2:** I cannot judge whether the institutional response was justified.
I see real fruit and real harm.

**Level 3:** I attend my diocesan parish.
I am open to the Traditional Mass and have attended occasionally.
I respect the SSPX and pray for regularization.
I would consider FSSP or similar communities if they were locally available.
I do not currently attend the SSPX as a regular part of my Catholic life.

This is a defensible position for someone who is genuinely uncertain about the higher-level questions and who is not in a pastoral situation that requires the SSPX.
It is honest about the limits of one's own judgment.

### What These Positions Share

All five positions accept what the compendium establishes as not seriously disputable:

- SSPX sacraments are valid.
- The SSPX is not currently in formal schism by Roman criteria.
- The 1988 excommunications were lifted in 2009.
- The Traditional Mass was never juridically abrogated.
- The post-conciliar period has included a real crisis in Catholic life.
- The SSPX has produced real spiritual fruit.
- The SSPX has also produced real institutional and formational problems.

What distinguishes the positions is how they weigh the contested questions and how they translate those weighings into personal pastoral practice.
None of them is unreasonable.
None of them is the only possible coherent position.

## Part VII: A Final Word on Method

A few principles for holding your position with integrity, whatever it turns out to be.

### Hold your position with the confidence it deserves - and no more.

Some elements of the SSPX question are settled: validity of sacraments, current canonical status, the historical facts of what happened.
Hold these with confidence.

Other elements are genuinely contested: whether *Dignitatis Humanae* contradicts prior teaching, whether the state of necessity was met, whether the SSPX's net fruit is positive.
Hold positions on these with appropriate humility, aware that serious Catholics looking at the same evidence reach different conclusions.

The worst mistake is to hold contested positions with the confidence appropriate to settled ones - and to dismiss those who disagree as fools, schismatics, or modernists.
The compendium has tried throughout to model what it looks like to engage seriously with both sides.
You should do the same.

### Be willing to revise.

The questions surrounding the SSPX have evolved over more than forty years.
Rome's position has shifted from "schismatic act" (1988) through "no formal schism" (Benedict XVI, 2009) to permanent confession faculties (2016) and the *Traditionis Custodes* paradox (2021) - and, in 2026, to a renewed "schismatic act" warning from the DDF and from Pope Leo XIV personally, directed at the episcopal consecrations the Society carried out at Écône on July 1, 2026.
The SSPX has expelled Bishop Williamson (2012), lost Bishop Tissier de Mallerais to death (2024), elected Fr. Pagliarani as Superior General (2018), and consecrated the first new SSPX bishops since 1988.
The situation in 2026 is different from the situation in 1988, and it will be different again in 2030.

A stance held rigidly is a stance that fails as the situation changes.
Hold your position as a working judgment, ready to revise as new evidence and developments emerge.
The compendium itself is meant to be a living document - you can and should update it as the situation evolves.

### The consecrations have occurred: what to watch now.

Your original purpose in commissioning this compendium was to form a stance on the SSPX episcopal consecrations.
They took place at Écône on July 1, 2026: four bishops consecrated by Bishop de Galarreta with Bishop Fellay assisting, against Pope Leo XIV's personal written plea to desist, with the Society disclaiming all jurisdiction and swearing fidelity to the pope within the rite itself [→ §2 Timeline; §7 Canon Law].
The framework above gives you the tools to evaluate the act; what remains open is Rome's response, and the questions to watch are these:

- Will Rome formally declare the excommunications of the six bishops, as it did within a day in 1988, or leave the *latae sententiae* question undeclared?
  As of this writing no decree has issued, and Cardinal Parolin has said he does not know "the timing or the manner in which this excommunication will be imposed."
  Whether Rome declares, and how quickly, will be highly diagnostic of Leo XIV's policy.
- Will the Francis-era confession and marriage faculties be maintained, restricted, or revoked?
  This is the question with the most direct consequences for SSPX faithful.
- Will a reconciliation structure emerge, such as the *Ecclesia Dei*-style commission Cardinal Müller proposed on June 29, and will the doctrinal discussion the Society's June 24 Profession of Faith invites ever take place?
- Will the SSPX treat the consecrations as a platform for continued dialogue (as Pagliarani's filial reply to Leo XIV frames them) or as a step toward institutional self-sufficiency (as its critics read them)?
  The Society's conduct over the next several years will show which reading was right.

The 1988 precedent suggests that the meaning of July 1, 2026 will not be fixed by the act itself but by the decade that follows it: 1988 looked like a definitive rupture and was followed by remission, faculties, and dialogue.
Whether 2026 follows the same arc is the live question, and you should pay attention.

### Hold Rome with filial love - even in the crisis.

One of the practical difficulties of the SSPX question is that it can wound the reader's filial sense toward Rome.
The arguments rehearsed in this compendium - that specific Council documents create real tensions with prior teaching, that Rome's own behavior over four decades has been in tension with itself, that the pope may in specific and grave cases be resisted - can be heard, by the unwary, as licensing a kind of contempt for the See of Peter.
They should not be.

Bishop Bernard Fellay has offered an analogy that the SSPX itself uses to frame this point.
Imagine a parent who is losing his or her mind to Alzheimer's or a similar erosion of the faculties - the parent by whom you were known and the household ordered.
Do you go away?
No. You do not pretend that what the parent says under the disease is sound; you do not endorse confused commands as if they were rational.
But you do not leave.
You take *more* care of the parent in such a condition, not less.
The filial relation intensifies precisely because the parent is hurt.

The SSPX commends a similar interior disposition toward Rome in the present hour: refuse to deny the problem; refuse to leave; *intensify* rather than withdraw filial devotion.
The combination - refusal of error and refusal of distance - is what Fellay calls "heroic," and is what the Society understands itself to be living out.
The Catholic who reaches a different judgment on the underlying questions and attends a diocesan parish rather than an SSPX chapel may still need precisely the same disposition: a refusal to deny the genuine problems in the post-conciliar Church combined with a refusal to abandon the visible Roman Church Christ established.
The temptation either to deny the crisis or to leave is real on every side of this dispute; the Catholic answer is the same in either direction.
*Stay.
And hold the truth.*

There are, in Fellay's own image, two abysses to be avoided: on one side, insulting God by accepting as Catholic what cannot be reconciled with the Catholic faith; on the other, rejecting the Church Christ founded.
The SSPX understands itself to be walking the narrow ridge between them.
Whatever you conclude about the SSPX, the ridge is real, and the discipline of walking it is required of every serious Catholic in the present situation.

## Part VIII: Closing Reflection

The Catholic Church in 2026 is not in a normal situation.
The post-conciliar period has not yet settled into a stable form.
*Traditionis Custodes* (2021) and the questions surrounding the 2025 conclave and the 2026 pontificate of Leo XIV reveal that the Council's reception is still being worked out.
The SSPX is one of the most visible signs of the unsettled situation, but it is not the only one.

The question of the SSPX is, in the end, a question about how the Catholic Church is going to receive the most disrupted Council in her history.
It is a question that will not be settled by individual Catholics forming personal stances.
It will be settled by the gradual work of the Magisterium, the response of the faithful across generations, the action of grace.

Your stance is not the resolution of the question.
It is your faithful participation in the question - the way you, as one Catholic in 2026, choose to inhabit a Church in which questions of this magnitude are still being worked out.

Hold your stance with seriousness.
Hold it with humility.
Hold it with charity toward those who disagree.
And hold it as a faithful Catholic - recognizing that you stand under the authority of the Church Christ founded, in communion with the Bishop of Rome whom Christ Himself appointed as the rock on which the Church is built, accountable to the Tradition that has been handed down from the apostles.

These are the marks of an authentic Catholic stance on the SSPX question: serious engagement with the evidence; humility about contested matters; charity toward those who disagree; fidelity to the Church Christ founded.
Whatever specific position you reach, these are the marks of holding it well.

The SSPX is a community of fellow Catholics - flawed, sometimes wrong, sometimes right, always called to communion.
Your stance toward them, whatever its specific content, should be the stance of one Catholic toward another in a Church that is, despite its present wounds, the Mystical Body of Christ on earth.

May Christ the King - whose Kingship the SSPX has so passionately defended, whose Kingship the Catholic Church has always proclaimed - guide your judgment and your practice.

The Catholic Church will work this out.
So will you.

*Pray for the Pope.*

*Pray for the Society.*

*Pray for the Church.*
