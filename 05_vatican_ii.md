## A Note Before We Begin

The dispute over Vatican II is genuinely difficult, and most popular treatments of it — on all sides — are inadequate. To engage the question seriously, several distinctions must be held in mind from the start.

**The SSPX does not reject Vatican II as a whole.** Lefebvre signed most Council documents. The SSPX accepts perhaps 90–95% of the Council's content without significant qualification. The dispute concerns specific passages in specific documents — primarily *Dignitatis Humanae*, *Unitatis Redintegratio*, *Nostra Aetate*, and certain passages in *Lumen Gentium* and *Gaudium et Spes*. These passages are not the entirety of the Council.

**Vatican II did not define any new dogma.** Unlike all previous ecumenical councils, Vatican II issued no canons with anathemas attached, no formal dogmatic definitions in the technical sense. Pope John XXIII explicitly stated this in his opening address: the Council was to be *pastoral* in character. This has significant consequences for how its texts are to be interpreted and what weight they carry.

**The level of authority of various Council texts varies.** Vatican II produced sixteen documents in three categories — four Constitutions, nine Decrees, and three Declarations. A Constitution (e.g., *Lumen Gentium*) carries greater weight than a Declaration (e.g., *Dignitatis Humanae*). Within each document, dogmatic content carries greater weight than pastoral application.

**The "hermeneutic" question is central.** The Council itself stated that its teachings must be understood in continuity with the prior Magisterium of the Church. Benedict XVI articulated this in his famous 2005 Christmas Address to the Roman Curia, distinguishing the "hermeneutic of continuity" from the "hermeneutic of rupture." How one reads ambiguous passages depends on whether one assumes the Council intended continuity with prior teaching (in which case ambiguities must be resolved in favor of continuity) or rupture (in which case the apparent novelty is taken at face value). The SSPX argues for continuity but holds that certain passages cannot be reconciled with prior teaching even under that reading.

**The prior Magisterium is not optional.** This is essential. Catholic teaching is not whatever the most recent document says. It is the unbroken Tradition extending from the apostles, transmitted by the ordinary and universal Magisterium, defined at councils and by popes, and developed organically. A new document that contradicts a clearly defined prior teaching presents a genuine theological problem — not a question of accepting the newer over the older, but a question of how to interpret the apparent contradiction.

With these clarifications, we proceed.

## Part I: The Hermeneutical Framework

Before examining specific documents, the question of how to *read* Vatican II must be addressed. This is itself a contested question.

### The Hermeneutic of Continuity (Benedict XVI)

In his Christmas Address to the Roman Curia on December 22, 2005, then-newly-elected Pope Benedict XVI articulated what would become the standard mainstream framework for interpreting Vatican II:

> "On the one hand, there is an interpretation that I would call 'a hermeneutic of discontinuity and rupture'; it has frequently availed itself of the sympathies of the mass media, and also one trend of modern theology. On the other, there is the 'hermeneutic of reform,' of renewal in continuity of the one subject-Church which the Lord has given to us. She is a subject which increases in time and develops, yet always remaining the same, the one subject of the journeying People of God."

Benedict argued that the rupture-hermeneutic was a misreading of the Council that had caused much of the post-conciliar damage. The Council, properly understood, taught in continuity with the prior Magisterium. Where its texts appear novel, they must be read as developments of prior teaching, not contradictions.

### The SSPX Position on Hermeneutics

The SSPX accepts the principle that Council documents *should* be read in continuity with Tradition. Their argument is not that the Council intended rupture but that certain passages, even when read with maximum charity for continuity, cannot be reconciled with the prior Magisterium.

Bishop Bernard Tissier de Mallerais, in *Faith Imperiled by Reason: Benedict XVI's Hermeneutics* (English ed. 2010), articulates the SSPX framework:

> "We accept the hermeneutic of continuity in principle. But continuity is a fact, not merely a method of interpretation. If a text genuinely contradicts prior teaching, no hermeneutical method can make it continuous. Where genuine contradiction exists, the obligation of the Catholic is to hold to the prior, definitive teaching."

The SSPX's claim is therefore narrower than is often supposed. They do not reject the Council as a whole. They do not reject the hermeneutic of continuity. They claim that specific passages, on examination, genuinely cannot be made continuous with prior definitions — and that in such cases, the prior definitive teaching prevails.

### The Question of Authority

The level of authority a Council document possesses determines what response is owed to it. Catholic theology distinguishes:

- **Solemnly defined dogmas** — irreformable; required of divine and Catholic faith (*de fide divina et catholica*). Vatican II did not produce any of these in the strict sense.
- **Doctrines of the ordinary universal Magisterium** — taught by the Church universally as belonging to the deposit of faith; firmly held.
- **Authoritative teaching of the Magisterium** — formally taught by competent authority; religious submission of will and intellect owed.
- **Pastoral and disciplinary teaching** — practical applications; obedience owed but not infallibility presumed.

Vatican II's most contested passages fall primarily into the third and fourth categories. *Dignitatis Humanae*, for instance, is a Declaration — the lowest of the three categories of Council documents. Its religious-liberty teaching is authoritative but not, in the technical sense, dogmatically defined.

The SSPX argument exploits this distinction. They argue that they owe "religious submission of will and intellect" to authoritative non-defined teaching — but that this submission is not absolute and may be withheld when the teaching appears to contradict prior dogmatic definitions.

This is a contested theological claim. Critics argue that "religious submission" requires acceptance even when one cannot perfectly reconcile the teaching with prior tradition; one should presume the Magisterium is correct and that the apparent contradiction is one's own failure of understanding. The SSPX responds that this principle, taken absolutely, would eliminate the possibility of any genuine theological judgment and would make all post-conciliar teaching by definition correct.

### *Lumen Gentium* §21 and the Conflation of Order and Jurisdiction

A particular passage of the Council that has become load-bearing in the 2026 schism dispute is *Lumen Gentium* §21:

> "Episcopal consecration, together with the office of sanctifying, also confers the office of teaching and of governing, which, however, of its very nature, can be exercised only in hierarchical communion with the head and the members of the college."
>
> — *Lumen Gentium* §21 (November 21, 1964)

[Vatican.va — *Lumen Gentium*](https://www.vatican.va/archive/hist_councils/ii_vatican_council/documents/vat-ii_const_19641121_lumen-gentium_en.html)

The §21 formulation is structurally novel. The perennial Catholic teaching, codified at Pius XII's *Ad Sinarum Gentem* §12 (October 7, 1954) and at 1917 CIC Canons 108–109, was that the Church possesses *two* distinct hierarchies — the hierarchy of *orders* (conferred by sacred ordination) and the hierarchy of *jurisdiction* (conferred by canonical mission from the Roman Pontiff). On the perennial reading, a bishop receives the *power of orders* by sacramental consecration but receives *jurisdiction* only by direct papal grant. §21 alters this framework: episcopal consecration now itself "confers the office of teaching and of governing," with hierarchical communion supplying the condition for *exercise* rather than the source of the *power* itself. [→ §7 for the canonical history of this distinction and the 1951 / 1958 Pius XII codifications.]

The pivot did not go unnoticed at the time. Joseph Ratzinger — the post-conciliar Council's most prominent theological commentator — explicitly identified §21 as a *change* from the perennial teaching. In the 1966 essay collection that became the English-language *Theological Highlights of Vatican II*, the then-Father Ratzinger wrote:

> "An evolution of a scope difficult to foresee is expressed in these affirmations [on the origins of episcopal power]. The rigid line of demarcation that had stood for centuries between the power of order and the power of jurisdiction in the spirit of most Western theologians has become passible. The strong bond between these two realities, which at bottom are only one, appears to view."

Ratzinger added that the same Council shift was "the point of departure that the law took apropos of the structure of the Latin church in the second millennium as compared to the first" — that is, that the pontifical-mandate requirement for episcopal consecration (the Latin-church-centralization development of the second millennium) had, in his judgment, "threatened to suffocate the collegial feeling of the patristic era."

The pivot has direct doctrinal consequences for the 2026 schism dispute. On the §21 reading, the consecration of a bishop without papal mandate is the conferral of a share in the Church's governance against the will of the head of the college — and is therefore intrinsically schismatic. On the perennial reading codified at *Ad Sinarum Gentem* §12, *Ad Apostolorum Principis* §39, 1917 CIC Canons 108–109, and articulated by Cardinal St. Robert Bellarmine in *De Ecclesia*, the consecration of a bishop without papal mandate confers the *power of orders* alone, leaves the *jurisdiction* untouched (the Roman Pontiff alone gives or withholds it), and is therefore a disorder in the administration of a sacrament rather than an attack on the unity of the Church. The 2026 SSPX case for the consecrations turns precisely on this perennial reading; the 2026 curial case against them turns precisely on the §21 reading. [→ §7 Canon Law; §10 Part VIII.]

## Part II: *Dignitatis Humanae* — The Declaration on Religious Liberty

This is the document the SSPX considers most problematic — and the document around which the most substantive theological dispute centers.

### The Document

**Title:** *Dignitatis Humanae* — Declaration on Religious Freedom
**Date promulgated:** December 7, 1965
**Genre:** Declaration (lowest of the three categories)
**Vote at promulgation:** 2,308 placet, 70 non placet
**Vatican.va:** [English](https://www.vatican.va/archive/hist_councils/ii_vatican_council/documents/vat-ii_decl_19651207_dignitatis-humanae_en.html)

### The Document's Central Teaching

*Dignitatis Humanae* §2:

> "This Vatican Council declares that the human person has a right to religious freedom. This freedom means that all men are to be immune from coercion on the part of individuals or of social groups and of any human power, in such wise that no one is to be forced to act in a manner contrary to his own beliefs, whether privately or publicly, whether alone or in association with others, within due limits.
>
> The council further declares that the right to religious freedom has its foundation in the very dignity of the human person as this dignity is known through the revealed word of God and by reason itself. This right of the human person to religious freedom is to be recognized in the constitutional law whereby society is governed and thus it is to become a civil right."

### What the SSPX Accepts in *Dignitatis Humanae*

The SSPX accepts that:

- No one should be physically coerced into accepting the Catholic faith. This was always Catholic teaching.
- Civil authorities should not directly force conversion. The Church has consistently held that faith must be free.
- A certain degree of civil tolerance for non-Catholic religions may be permitted as a prudential matter, especially in religiously plural societies.

The principle that "faith must be free" — that one cannot be forced to believe — is ancient Catholic teaching. It is found in St. Thomas Aquinas (ST II-II, q. 10, a. 8), in numerous papal documents, and in canon law (the requirement that baptism be received voluntarily).

### What the SSPX Objects To

The SSPX objects to a specific claim in *Dignitatis Humanae*: that religious liberty is a **natural right** rooted in the **dignity of the human person**, that it is to be **recognized in constitutional law as a civil right**, and that this right extends to the **public profession and propagation** of false religions.

Lefebvre articulated the objection most fully in *They Have Uncrowned Him* (1987):

> "Religious liberty, as defined by *Dignitatis Humanae*, claims that every human person has a natural right — based on the dignity of his nature — to be free from coercion in religious matters, even when his religion is objectively false. This means that error has a right to public expression. But error has no rights. Only persons have rights. And persons have the right to know the truth and to follow the truth, not to be confirmed in error."

The SSPX position can be summarized as follows:

1. The right to religious freedom is, in the document, presented as a natural right rooted in human dignity.
2. This right is said to apply equally to true and false religions in their civil expression.
3. The Catholic state — historically endorsed by the Magisterium as the ideal political order — is therefore implicitly denied; civil authorities are required to treat true and false religions equally.
4. This contradicts prior definitive papal teaching, particularly Gregory XVI and Pius IX, which condemned this very position.

### The Prior Magisterium on Religious Liberty

The SSPX's argument depends on what previous popes said on this specific question. The relevant documents are extensive; the most important follow.

**Pope Gregory XVI, *Mirari Vos* (1832):**

> "We come now to another and most fruitful cause of evils by which, We lament, the Church is at present afflicted, namely, *indifferentism*, or that base opinion which has become prevalent everywhere through the deceit of wicked men, that the eternal salvation of the soul can be acquired by any profession of faith whatsoever, provided that morals are conformed to the standard of justice and probity...
>
> From this poisoned source of indifferentism flows that absurd and erroneous opinion, or rather insanity, that *liberty of conscience* must be claimed and defended for everyone."

[Papal Encyclicals Online — *Mirari Vos](https://www.papalencyclicals.net/greg16/g16mirar.htm)* (English); [Vatican.va — *Mirari Vos](https://www.vatican.va/content/gregorius-xvi/it/documents/encyclica-mirari-vos-15-augusti-1832.html)* (Italian)

**Pope Pius IX, *Quanta Cura* (1864):**

The encyclical condemned, among other propositions:

> "Contrary to the doctrine of Sacred Scripture, of the Church, and of the holy Fathers, [some] do not hesitate to assert that *'the best constitution of public society and (also) civil progress altogether require that human society be conducted and governed without regard being had to religion any more than if it did not exist; or, at least, without any distinction being made between the true religion and false ones.'*
>
> And, against the doctrine of Scripture, of the Church, and of the Holy Fathers, they do not hesitate to assert, that 'the best theory of civil society requires that popular schools open to children of every class of the people, and, generally, all public institutes intended for instruction in letters and philosophical sciences and for carrying on the education of youth, should be freed from all ecclesiastical authority, control and interference, and should be fully subjected to the civil and political power at the pleasure of the rulers, and according to the standard of the prevalent opinions of the age.'"

The accompanying **Syllabus of Errors** (1864) listed condemned propositions including:

> "**Proposition 15:** Every man is free to embrace and profess that religion which, guided by the light of reason, he shall consider true. *— Condemned.*"
>
> "**Proposition 77:** In the present day it is no longer expedient that the Catholic religion should be held as the only religion of the State, to the exclusion of all other forms of worship. *— Condemned.*"
>
> "**Proposition 78:** Hence it has been wisely decided by law, in some Catholic countries, that persons coming to reside therein shall enjoy the public exercise of their own peculiar worship. *— Condemned.*"
>
> "**Proposition 80:** The Roman Pontiff can, and ought to, reconcile himself, and come to terms with progress, liberalism and modern civilization. *— Condemned.*"

[Vatican.va — *Quanta Cura](https://www.vatican.va/content/pius-ix/la/documents/encyclica-quanta-cura-8-decembris-1864.html)*
[Papal Encyclicals Online — Syllabus of Errors](https://www.papalencyclicals.net/pius09/p9syll.htm)

**Pope Leo XIII, *Libertas Praestantissimum* (1888):**

> "Justice therefore forbids, and reason itself forbids, the State to be godless; or to adopt a line of action which would end in godlessness — namely, to treat the various religions (as they call them) alike, and to bestow upon them promiscuously equal rights and privileges. Since, then, the profession of one religion is necessary in the State, that religion must be professed which alone is true, and which can be recognized without difficulty, especially in Catholic States, because the marks of truth are, as it were, engraved upon it."

[Vatican.va — *Libertas](https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiii/en/encyclicals/documents/hf_l-xiii_enc_20061888_libertas.html)*

**Pope Pius IX, *Quanta Cura* (1864), explicit condemnation:**

> "From which totally false idea of social government they do not fear to foster that erroneous opinion, most fatal in its effects on the Catholic Church and the salvation of souls, called by Our Predecessor, Gregory XVI, an 'insanity' (deliramentum), namely, that 'liberty of conscience and worship is each man's personal right.'"

The phrase *deliramentum* — "insanity," "madness" — applied to the very proposition (right of liberty of worship as a personal right) that *Dignitatis Humanae* would later seem to affirm, is striking.

### Comparing the Texts

The SSPX's argument is that *Dignitatis Humanae* §2 and these prior papal teachings cannot be reconciled. Consider the formulations side by side:

**Pius IX (1864):** The proposition that "every man is free to embrace and profess that religion which, guided by the light of reason, he shall consider true" is **condemned** (Syllabus, prop. 15).

**Vatican II (1965):** "The human person has a right to religious freedom... [which means] no one is to be forced to act in a manner contrary to his own beliefs, whether privately or publicly, whether alone or in association with others, within due limits" (*Dignitatis Humanae* §2).

**The apparent contradiction:** Pius IX condemns the claim that man is "free to embrace and profess that religion which... he shall consider true." Vatican II affirms a right to be free in religious matters "privately or publicly, whether alone or in association with others." These appear to say opposite things on the same question.

### The Defense of *Dignitatis Humanae*

Defenders of *Dignitatis Humanae* offer several responses:

**1. The continuity argument (preferred by Benedict XVI and most magisterial defenders).**

Pius IX was condemning *religious indifferentism* — the claim that all religions are equally true. *Dignitatis Humanae* is teaching *civil liberty* — the claim that civil authorities should not coerce religious belief. These are distinct questions. Pius IX held (rightly, on this reading) that no one has a moral right to embrace false religion. Vatican II holds (also rightly) that no one should be civilly coerced about religion. These positions are compatible.

**2. The "due limits" argument.**

*Dignitatis Humanae* §2 grants religious liberty "within due limits." The Council leaves it to natural and revealed law to determine what those limits are. A Catholic state may, on this reading, still place limits on the public exercise of false religion — perhaps quite stringent limits. The Council has not endorsed unlimited religious liberty.

**3. The development argument.**

What appeared to Pius IX, in the context of 19th-century anti-clerical liberalism, as a threat to the faith, looks different in the 20th century after the experience of totalitarianism. The Church recognized, particularly in the experience of Communist persecution, that religious freedom protects the Church's own ability to teach the faith. *Dignitatis Humanae* represents not a contradiction of prior teaching but a development informed by historical experience.

**4. The personalist argument.**

Pius IX was reasoning about religion in the abstract; Vatican II reasons about the human person. The dignity of the person — created in the image of God, endowed with reason and conscience — requires that he be free from coercion in his approach to religious truth. This is not a denial that there is religious truth; it is an affirmation that the human person must come to that truth freely.

### The SSPX Responses to These Defenses

The SSPX's responses to these defenses are extensive. The most important:

**On the continuity argument:**

The SSPX argues that *Dignitatis Humanae* is doing something different from merely teaching that no one should be physically coerced into faith. It is teaching that the human person has a *natural right* to religious freedom rooted in *the dignity of the human person* — and that this right extends to public profession.

Pius IX did not deny that civil authorities should refrain from forcing private religious belief. He denied that there was a *natural right* to publicly profess any religion one wished, regardless of truth. *Dignitatis Humanae*, in claiming such a natural right, is teaching the very proposition Pius IX condemned.

**On the "due limits" argument:**

The SSPX notes that while *Dignitatis Humanae* mentions "due limits," its overall framework treats religious liberty as the normative principle, with limits as exceptions to be justified. The historical Catholic position treated the opposite as normative: civil endorsement of truth as the principle, with toleration of error as the prudential exception.

**On the development argument:**

The SSPX accepts that doctrine can develop. They deny that development can include contradiction. A teaching may be more fully understood, more precisely formulated, more richly elaborated — but a previously condemned proposition cannot, by development, become an affirmed proposition.

**On the personalist argument:**

The SSPX argues that the personalist framework — while not in itself wrong — has been used to displace the prior framework grounded in the objective order of truth. The dignity of the person comes from his ordering to truth; it does not give him a right against truth. A man's dignity requires that he be allowed to approach truth freely — not that he be permitted to embrace and propagate falsehood as a civil right.

**On magisterial weight:**

The SSPX argues that the specific weight a teaching carries depends on how it has been taught. A doctrine taught universally by the ordinary magisterium across a long period — by popes, by bishops, by the catechisms and theology manuals of the Church, with the moral unanimity of Catholic authorities — approaches the infallibility proper to the universal ordinary magisterium (Vatican I, *Dei Filius*; Vatican II, *Lumen Gentium* §25). The prior teaching on the religious obligations of the civil order — *Mirari Vos*, *Quanta Cura*, the Syllabus, *Immortale Dei*, *Libertas Praestantissimum*, and confirmed in *Quas Primas* — meets this test. A Conciliar Declaration of the lowest formal category cannot, on the SSPX's reading, overturn what the universal ordinary magisterium has consistently taught for more than a century. The teaching may be developed, refined, clarified — but its substance cannot be reversed without surrendering the doctrine of magisterial continuity itself.

### Two Historical Exchanges Documenting the Impasse

The doctrinal dispute over religious liberty was not academic. It was engaged in two documented direct encounters between Lefebvre and Roman authorities, both of which the SSPX considers paradigmatic of why a merely practical regularization could not resolve the question.

**September 11, 1976 — Castel Gandolfo. Lefebvre and Paul VI.**

During Lefebvre's private audience with Paul VI on September 11, 1976 — only weeks after the Society's canonical suppression and his suspension *a divinis* — Lefebvre attempted to put the doctrinal question directly to the pope. He cited the prior popes who had taught the Catholic doctrine of the religious obligations of the civil order, and asked the pope which teaching he was now bound to follow when the recent teaching appeared to contradict the older. According to the transcript of the audience drawn up afterwards by Cardinal Giovanni Benelli, then *Sostituto* of the Secretariat of State, and according to Lefebvre's own account given to the seminarians of Écône on September 12 and 18, 1976, Paul VI declined to engage:

> "Let us leave that aside. We are not here to discuss theology."
>
> — Paul VI, audience of September 11, 1976, Castel Gandolfo (Benelli transcript)

The exchange — brief, polite, and final — captures the SSPX's claim that the doctrinal question has never been engaged at the level of doctrine by the post-conciliar Roman authorities; the response has consistently been to bracket the question rather than answer it.

**July 14, 1987 — Rome. Lefebvre and Cardinal Ratzinger.**

Eleven years later, with negotiations toward a regularization underway and the consecrations not yet a settled decision, Lefebvre met with Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, Prefect of the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith. The meeting addressed the *Dignitatis Humanae* question directly. Ratzinger argued for the legitimacy of the Council's teaching as a development from the prior magisterium; Lefebvre argued that the prior universal teaching could not simply be set aside as erroneous without surrendering the continuity of the Magisterium itself. According to Bishop Bernard Fellay's recollection (publicly recounted in 2026), Lefebvre at one point pressed Ratzinger:

> "But Your Eminence, you cannot oppose fifteen hundred years of the Church. It is not only a teaching — it is also a practice. The relation between Church and state, the Catholic state, is really a practice. You cannot say it was wrong."

### Where the Dispute Stands

The dispute over *Dignitatis Humanae* is the central theological question dividing the SSPX from Rome. Three things can be said with confidence:

- **The texts read in their plain sense create a serious tension with prior magisterial documents.** This is acknowledged by serious theologians on multiple sides of the debate.
- **The continuity-resolutions, while possible, require sophisticated interpretive work.** They do not flow from the plain reading of either set of texts.
- **No subsequent papal document has formally addressed the apparent contradiction.** Benedict XVI, in his Christmas Address, asserted continuity as a hermeneutical principle but did not provide a detailed reconciliation of the specific texts.

This is the unresolved doctrinal question. The 2009 Doctrinal Discussions between Rome and the SSPX focused substantially on religious liberty without producing agreement. It remains the central barrier to regularization.

## Part III: *Unitatis Redintegratio* — The Decree on Ecumenism

### The Document

**Title:** *Unitatis Redintegratio* — Decree on Ecumenism
**Date promulgated:** November 21, 1964
**Genre:** Decree
**Vatican.va:** [English](https://www.vatican.va/archive/hist_councils/ii_vatican_council/documents/vat-ii_decree_19641121_unitatis-redintegratio_en.html)

### The Document's Central Teaching

*Unitatis Redintegratio* introduces the Catholic Church's formal engagement with the modern ecumenical movement — the effort to restore Christian unity among separated Christians.

Key passages:

**On separated Christian communities (§3):**

> "But even in spite of [the obstacles to communion], it remains true that all who have been justified by faith in Baptism are members of Christ's body, and have a right to be called Christian, and so are correctly accepted as brothers by the children of the Catholic Church...
>
> Moreover, some and even very many of the significant elements and endowments which together go to build up and give life to the Church itself, can exist outside the visible boundaries of the Catholic Church: the written word of God; the life of grace; faith, hope and charity, with the other interior gifts of the Holy Spirit, and visible elements too. All of these, which come from Christ and lead back to Christ, belong by right to the one Church of Christ."

**On the role of separated communities in salvation (§3):**

> "The brethren divided from us also use many liturgical actions of the Christian religion. These most certainly can truly engender a life of grace in ways that vary according to the condition of each Church or Community. These liturgical actions must be regarded as capable of giving access to the community of salvation."

### What the SSPX Accepts

The SSPX accepts:

- Christians outside the Catholic Church may be in genuine subjective good faith.
- Such Christians may receive grace in various ways — through their baptism (if validly performed), through their prayers, through their reading of Scripture.
- The Catholic Church has the duty to seek the return of separated Christians to full communion.
- A non-Catholic Christian who is invincibly ignorant of the truth of the Catholic faith and dies in a state of grace can be saved (this was always Catholic teaching).

### What the SSPX Objects To

The SSPX objects to specific aspects of *Unitatis Redintegratio*'s ecumenical framework:

**1. The claim that separated communities are means of salvation.**

The passage that says non-Catholic liturgical actions "must be regarded as capable of giving access to the community of salvation" appears to imply that non-Catholic communities, as such, are means of salvation. The SSPX argues that this contradicts prior teaching: salvation comes through the Catholic Church alone, and non-Catholics may be saved only insofar as they are, by invincible ignorance, ordered toward the Catholic Church which they would join if they knew the truth.

**2. The shift from "reunion" to "ecumenism."**

The prior Catholic approach to non-Catholic Christians was "reunion" — the call for them to return to the Catholic Church, which alone is the true Church. *Unitatis Redintegratio* shifts to "ecumenism" — a process of mutual learning and convergence in which Catholics and non-Catholics together discover the fullness of unity. The SSPX argues this implies a parity between Catholic and non-Catholic Christianity that is incompatible with the Catholic claim to be the one true Church.

**3. Specific applications in subsequent practice.**

The document opened the door to practices the SSPX considers incompatible with Catholic identity: shared worship with Protestants and Orthodox in some circumstances, Catholic participation in Protestant prayer services, etc.

### The Prior Magisterium on Ecumenism

The relevant prior teaching is most clearly articulated in Pope Pius XI's encyclical *Mortalium Animos* (1928):

> "These pan-Christians who turn their minds to uniting the churches seem, indeed, to pursue the noblest of ideas in promoting charity among all Christians: nevertheless how does it happen that this charity tends to injure faith?...
>
> So, Venerable Brethren, it is clear why this Apostolic See has never allowed its subjects to take part in the assemblies of non-Catholics: for the union of Christians can only be promoted by promoting the return to the one true Church of Christ of those who are separated from it... The unity of Christians cannot be otherwise obtained than by securing the return of the separated to the one true Church of Christ from which they once unhappily withdrew."

[Vatican.va — *Mortalium Animos](https://www.vatican.va/content/pius-xi/en/encyclicals/documents/hf_p-xi_enc_19280106_mortalium-animos.html)*

The contrast between *Mortalium Animos* and *Unitatis Redintegratio* is striking:

- *Mortalium Animos*: Christian unity comes through *return* of separated Christians to the Catholic Church.
- *Unitatis Redintegratio*: Christian unity is to be sought through *ecumenical dialogue* and mutual recognition.

### The Defense of *Unitatis Redintegratio*

Defenders argue:

**1. The "subsists in" framework (from *Lumen Gentium*).**

*Lumen Gentium* §8 states that the Church of Christ "subsists in" the Catholic Church — implying that genuine elements of the Church may exist outside its visible boundaries while the fullness of the Church is found only in Catholicism. This framework allows the affirmation that:

- The Catholic Church is the one true Church.
- Genuine ecclesial elements exist outside it.
- Non-Catholic Christians may be in real, if imperfect, communion with the true Church through these elements.

**2. The personal/ecclesial distinction.**

*Mortalium Animos* was concerned with formal Catholic participation in the early Protestant-led ecumenical movement, which Pius XI rightly perceived as treating Catholic and Protestant Christianity as equivalent partners. *Unitatis Redintegratio* operates from a different framework: the Catholic Church remains the one true Church, but engages with separated Christians both as individuals (recognizing their baptism, faith, and possible salvation) and as communities (acknowledging the genuine ecclesial elements they possess).

**3. The development of recognition of valid baptism.**

The Catholic Church has always recognized the validity of Protestant baptisms when properly performed. *Unitatis Redintegratio* extends this principle to other genuine elements found in Protestant communities, without thereby denying the unique status of the Catholic Church.

### The SSPX Response

The SSPX responds:

- The shift from "return" to "ecumenical dialogue" is not merely terminological. It implies a fundamentally different conception of what is happening when non-Catholics consider Catholic claims.
- Post-conciliar ecumenism in practice has often led to *de facto* indifferentism, even where the formal documents maintain the Catholic Church's unique identity.
- The Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification (1999) — in which Catholics and Lutherans declared substantial agreement on a doctrine that had been the central dispute of the Reformation — is presented by SSPX critics as an example of how post-conciliar ecumenism has obscured rather than clarified Catholic doctrine.

### The Project in the Words of the Popes

A separate question from the document itself is what the post-conciliar pontiffs have understood the ecumenical project to mean and to require. > "In this spirit, we reject every form of proselytism, every attitude which would or could be perceived as a lack of respect."

### Where the Dispute Stands

The ecumenism dispute is less philosophically sharp than the religious liberty dispute, but more pastorally consequential. Many of the post-conciliar developments most distressing to traditionalists — interreligious prayer services, Catholic participation in non-Catholic worship, the apparent equalization of Christian denominations — flow from the framework of *Unitatis Redintegratio*.

The defense that the document maintains Catholic identity while engaging ecumenically is theologically defensible; the lived practice of post-conciliar ecumenism — and the pontifical statements interpreting it across four pontificates — has consolidated in a direction many readers find harder to reconcile with the prior magisterium than the document itself would have suggested.

## Part IV: *Nostra Aetate* — The Declaration on Non-Christian Religions

### The Document

**Title:** *Nostra Aetate* — Declaration on the Relation of the Church to Non-Christian Religions
**Date promulgated:** October 28, 1965
**Genre:** Declaration (the lowest of the three categories)
**Vatican.va:** [English](https://www.vatican.va/archive/hist_councils/ii_vatican_council/documents/vat-ii_decl_19651028_nostra-aetate_en.html)

### The Document's Central Teaching

*Nostra Aetate* addresses the Catholic Church's relationship to non-Christian religions — particularly Judaism, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism.

Key passages:

**On non-Christian religions in general (§2):**

> "The Catholic Church rejects nothing that is true and holy in these religions. She regards with sincere reverence those ways of conduct and of life, those precepts and teachings which, though differing in many aspects from the ones she holds and sets forth, nonetheless often reflect a ray of that Truth which enlightens all men. Indeed, she proclaims, and ever must proclaim Christ 'the way, the truth, and the life' (John 14:6), in whom men may find the fullness of religious life, in whom God has reconciled all things to Himself."

**On Islam (§3):**

> "The Church regards with esteem also the Moslems. They adore the one God, living and subsisting in Himself; merciful and all-powerful, the Creator of heaven and earth, who has spoken to men; they take pains to submit wholeheartedly to even His inscrutable decrees, just as Abraham, with whom the faith of Islam takes pleasure in linking itself, submitted to God."

**On Judaism (§4):**

> "True, the Jewish authorities and those who followed their lead pressed for the death of Christ; still, what happened in His passion cannot be charged against all the Jews, without distinction, then alive, nor against the Jews of today. Although the Church is the new people of God, the Jews should not be presented as rejected or accursed by God, as if this followed from the Holy Scriptures."

### What the SSPX Accepts

The SSPX accepts:

- All persons, regardless of religion, are made in the image of God and deserve respect.
- Catholics should not engage in hatred or contempt toward Jews, Muslims, or any other religious group.
- The collective guilt thesis — that all Jews of all times are responsible for Christ's death — is rejected, as it had been by sound Catholic theology before the Council.
- Non-Christian religions may contain elements of natural truth (the existence of God, the moral law, the recognition of human moral obligation) that prepare for the Gospel.

### What the SSPX Objects To

**1. The framework of "religions" containing "rays of truth."**

The SSPX argues that *Nostra Aetate*'s framework — describing other religions as containing "rays of that Truth" — risks suggesting that non-Christian religions are themselves means of approach to God. The Catholic position is that natural reason can know certain truths about God (the existence of one God, the moral law); the *religions* of non-Christians, as positive religious systems, are largely human constructions or, in some cases, vehicles of false worship.

**2. The treatment of Islam.**

The statement that Muslims "adore the one God, living and subsisting in Himself" is taken by the SSPX as theologically imprecise. While Muslims affirm monotheism, the Muslim conception of God explicitly denies the Trinity — making the Muslim "Allah" significantly different from the Christian God. The SSPX argues that to say Catholics and Muslims worship the same God obscures crucial doctrinal differences.

**3. The Jewish question.**

The SSPX accepts that collective guilt for the death of Christ should not be attributed to Jews of any era. But they note that *Nostra Aetate* is silent on the relationship between Judaism after Christ and the Church — historically, Catholic teaching held that with the coming of Christ, the Mosaic covenant gave way to the New Covenant, and that practicing Jews of the present age, like all others outside the Church, must come to Christ for salvation. *Nostra Aetate*'s warm tone toward Judaism has, in subsequent decades, led to formulations (notably in some post-conciliar statements) suggesting that Jews need not embrace Christ — a position the SSPX argues directly contradicts the Catholic faith.

### The Prior Magisterium on Non-Christian Religions

The relevant background includes:

- **The doctrine *Extra Ecclesiam nulla salus*** — "Outside the Church there is no salvation." Defined at the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) — "*Una vero est fidelium universalis Ecclesia, extra quam nullus omnino salvatur*" ("There is one universal Church of the faithful, outside which no one at all is saved") — and reaffirmed in the strongest terms at the Council of Florence, Bull *Cantate Domino* (February 4, 1442): "The Holy Roman Church firmly believes, professes and preaches that none of those existing outside the Catholic Church, not only pagans but also Jews and heretics and schismatics, can have eternal life, but will go into the eternal fire which was prepared for the devil and his angels, unless before death they are joined with Her."
- **Pope Pius IX in *Quanto Conficiamur Moerore* (1863)** modified this for the modern context: those invincibly ignorant of the true religion, who follow the natural law and the will of God as best they know it, may be saved by extraordinary grace. But the salvation, in such cases, comes from Christ and through the Church, not from the false religion they nominally professed.
- **Pope Pius XII in *Mystici Corporis* (1943)** elaborated: those outside the visible Catholic Church may be "ordered to" the Church by an unconscious desire (*votum implicitum*), and so saved through her. The Church remains the one ark of salvation; non-Catholics are saved through her, not despite her.

The Catholic teaching has consistently been: salvation comes from God through Christ through the Catholic Church; those outside the visible Church may be saved only insofar as they are ordered toward her by invincible ignorance and good will.

### The Defense of *Nostra Aetate*

Defenders argue:

- *Nostra Aetate* makes no statement about salvation outside Christianity. It addresses the relationship of the Church to other religions, not the question of salvation through them.
- The "rays of truth" formulation is consistent with the Catholic tradition that natural reason can attain to some truths about God.
- The warm tone toward Judaism reflects the historical reality that Catholic-Jewish relations had been marked by tragic violence and contempt for centuries; the Council appropriately addressed this with a tone of repentance and respect.
- The Council was speaking pastorally, not dogmatically. It does not address — and does not need to address — the theological questions about what these religions are in their essence.

### The Post-Conciliar Trajectory — From Assisi (1986) to Abu Dhabi (2019)

The SSPX's case at §5 Part IV does not rest on the text of *Nostra Aetate* alone but on the trajectory of post-conciliar practice and teaching that has flowed from it. Two later high-profile events anchor the SSPX reading.

**Assisi (October 27, 1986).** Pope John Paul II convened 160 religious leaders — 32 Christian and 11 non-Christian religions, including Christians, Jews, Muslims, Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, Sikhs, Zoroastrians, leaders of African and American Traditional Religions, and Shinto — at the Basilica of St. Francis of Assisi to pray for peace. Each religion prayed separately; John Paul II did not personally pray to any non-Catholic god. The optics — the leaders of every major world religion gathered in Catholic sacred space, each invoking his own god for the common end of peace — were precisely the SSPX's concern, and Lefebvre identified Assisi as one of two "signs from God" justifying his June 30, 1988 consecrations (the other being the Roman silence in response to his 1987 *dubia* on religious liberty; see §3 Lefebvre profile and §2 Timeline).

**Abu Dhabi (February 4, 2019).** Pope Francis and Sheikh Ahmed el-Tayeb, Grand Imam of Al-Azhar, signed the *Document on Human Fraternity for World Peace and Living Together*. In a section listing the convictions the joint declaration upholds, the document states:

> "The pluralism and the diversity of religions, colour, sex, race and language are willed by God in His wisdom, through which He created human beings."
>
> — *Document on Human Fraternity*, Abu Dhabi, February 4, 2019

[Vatican.va — Abu Dhabi Document](https://www.vatican.va/content/francesco/en/travels/2019/outside/documents/papa-francesco_20190204_documento-fratellanza-umana.html)

The wording produced immediate and continuing controversy. The phrase is bracketed with "colour, sex, race and language" — categories where "willed by God in His wisdom" is uncontroversial — but the inclusion of *religions* in the same list, on the SSPX reading, formulates a sharp indifferentist claim: the diversity of religions, as such, is presented as part of God's positive will for creation, not as a fallen condition awaiting evangelization. Bishop Athanasius Schneider has reported that Pope Francis subsequently clarified, in conversation with him, that the phrase referred only to the "permissive will of God." On June 10, 2019, Cardinals Raymond Burke and Jānis Pujats, Archbishops Tomasz Peta and Jan Paweł Lenga, and Bishop Schneider published a 40-point *Declaration of Truths Relating to Some of the Most Common Errors in the Life of the Church of Our Days*, in which they affirmed against the Abu Dhabi formulation that "the religion born of faith in Jesus Christ" is the "only religion positively willed by God." For the SSPX, this is the point: the controversy was not raised by the Society alone but by mainstream-loyal Catholic prelates who found the joint declaration's formulation incompatible with the perennial teaching that the Catholic Church is the only religion positively willed by God for the salvation of souls.

The line from *Nostra Aetate* through Assisi 1986 to Abu Dhabi 2019 is, on the SSPX reading, the consolidation of a trajectory — from the carefully irenic Council text, through the optic ambiguity of Assisi, to a formal joint-signature claim that goes substantially beyond what the Council document itself said. Defenders argue each step is reconcilable with the prior teaching (Schneider's "permissive will" reading of Abu Dhabi being a clear instance); the SSPX argues that *needing* such a reconciling reading at each step is itself the evidence that the formulations have drifted from the perennial teaching they ought to be expressing.

### Where the Dispute Stands

*Nostra Aetate* itself is a brief and largely irenic document. The SSPX's concerns are real but less about the document itself than about the subsequent ecumenical and interreligious practice that has flowed from it — Assisi 1986, Assisi 2002, the Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification (1999), and the Abu Dhabi Document (2019).

The substantive theological question — what is the relationship between non-Christian religions, the natural law, the universal salvific will of God, and the Catholic Church as the one ark of salvation — is genuinely difficult, and was inadequately addressed by *Nostra Aetate*'s brief text. Both defenders and critics can find support in subsequent magisterial documents, particularly *Dominus Iesus* (2000), which reaffirmed the Catholic Church's unique role in salvation.

## Part V: *Lumen Gentium* §8 — "Subsists In"

### The Issue

*Lumen Gentium* — the Dogmatic Constitution on the Church — is one of the most significant documents of Vatican II. Most of it is uncontested by the SSPX. The specific point of contention is the formula in §8:

> "This Church constituted and organized in the world as a society, *subsists in* (*subsistit in*) the Catholic Church, which is governed by the successor of Peter and by the Bishops in communion with him, although many elements of sanctification and of truth are found outside of its visible structure."

### Why This Phrase Matters

Prior Catholic teaching — articulated for instance in Pius XII's *Mystici Corporis Christi* (1943) — had used the formula "is" (*est*). The encyclical identifies "this true Church of Jesus Christ — which is the One, Holy, Catholic, Apostolic and Roman Church" with "the Mystical Body of Christ" (§13). The Catholic Church *is* the Mystical Body; not merely something in which the Mystical Body subsists.

*Lumen Gentium*'s "subsists in" was a deliberate choice. The drafting committee chose it specifically to leave room for the recognition that "many elements of sanctification and of truth" exist outside the visible Catholic Church.

### What the SSPX Objects To

The SSPX argues that "subsists in" is ambiguous in a way that "is" was not:

- **"Is"** clearly identifies the Catholic Church *with* the Church of Christ. They are the same. There is no Church of Christ apart from the Catholic Church.
- **"Subsists in"** can be read in either of two ways: (1) the Catholic Church *is* the Church of Christ, and "subsists in" merely affirms this with greater nuance; or (2) the Church of Christ is a broader reality that subsists in the Catholic Church but also subsists, in different ways, in other Christian communities.

The second reading suggests a "branch theory" of the Church — the position that the one Church of Christ has multiple manifestations in different Christian communities. This was historically a Protestant or Anglican view, not a Catholic one.

### The Defense

Defenders argue:

- The 2007 CDF document *Responses to Some Questions Regarding Certain Aspects of the Doctrine on the Church* (signed by Cardinal Levada, with Benedict XVI's approval) explicitly clarified that "subsists in" means the Catholic Church *is* the Church of Christ, not that it is one branch among others.
- The reason for using "subsists in" was precisely to allow recognition of genuine ecclesial elements outside the Catholic Church without thereby creating a multi-Church framework.
- *Lumen Gentium*'s overall teaching — including its strong affirmation of the papacy, the episcopate, and the unique role of the Catholic Church — clearly excludes a branch-theory reading.

### Where the Dispute Stands

The 2007 CDF clarification substantially addressed the SSPX's specific concern about "subsists in." It states clearly that the Catholic Church *is* the Church of Christ, and that "subsists in" was chosen to allow recognition of genuine ecclesial elements outside without compromising this identification.

The SSPX's broader concern — that the post-conciliar Church has, in practice, often operated with an effective branch-theory ecclesiology — is less easily resolved by clarifications of the text. The dispute is partly about the text and partly about its reception.

## Part VI: *Gaudium et Spes* — The Pastoral Constitution

### The Document

**Title:** *Gaudium et Spes* — Pastoral Constitution on the Church in the Modern World
**Date promulgated:** December 7, 1965
**Genre:** Pastoral Constitution

### What the SSPX Accepts

The SSPX accepts most of *Gaudium et Spes*:

- The Church should engage with the modern world.
- Catholics have an obligation to work for justice, peace, and the common good.
- The Church has something to say to contemporary problems.

### What the SSPX Objects To

The SSPX's principal objection to *Gaudium et Spes* is its tone — a tone of optimistic engagement with modernity that, in their view, fails to adequately recognize the gravity of modern errors.

Key passages of concern:

**On the human person (§22):**

> "For by His incarnation the Son of God has united Himself in some fashion with every man."

The SSPX argues this formulation, taken in isolation, suggests a universal ontological union of Christ with all persons that obscures the distinction between the baptized (who are members of Christ's Mystical Body) and the unbaptized (who are not, until they are baptized).

**On modernity:**

The document's overall framework treats modern developments as substantially positive — scientific progress, democratic government, expanded human freedom — without adequate criticism of the errors that have accompanied them. The SSPX argues that this contrasts with the more sober assessment of modern errors found in *Pascendi Dominici Gregis* (Pius X, 1907), *Quas Primas* (Pius XI, 1925), and other pre-conciliar documents.

### Where the Dispute Stands

The criticism of *Gaudium et Spes* is more tonal than textual. Specific propositions in the document can usually be defended; the overall optimistic posture is harder to defend in light of the actual record of modernity in the 20th century — two world wars, totalitarianism, the abortion industry, the collapse of family structure, etc.

This is an area where the SSPX critique has been substantially confirmed by subsequent events, even if not by subsequent magisterium. The "spirit of optimism" pervading *Gaudium et Spes* looks, in retrospect, less like prophetic engagement with modernity than like a moment of naïve confidence that subsequent decades have not vindicated.

## Part VII: The Substantive Theological Question

We can now state the core dispute precisely.

### What the SSPX is Actually Claiming

The SSPX claims:

- Vatican II produced certain texts that, on their plain reading, appear to contradict prior definitive Catholic teaching.
- The contested texts concern religious liberty (DH), the nature of the Church and ecumenism (UR, LG §8), non-Christian religions (NA), and the relationship to modernity (GS).
- When prior teaching is definitive (i.e., taught by the ordinary universal Magisterium or solemnly defined), it cannot be contradicted by subsequent teaching, even by a council.
- The hermeneutic of continuity requires reconciling Council documents with prior definitive teaching where possible — but where reconciliation is impossible, the prior definitive teaching prevails.
- Therefore, certain Council passages must either be reinterpreted in continuity with prior teaching or, where this is impossible, set aside in favor of the prior definitive teaching.
- This is not rejection of Vatican II as a Council; it is the application of Catholic principles of theological method to specific texts of the Council.

### What the Mainstream Catholic Position Holds

The mainstream Catholic position (articulated by Benedict XVI, Francis, and the broad theological consensus):

- Vatican II is a legitimate exercise of the ordinary Magisterium.
- Its texts must be read in continuity with prior teaching.
- Apparent contradictions can be resolved through careful theological work that respects both the prior teaching and the development represented by the Council.
- A Catholic owes religious submission of will and intellect to authoritative Council teaching even when reconciliation with prior teaching is not immediately evident.
- Refusal to accept Council teaching on the grounds that one cannot personally reconcile it with prior teaching is a failure of proper docility to the Magisterium.

### The Crux of the Dispute

The dispute reduces to two related questions:

**1. Is genuine contradiction possible between Council teaching and prior definitive teaching?**

The SSPX says yes (in the cases they identify). Mainstream Catholicism says no (by definition — if it were possible, the Church would not be infallible in her ordinary universal Magisterium).

**2. If contradiction appears to exist, what is the proper Catholic response?**

The SSPX says: hold to the prior definitive teaching while presuming the contradiction is apparent rather than real, awaiting magisterial clarification. Mainstream Catholicism says: presume the apparent contradiction is one's own failure of understanding, and submit to the current teaching while seeking to understand it.

These are deep questions of theological method. They are not easily resolved by quoting texts. They concern the very nature of the Magisterium and its relationship to Tradition.

---

## Part VIII: Where the Disputes Genuinely Stand

For the purpose of forming a stance, an honest evaluation requires distinguishing:

### Where the SSPX has the stronger textual case

- **Religious liberty (DH).** The textual tension between *Dignitatis Humanae* and 19th-century papal teaching is real and serious. The continuity-resolutions are possible but require sophisticated interpretive work. No magisterial document has ever explicitly resolved the apparent contradiction at the level of texts.

### Where the SSPX has a real but contestable case

- **Ecumenism (UR).** The shift from "return" to "dialogue" is documented. Whether this represents a contradiction or a development depends on one's prior theological commitments.
- **Non-Christian religions (NA).** The framework of "rays of truth" is theologically defensible but has been used to support post-conciliar interreligious practices that are harder to defend.
- **The "subsists in" question (LG 8).** The CDF clarification (2007) substantially addresses the textual concern. The lived ecclesiology of the post-conciliar Church is a separate question.

### Where the SSPX position is harder to sustain

- **The claim that Vatican II is "modernist" or "Protestantizing" as a whole.** This dramatically overstates the case. Most of the Council's content is unproblematic, and even the contested passages do not amount to a wholesale modernism.
- **The claim that Council teaching can be ignored because it is "merely pastoral."** This understates the authority owed to Council teaching, even on questions not strictly defined.

### Where the mainstream position is genuinely difficult

- **Reconciling *Dignitatis Humanae* with *Quanta Cura* and the Syllabus of Errors at the level of texts.** Continuity is asserted but rarely worked out in detail.
- **Explaining why post-conciliar practice has so often diverged from claimed continuity.** If the Council taught in continuity with prior teaching, why has the post-conciliar Church frequently appeared to embody discontinuity?
- **Accounting for the catastrophic decline in Catholic faith and practice in the post-conciliar era.** While the Council is not the sole cause, the correlation is undeniable.

---

## Part IX: A Final Observation

The dispute over Vatican II is not a question of whether the Council should have happened — both sides accept that it did. It is not a question of whether the Council is binding in some sense — both sides accept that it is. It is a question about *how to read* certain Council passages in light of prior definitive teaching.

For the Catholic who is forming a stance on the SSPX question, several things should be held simultaneously:

- The SSPX's specific textual concerns are not frivolous. They involve real tensions identified by serious theologians, including (in qualified ways) by Benedict XVI himself.
- The SSPX's institutional response — operating outside canonical structures, consecrating bishops without papal mandate — is a separate question from whether their textual concerns have force.
- The mainstream Catholic position, defending Council teaching in continuity, is theologically defensible and is held by the vast majority of serious Catholic theologians.
- The post-conciliar period has, by widespread acknowledgment, included a crisis in Catholic life — declining belief, declining practice, declining vocations, weakened catechesis. Whether this crisis is caused by, correlated with, or merely contemporaneous with the Council is itself disputed.
- The honest evaluator must distinguish between the questions of (a) whether the SSPX is *right* about Vatican II and (b) whether their *response* to being right is appropriate.

A Catholic could agree with the SSPX on (a) without agreeing on (b). A Catholic could disagree with the SSPX on (a) and therefore disagree on (b) entailed by it. The full evaluation requires holding all of these in proper relation.

This is the substance of what §10–§12 will explore at greater length.
