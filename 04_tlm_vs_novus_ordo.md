# Section 4: The Traditional Latin Mass vs the Novus Ordo
## The SSPX Compendium

> **How to use this section.** This section examines the central liturgical dispute that gave rise to the SSPX. It covers the historical development of both rites, the specific theological differences the SSPX identifies between them, the *Ottaviani Intervention* in full, the official Catholic defense of the new Mass, and an honest assessment of which criticisms have been vindicated and which have not. The goal is to understand the substantive theological disagreement — not merely the aesthetic preference for Latin or chant, but the doctrinal claims about what the Mass *is* and what each rite *teaches*. Read §2 (Timeline) for context. After this section, §5 (Vatican II) examines the broader doctrinal disputes of which the liturgical question is one part.

---

## A Note Before We Begin

This section requires precision because the topic is often handled imprecisely. Several common confusions to set aside before we begin:

**The SSPX does not claim the Novus Ordo is invalid.** Neither did Lefebvre. The Society holds that the new Mass, when properly celebrated, is a valid Mass — Christ becomes really present, the sacrifice is offered. The dispute is not about validity but about whether the new rite *adequately expresses* and *fully transmits* the Catholic faith concerning the Mass.

**The SSPX does not reject Latin liturgical reform in principle.** The Council of Trent reformed the liturgy. Pius V's 1570 Missal was itself a reform. *Sacrosanctum Concilium* (1963) authorized further reforms — extending vernacular, simplifying certain elements, restoring others. The SSPX position is not that liturgy must never change, but that the specific changes of 1969 went far beyond legitimate reform and constituted a rupture.

**The Ottaviani Intervention was not written by the SSPX.** It was authored in 1969 by a small group of Roman theologians and signed by two cardinals (Ottaviani, former head of the Holy Office, and Bacci) who had no connection to Lefebvre. It represents a critique from within the highest reaches of the pre-conciliar Roman theological establishment, not a partisan SSPX document.

**Benedict XVI substantially vindicated key SSPX liturgical claims.** *Summorum Pontificum* (2007) declared the Traditional Mass had "never been juridically abrogated" — a striking concession. His 2007 letter to bishops stated that "what earlier generations held as sacred remains sacred and great for us too." These statements, from a sitting pope, validate at least the framework of the SSPX's liturgical concerns even where they do not endorse the SSPX's institutional response.

With these clarifications in mind, we proceed.

## Part I: The Two Missals — Historical Background

### The Traditional Latin Mass

The Traditional Latin Mass — often called the Tridentine Mass, the Usus Antiquior, the Extraordinary Form, or simply "the old Mass" — refers to the Roman Rite as codified by Pope Pius V in 1570 in the wake of the Council of Trent (1545–1563), and as it developed through subsequent centuries with minor revisions.

The 1570 Missal of Pius V was not itself an innovation. It codified what had been organically developing in the city of Rome for centuries. The structure of the Mass — Introit, Kyrie, Gloria, Collect, Epistle, Gradual, Gospel, Credo, Offertory, Canon, Pater Noster, Communion, Postcommunion — substantially preceded Pius V. The Roman Canon (Eucharistic Prayer I) was substantively present in the Gregorian Sacramentary of the 7th century and in some form goes back significantly earlier. The Mass said by Pius V was the Mass said by Gregory the Great (590–604), and substantially the Mass said by Ambrose, Augustine, and the Roman Christians of the patristic age.

**The 1570 Missal was revised periodically over the centuries:**
- Clement VIII (1604) — minor textual corrections.
- Urban VIII (1634) — revision of the hymns of the Divine Office (not the Missal proper).
- Leo XIII (1884) — small revisions.
- Pius X (1911) — reform of the breviary; Mass essentially unchanged.
- Pius XII (1955) — restoration of the Easter Vigil; reform of Holy Week.
- John XXIII (1962) — final pre-conciliar typical edition; small additions to the Canon (the name of St. Joseph).

The **1962 Missal of John XXIII** — the version authorized by *Summorum Pontificum* as the Extraordinary Form — is the last typical edition of the Roman Missal before the post-conciliar reform. It is the Mass the SSPX celebrates.

### The Novus Ordo Missae

The Novus Ordo Missae was promulgated by Pope Paul VI in 1969 through the apostolic constitution *Missale Romanum* and took effect on the First Sunday of Advent (November 30) of that year. It is the ordinary Mass of the Roman Rite as celebrated today across the Catholic world.

The new Mass was produced by a body called the *Consilium ad exsequendam Constitutionem de sacra Liturgia* (the Consilium for Implementing the Constitution on the Sacred Liturgy) under the leadership of Archbishop Annibale Bugnini. The Consilium was established in 1964 to carry out the reforms mandated by *Sacrosanctum Concilium*.

**Key changes from the 1962 Missal:**

| Element | 1962 Missal | 1969 Missal |
|---|---|---|
| Language | Latin (almost universally) | Vernacular permitted; Latin retained as official |
| Direction of celebration | *Ad orientem* (priest and people facing same direction, toward the altar) | *Versus populum* (priest facing people) widely adopted, though not strictly required |
| Lectionary | One-year cycle; relatively limited selections | Three-year cycle (Sunday); two-year cycle (weekday); substantially expanded |
| Eucharistic Prayer | Roman Canon only (Eucharistic Prayer I) | Four Eucharistic Prayers; three additional later approved |
| Offertory prayers | Sacrificial offering prayers (Suscipe, sancte Pater; Offerimus tibi, Domine) | Replaced with "blessing" prayers based on Jewish meal blessings |
| Prayers at the foot of the altar | Psalm 42, Confiteor, etc. | Eliminated |
| Last Gospel (John 1:1–14) | Recited at end of every Mass | Eliminated |
| Confiteor | One for priest, one for people; mentions saints by name | Simplified; reduced mentions of saints |
| Communion | Kneeling, on the tongue, host placed on tongue by priest | Standing also permitted; communion in the hand widely allowed |
| Distribution | By priest or deacon only | Lay extraordinary ministers permitted |
| Servers | Male only | Female altar servers permitted (1994) |
| Sign of peace | Among clergy only | Extended to all faithful |
| Words of consecration | "*Mysterium fidei*" within the consecration; "*pro multis*" ("for many") | "*Mysterium fidei*" moved out; vernacular translations often rendered "*pro multis*" as "for all" (later corrected) |
| Prefaces | About 20 | Over 80 |
| Saints' feasts | Calendar emphasizing martyrs and ancient saints | Calendar revised; many feasts removed, demoted, or relocated |

This list is partial. The total transformation was extensive.

### The Consilium and Its Work

The composition of the Consilium that produced the Novus Ordo is important for understanding the dispute.

- The Consilium operated from 1964 to 1969.
- Its proceedings were largely private. Vernacular liturgy emerged before the official promulgation; many reforms were introduced experimentally.
- **Six Protestant observers** participated in the Consilium's work as invited consultors: A. Raymond George (Methodist), Ronald Jasper (Anglican), Massey Shepherd (Episcopalian), Friedrich Künneth (Lutheran), Eugene Brand (Lutheran), and Max Thurian (Reformed/Taizé).

Their precise role is contested. Defenders of the new Mass argue they were observers only, with no formal voice in decisions. Critics — including the *Ottaviani Intervention* — argue that the very inclusion of Protestant consultors in the design of a Catholic liturgy signaled an ecumenical intent that shaped the outcome. Bugnini himself, in his memoir *The Reform of the Liturgy 1948–1975* (1990), confirmed the participation but described it as consultative.

The point most commonly made by SSPX-aligned critics: a Catholic Mass should be designed by Catholic theologians without input from those who reject Catholic Eucharistic doctrine. Whether or not the Protestant observers materially influenced specific decisions, their presence is documentary evidence of the ecumenical orientation that shaped the reform.

## Part II: The Ottaviani Intervention

The most important single document in the liturgical debate is the *Brief Critical Study of the New Order of Mass*, commonly called the *Ottaviani Intervention* after its most prominent signatory.

### Authorship and Significance

The document was authored primarily by a group of Roman theologians (including Fr. Antonio Cipriotti and Fr. M. L. Guérard des Lauriers, O.P., among others) and signed by:

- **Cardinal Alfredo Ottaviani** — former Pro-Prefect of the Sacred Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith (the Holy Office); one of the most prominent traditionalist figures at Vatican II.
- **Cardinal Antonio Bacci** — Latinist; participant in the Council.

It was submitted to Pope Paul VI on September 25, 1969 — two months before the Novus Ordo took effect on the First Sunday of Advent.

This is not an SSPX document. It predates the SSPX. It was written by men who served at the highest levels of the pre-conciliar Roman Curia. Cardinal Ottaviani himself, in his cover letter to Paul VI, described it as a "humble but pressing prayer that the Roman Catholic Church should not be deprived of the heritage of theological and spiritual riches" found in the traditional Mass.

### The Document's Central Claim

The Ottaviani Intervention's most-cited passage:

> "The *Novus Ordo Missae* — considering the new elements widely susceptible to widely different interpretations which are implied or taken for granted — represents, both as a whole and in its details, a striking departure from the Catholic theology of the Holy Mass as it was formulated in Session XXII of the Council of Trent. The 'canons' of the rite definitively fixed at that time provided an insurmountable barrier to any heresy directed against the integrity of the Mystery."

**Analysis:**
- The claim is precise: not that the new Mass is invalid, not that it is heretical, but that it constitutes a "striking departure" from the theology defined at Trent.
- The reference to Trent Session XXII is to the canons defined September 17, 1562, which condemned specific Protestant errors about the Mass. The Council of Trent had explicitly fixed certain liturgical elements to safeguard those definitions.
- The argument is structural: by removing or modifying the elements that Trent had fixed to safeguard against Protestant errors, the new Mass removes the safeguards even if it does not formally teach the errors.

### Specific Criticisms

The Ottaviani Intervention identifies a series of specific concerns. The most theologically significant follow.

**1. The new definition of the Mass.**

The Intervention quotes the original *General Instruction of the Roman Missal* (GIRM, 1969), which defined the Mass at §7:

> "The Lord's Supper or Mass is the sacred meeting or congregation of the people of God assembled, the priest presiding, to celebrate the memorial of the Lord."

The Intervention's response:

> "Note here the definition of the Mass is limited to that of the 'Supper'... Nothing further is said about the Real Presence, about the reality of the sacrifice, the Sacrament of the Sacrificing Priesthood, and the intrinsic value of the Eucharistic Sacrifice, independently of the people's presence."

This was perhaps the most successful critique. **Paul VI revised the GIRM in 1970 in response**, expanding the definition to include sacrificial language. The original §7 was rewritten to read:

> "At Mass — that is, the Lord's Supper — the People of God is called together, with a priest presiding and acting in the person of Christ, to celebrate the memorial of the Lord, the eucharistic sacrifice."

The fact that Paul VI revised the GIRM in response demonstrates that the critique had identified a real problem in the original text. Critics note that the underlying liturgical changes — which the revised definition was meant to interpret — remained unchanged.

**2. The "essential elements" of the Mass.**

The Intervention argues that the new rite, while containing the essential elements for validity, has altered or removed many elements that historically expressed the Catholic doctrine of the Mass as a propitiatory sacrifice offered for the living and the dead.

Specific concerns:

- **The Offertory prayers.** The traditional offertory included prayers explicitly identifying the bread and wine as offerings of sacrifice: *Suscipe, sancte Pater* ("Receive, holy Father, this spotless host which I, Your unworthy servant, offer to You..."); *Offerimus tibi, Domine* ("We offer You, O Lord, the chalice of salvation..."). These were replaced with prayers modeled on Jewish meal blessings: "Blessed are You, Lord God of all creation. Through Your goodness we have this bread to offer, which earth has given and human hands have made." The new prayers can be understood sacrificially — but they no longer *explicitly state* the sacrificial offering.

- **The Roman Canon's reduced prominence.** Eucharistic Prayer I (the Roman Canon) was retained but is rarely used in practice. Eucharistic Prayer II — modeled in part on the early *Anaphora of Hippolytus* — is shorter and is the most commonly chosen. The Intervention argues this represents a marginalization of the Roman tradition's specific theology of sacrifice.

- **Removal of explicit references to sin, judgment, and the propitiatory nature of the sacrifice** in various places throughout the rite.

**3. The position and role of the priest.**

The Intervention notes the change in language regarding the priest. The 1962 Missal calls the priest *sacerdos* (sacrificing priest) throughout. The 1969 Missal increasingly uses *presbyter* (elder), and the GIRM describes the priest as *presiding* over the assembly.

> "[The priest's] function is reduced to that of a *Protestant minister*. In the new rite, he is no longer fundamentally a 'sacerdotal' figure but a 'presider' over the assembly."

This critique was, in the Intervention's judgment, especially concerning given that the Protestant reformers had specifically rejected the sacerdotal/sacrificial character of Catholic priesthood, identifying it as one of the principal errors of "Romanism."

**4. The vernacular and the loss of Latin.**

The Intervention notes that while *Sacrosanctum Concilium* §36 had stated that "the use of the Latin language is to be preserved in the Latin rites," the practical reform had reduced Latin to a vestigial role in most parishes. The Intervention argues this loss is not merely sentimental but doctrinal:
- Latin's universality expressed the universality of the Catholic faith.
- Latin's stability across centuries expressed the stability of Catholic doctrine.
- Latin's separateness from everyday speech expressed the sacred character of the liturgy.

**5. The orientation toward the people.**

While *versus populum* is not strictly required by the new Missal, it has been almost universally implemented. The Intervention argues this change embodies a fundamentally different theology of the Mass:
- *Ad orientem* expresses that the priest and people together face God — the priest leads the people in offering sacrifice to God.
- *Versus populum* expresses that the priest faces the assembly — a "meal" rather than a "sacrifice," a "communal celebration" rather than a "presentation of the offering."

The traditional position has been articulated by, among others, Cardinal Ratzinger in *The Spirit of the Liturgy* (2000): "A common turning to the east during the Eucharistic Prayer remains essential. This is not a case of accidentals, but of essentials. Looking at the priest has no importance. What matters is looking together at the Lord."

### The Cover Letter to Paul VI

In the cover letter accompanying the Intervention, Cardinals Ottaviani and Bacci wrote:

> "Most Holy Father, having carefully examined, and presented for the scrutiny of others, the *Novus Ordo Missae*, prepared by experts of the *Consilium ad exsequendam Constitutionem de sacra Liturgia*, and after lengthy prayer and reflection, we feel it to be our bounden duty in the sight of God and towards Your Holiness, to put before you the following considerations: 1. The accompanying critical study of the *Novus Ordo Missae*, the work of a group of theologians, liturgists and pastors of souls, shows quite clearly in spite of its brevity that if we consider the innovations implied or taken for granted which may of course be evaluated in different ways, the *Novus Ordo* represents, both as a whole and in its details, a striking departure from the Catholic theology of the Mass as it was formulated in Session XXII of the Council of Trent."

The letter is a remarkable document — two cardinals, one of them the immediate past head of the Holy Office, addressing the pope with both filial reverence and theological urgency.

### Paul VI's Response

Paul VI received the Intervention and made some revisions in response. The most significant was the rewriting of GIRM §7 (discussed above). The new Mass otherwise proceeded to promulgation.

In a general audience on November 26, 1969 — four days before the new Mass took effect — Paul VI defended the reform:

> "It is a great new step forward in tradition. A new rite of the Mass: a change in a venerable tradition that has gone on for centuries... It is more important to perceive than to see; more important to grasp than to make tangible... We will lose a great part of that stupendous and incomparable artistic and spiritual thing, the Gregorian chant. We have reason indeed for regret, reason almost for bewilderment. What can we put in the place of that language of the angels? We are giving up something of priceless worth."

This concession — "we are giving up something of priceless worth" — is striking. Paul VI did not deny that something real was being lost. He defended the reform as nonetheless necessary.

## Part III: The Theological Disagreement in Detail

Beyond the specific liturgical changes documented above, the dispute between defenders of the traditional Mass and defenders of the Novus Ordo involves substantive theological claims about what the Mass *is*.

### The Catholic Doctrine of the Mass

Both forms of the Roman Rite affirm — and the SSPX and Rome equally hold — the following Catholic doctrines on the Mass:

- **The Real Presence.** Christ is substantially, truly, really present under the species of bread and wine after the words of consecration.
- **Transubstantiation.** The substance of bread is changed into the substance of Christ's body; the substance of wine is changed into the substance of Christ's blood; the accidents (appearances) remain.
- **The Mass as Sacrifice.** The Mass is not merely a memorial meal but the unbloody re-presentation of the one sacrifice of Calvary; it is propitiatory (offered for the remission of sins) and impetratory (offered to obtain graces).
- **The Mass for the Living and the Dead.** The Mass may be offered for specific intentions, including the suffering souls in purgatory.
- **The Ministerial Priesthood.** The Mass requires a validly ordained priest who acts *in persona Christi* (in the person of Christ) and offers the sacrifice.

These doctrines were defined or solemnly reaffirmed at the **Council of Trent, Session XXII** (September 17, 1562):

> "Canon 1: If anyone says that in the Mass a true and proper sacrifice is not offered to God; or that to be offered is nothing else than that Christ is given to us to eat: let him be anathema."
>
> "Canon 3: If anyone says that the sacrifice of the Mass is one only of praise and thanksgiving; or that it is a mere commemoration of the sacrifice consummated on the cross, but not a propitiatory one; or that it benefits only him who receives, and ought not to be offered for the living and the dead, for sins, punishments, satisfactions, and other necessities: let him be anathema."

The dispute is not about whether these doctrines are true. Both sides affirm them. The dispute is about whether the new Mass *adequately expresses and transmits* them.

### The SSPX (and Traditionalist) Argument: Lex Orandi, Lex Credendi

The traditionalist argument relies on the ancient principle *lex orandi, lex credendi* — the law of praying is the law of believing. What the Church prays shapes what the Church believes. Liturgical formulations have doctrinal weight; how the Church worships forms the faith of those who worship.

The traditionalist claim:

- The traditional Mass, in its specific structure and prayers, embodies and transmits the Tridentine doctrine of the Mass.
- The new Mass, while doctrinally compatible with the Tridentine teaching when properly interpreted, has been altered in ways that *weaken* the expression of that doctrine.
- Over time, a weakened liturgical expression produces a weakened faith — even if the underlying doctrine has not formally changed.
- The catastrophic decline in Catholic belief about the Real Presence, the sacrificial character of the Mass, and the necessity of the priesthood — documented in survey after survey since 1969 — is at least partially attributable to the liturgical reform.

**Empirical support:** A 2019 Pew Research survey found that only **31% of self-identified American Catholics** believed in the Real Presence — that the bread and wine actually become the Body and Blood of Christ. **69% believed they were merely symbolic.** This represents a catastrophic loss of basic Catholic Eucharistic faith. While many factors contribute, the SSPX and traditionalists argue that the liturgical reform — which made the Mass more ambiguously expressive of these doctrines — bears significant responsibility.

### The Defense of the Novus Ordo

Defenders of the new Mass — including most of the Catholic hierarchy and many serious theologians — argue:

- The new Mass *does* teach the same Catholic doctrines, properly understood. The Eucharistic Prayers, including the Roman Canon retained in the new Missal, contain explicit sacrificial language.
- The reform reflected genuine pastoral concerns: making the liturgy more accessible to the faithful, restoring elements that had been lost, simplifying needless duplications.
- The decline in Catholic faith is attributable to many factors (the sexual revolution, the collapse of catechesis, secularization broadly) — not primarily to the liturgical reform.
- The traditionalist position confuses *form* with *substance*. Catholic doctrine is taught by the Church's Magisterium, not solely by liturgical texts. A weakened liturgical expression can be remedied by catechesis without reverting the liturgy.

**Paul VI in *Mysterium Fidei* (1965)** had reaffirmed the doctrine of the Real Presence and transubstantiation in the strongest terms:

> "The Catholic Church has always devoutly guarded as a most precious treasure the mystery of faith — that is, the ineffable gift of the Eucharist which she received from Christ her Spouse..."

This is the official position: the Catholic Church teaches the same doctrine on the Eucharist. The new Mass is not intended to deny anything Trent defined. Whether the new Mass adequately *expresses* what Trent defined is a separate question on which there is genuine disagreement.

### Benedict XVI's Mediating Position

Cardinal Ratzinger, before his election as Pope Benedict XVI, addressed these questions extensively. His position is significant because it represents a sympathetic but mainstream Catholic engagement with traditionalist concerns.

In *The Spirit of the Liturgy* (2000), Ratzinger acknowledged real losses in the liturgical reform:

> "I am convinced that the crisis in the Church that we are experiencing today is to a large extent due to the disintegration of the liturgy, which at times has even come to be conceived of *etsi Deus non daretur*: in that it is a matter of indifference whether or not God exists and whether or not He speaks to us and hears us."

And:

> "The prohibition of the missal that was now decreed, a missal that had known continuous development over the centuries ever since the time of the Sacramentary of the Gelasian period right up to the time of Pope John XXIII, was something quite extraordinary. Nothing of the sort had ever happened in the entire history of the liturgy."

These are remarkable statements. Ratzinger acknowledges that the post-conciliar liturgical reform had been carried out in a manner unprecedented in the history of the Church — a *de facto* suppression of an organically developed rite. This is the SSPX's central liturgical claim, articulated by a man who would become pope.

In *Summorum Pontificum* (2007), Benedict XVI formalized the substance of this position by declaring the Traditional Mass had "never been juridically abrogated" and was permanently available as the Extraordinary Form. He thereby vindicated the traditionalist position that the Traditional Mass had never been legally suppressed — Catholics attached to it had always had a right to it.

### What Benedict Did Not Do

Benedict did not declare the Novus Ordo deficient. He did not require its modification. He did not undo any of the specific changes the Ottaviani Intervention had criticized. His position was that both forms are legitimate expressions of the Roman Rite, and that the existence of the Traditional Form provides a constant reference point that can enrich the celebration of the Ordinary Form.

This is the position of most mainstream traditionalists today (FSSP, ICKSP, Institute of the Good Shepherd, and many independent Catholics): both forms are valid Catholic liturgy; the Traditional Form is preferable on theological and devotional grounds; both should be available; the Ordinary Form should be celebrated as reverently and traditionally as possible.

The SSPX position is somewhat stronger: the Novus Ordo, while valid, is so problematic that priests should not normally celebrate it. SSPX priests do not concelebrate at the new Mass and generally do not assist at it. This is a more radical position than the FSSP or the Institute of Christ the King.

## Part IV: A Closer Look at Specific Changes

We turn now to a more detailed examination of specific liturgical changes that have been most controverted.

### The Offertory

This is the change the Ottaviani Intervention treats most extensively, and the change most often cited by traditionalists.

**Traditional Offertory** (1962 Missal, key prayers):

> "*Suscipe, sancte Pater, omnipotens aeterne Deus, hanc immaculatam hostiam, quam ego indignus famulus tuus offero tibi, Deo meo vivo et vero, pro innumerabilibus peccatis, et offensionibus, et negligentiis meis, et pro omnibus circumstantibus, sed et pro omnibus fidelibus christianis vivis atque defunctis...*"
>
> "Accept, O holy Father, almighty and eternal God, this spotless host, which I, Your unworthy servant, offer to You, my living and true God, for my own countless sins, offenses, and negligences, and for all here present; also for all faithful Christians both living and dead..."

> "*Offerimus tibi, Domine, calicem salutaris, tuam deprecantes clementiam: ut in conspectu divinae majestatis tuae, pro nostra et totius mundi salute, cum odore suavitatis ascendat. Amen.*"
>
> "We offer You, O Lord, the chalice of salvation, beseeching Your mercy that it may rise up with a pleasing fragrance in the sight of Your divine majesty, for our salvation and for that of the whole world. Amen."

**New Offertory** (1969 Missal):

> "Blessed are You, Lord, God of all creation. Through Your goodness we have this bread to offer, which earth has given and human hands have made. It will become for us the bread of life."

> "Blessed are You, Lord, God of all creation. Through Your goodness we have this wine to offer, fruit of the vine and work of human hands. It will become our spiritual drink."

**Analysis:**
- The traditional prayers explicitly identify the bread and wine as a sacrificial offering (*hostia* — "host," literally "victim") offered for sins.
- The new prayers describe the bread and wine as offerings made for the assembly's spiritual benefit; the sacrificial language is significantly reduced.
- The traditional prayers explicitly reference the propitiatory character (offered for sins) — central to Tridentine doctrine.
- The new prayers are modeled on Jewish *berakhah* (blessing) prayers — appropriate for a meal blessing but theologically weaker as expressions of sacrificial offering.

**Defense:** Catholic theologians who defend the new prayers note that:
- The Roman Canon (Eucharistic Prayer I), if used, retains explicit sacrificial language throughout.
- The other Eucharistic Prayers also contain sacrificial language, even if less abundantly than EP I.
- The Offertory is technically preparatory to the Eucharistic Prayer; the sacrifice is offered in the consecration, not the Offertory.
- The Jewish berakhah background is theologically rich: Christ Himself prayed such prayers at the Last Supper.

**Assessment:** The traditional Offertory more explicitly states the sacrificial doctrine. The new Offertory does not contradict it but expresses it less directly. Whether this is a feature or a problem depends on one's view of the principle *lex orandi, lex credendi*.

### The Words of Consecration: *Pro Multis*

The traditional formula for the consecration of the chalice:

> "*Hic est enim Calix Sanguinis mei, novi et aeterni Testamenti: mysterium fidei: qui pro vobis et pro multis effundetur in remissionem peccatorum.*"
>
> "For this is the chalice of My blood, of the new and eternal Testament: the mystery of faith: which shall be shed for you and *for many* unto the remission of sins."

The new formula (in Latin):

> "*Hic est enim calix Sanguinis mei novi et aeterni testamenti, qui pro vobis et pro multis effundetur in remissionem peccatorum.*"
>
> "For this is the chalice of My blood, of the new and eternal covenant, which will be poured out for you and *for many* for the forgiveness of sins."

In the Latin, the substance is essentially unchanged. *Mysterium fidei* has been moved out of the consecration formula proper, but *pro multis* (for many) remains.

**The controversy was over translation, not the Latin.**

For decades after the 1969 reform, the standard English translation rendered *pro multis* as "for all" rather than "for many." This was a translation choice, not a change in the Latin. The same was done in many other vernaculars (German *für alle*, etc.).

**The traditionalist objection:**
- "For many" reflects Christ's own words in the Gospels (Matthew 26:28, Mark 14:24: *peri pollōn*) — "for many."
- "For many" preserves the Tridentine teaching that while Christ died for all in His sufficient sacrifice, the actual fruits of redemption are applied to those who accept them (in scholastic terms, the sacrifice is *sufficient* for all but *efficacious* for many).
- "For all" risks suggesting universal salvation — that all are necessarily saved regardless of faith and works.

**The defense:**
- "For all" reflects God's universal salvific will (1 Timothy 2:4).
- "Many" in Semitic usage often functioned as the opposite of "few," signifying a great multitude — i.e., not numerically less than "all" but expressing abundance.
- Both translations are theologically defensible.

**Resolution:** In 2006, Pope Benedict XVI through the Congregation for Divine Worship issued a directive that *pro multis* must be translated as "for many" in all vernacular Missals. This change was implemented in English-speaking countries in 2011 with the new Roman Missal translation. The traditionalist criticism on this point has been effectively vindicated by Rome itself.

### The Roman Canon vs. Eucharistic Prayer II

The Roman Canon — the only Eucharistic Prayer in the Tridentine Missal, retained as Eucharistic Prayer I in the Novus Ordo — is a prayer of great antiquity. Its core dates to at least the 4th century; it received its substantive form by the late patristic period.

**Eucharistic Prayer II** in the Novus Ordo is much shorter and is the most commonly used in parish celebrations. It is loosely based on the *Anaphora of Hippolytus* (early 3rd century).

**Traditionalist concerns:**

- The Roman Canon's specific theological emphases — the sacrificial language ("we offer to Thy supreme majesty this pure victim, this holy victim, this spotless victim"), the explicit invocation of the Saints by name, the explicit prayer for the dead — are not replicated in Eucharistic Prayer II.
- The widespread use of EP II — quicker, simpler — has meant that the average Catholic rarely hears the fuller theological articulation of the Roman Canon.
- The choice of which Eucharistic Prayer to use is left to the priest. Different choices produce different theological emphases.

**Defense:**
- All four (and now seven) Eucharistic Prayers contain the essential elements of a valid Eucharist.
- Variety in Eucharistic Prayers reflects the diverse traditions of the early Church.
- The Roman Canon remains available and is used at major celebrations.

### Communion in the Hand and the Tabernacle's Place

Two further changes have drawn particular traditionalist criticism:

**Communion in the hand.** Permitted in many countries since the 1970s, this practice is widely contested by traditionalists as risking irreverence (loss of particles of the Host, careless handling). The traditional practice — communion on the tongue while kneeling — was understood to express the awe-filled reception of God Himself.

**The tabernacle moved from the central altar.** In many post-conciliar churches, the tabernacle was moved from the high altar to a side chapel or eliminated entirely from the main sanctuary. Traditionalists argue this expressed and reinforced a weakening of Eucharistic faith — the perpetual Presence of Christ in the tabernacle being displaced from its central location.

Both practices are *permitted* but not *required* by the new Missal. The drift toward less reverent practice was, in significant part, a consequence of how the reform was implemented rather than what the reform mandated.

## Part V: The Theological Stakes

We can now state the theological dispute precisely.

### What Both Sides Agree On

- Both forms of the Roman Rite are valid Catholic liturgies.
- Both express the Catholic faith on the Eucharist as articulated by Trent.
- The Real Presence, transubstantiation, the sacrificial character of the Mass, and the ministerial priesthood are taught (whether explicitly or implicitly) in both rites.
- Reverent celebration is essential to either form.

### What They Disagree On

**On the adequacy of the new rite:**
- *Traditionalist position:* The new rite, while valid, expresses Catholic doctrine less clearly and less fully than the traditional rite. Its ambiguities have led to weakened Catholic belief in practice.
- *Mainstream defender position:* The new rite expresses Catholic doctrine adequately when properly celebrated. Problems in implementation should not be confused with problems in the rite itself.

**On the principle *lex orandi, lex credendi*:**
- *Traditionalist position:* Liturgical formulations shape belief. Weakened liturgical expression produces weakened belief.
- *Mainstream defender position:* The principle is real but not absolute. Doctrine is taught principally by the Magisterium; liturgy expresses but does not solely determine faith.

**On the historical event of the reform:**
- *Traditionalist position:* The 1969 reform was unprecedented in scope and was carried out in violation of the principle of organic development. It was effectively a fabrication rather than a reform.
- *Mainstream defender position:* The reform was a legitimate exercise of papal authority over liturgy, mandated by an ecumenical council, carried out by competent authority.

**On the role of Vatican II:**
- *Traditionalist position:* The reform went far beyond what *Sacrosanctum Concilium* mandated. The Council called for preserving Latin and chant; the reform implementers ignored these directives.
- *Mainstream defender position:* The reform appropriately developed what the Council had called for. *Sacrosanctum Concilium* anticipated and authorized substantial change.

**On the practical consequences:**
- *Traditionalist position:* The collapse of Catholic belief, vocations, and practice since 1969 is partially attributable to the liturgical reform.
- *Mainstream defender position:* The collapse has many causes; blaming the liturgy is reductive.

### Where Benedict XVI Stood

Benedict XVI's position, articulated in *Summorum Pontificum* and his many liturgical writings, attempts to mediate:

- Both forms are legitimate.
- The Traditional Form was never abrogated and remains an inheritance the Church should not lose.
- The Ordinary Form should be celebrated reverently, with sacred music, with sufficient Latin, with deep continuity with tradition.
- The "reform of the reform" — restoring elements of continuity to the Ordinary Form — is the path forward.

This position has been criticized from both sides: traditionalists find it insufficient (why not return entirely to the traditional rite?), while progressives find it reactionary (why permit a "museum rite" at all?).

### Where Francis Stood

Francis's *Traditionis Custodes* (2021) represents a sharp departure from Benedict XVI's mediating position. The new framework:

- Declares the post-Vatican II rite "the *unique* expression of the Roman Rite."
- Significantly restricts the celebration of the Traditional Rite.
- Argues that traditional Mass communities have been instrumentalized to oppose Vatican II.

The SSPX response — and the response of many mainstream traditionalists — has been that Francis's framework contradicts Benedict's, and that this contradiction is itself evidence of the doctrinal instability the SSPX has long warned against. The Magisterium, traditionalists argue, cannot consistently affirm in 2007 that the Traditional Mass was "never juridically abrogated" and then in 2021 effectively reverse course.

---

## Part VI: An Honest Assessment

For the purpose of forming a stance, an honest evaluation of the SSPX's liturgical critique should distinguish:

### Where the SSPX critique has been substantially vindicated

- **The claim that the Traditional Mass was never legally abrogated.** Confirmed by Benedict XVI in *Summorum Pontificum*. This is no longer disputed.
- **The claim that the "pro multis" translation was problematic.** Confirmed by Benedict XVI's 2006 directive requiring "for many."
- **The claim that the reform process produced something unprecedented in its scope.** Ratzinger's own description: "Nothing of the sort had ever happened in the entire history of the liturgy."
- **The claim that the implementation produced significant liturgical abuses and a decline in reverence.** Acknowledged at the highest levels of the Church, including by Benedict XVI.

### Where the SSPX critique is genuinely contested

- **The claim that the new rite itself (apart from abuses in implementation) is theologically deficient.** Many serious theologians — including those sympathetic to traditional liturgy — argue that the rite is doctrinally sound when properly celebrated.
- **The claim that the decline in Catholic faith is primarily attributable to the liturgical reform.** Empirically difficult to establish; many factors contribute.
- **The claim that priests should not celebrate the Novus Ordo.** A more radical position than most traditionalists hold.

### Where the SSPX critique is contested but with serious arguments

- **The claim that the new Offertory is a meaningful theological weakening.** The textual evidence is strong; the question is whether the weakening is fatal or remediable.
- **The claim that the involvement of Protestant observers in the Consilium signaled an ecumenical orientation that materially shaped the reform.** Documentary evidence exists; the interpretation is disputed.
- **The claim that the new Mass, in its widespread implementation, contributed to the collapse of Eucharistic faith.** Empirically suggestive (the 31% Real Presence belief figure is striking) but causally complex.

### A Final Observation

The dispute about the Mass is, in many ways, the most concrete locus of the broader SSPX dispute. The doctrinal questions about Vatican II are abstract; the liturgical questions are immediately visible to any Catholic who has attended both forms. The SSPX argues — and Benedict XVI partially confirmed — that something real was lost in the reform of 1969. Whether that loss justifies the SSPX's institutional response is a separate question, treated in §10–§12.

What can be said with confidence: the liturgical critique is not frivolous. It is supported by serious theologians, by two cardinals of the pre-conciliar Roman Curia, by Benedict XVI's own writings, and by survey data on Catholic belief. The SSPX's commitment to the Traditional Mass is not nostalgia or aesthetic preference. It is a substantive theological position with substantive evidence in its favor.

What can also be said: the dispute is not closed. Catholics in good faith disagree about the relative weight of the considerations on each side. The Roman position itself has shifted significantly over four decades — from suppression to liberalization (Benedict) to restriction again (Francis). The question of what the Mass *should* be remains, in important respects, an open question in the Catholic Church.
