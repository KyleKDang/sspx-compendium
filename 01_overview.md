# Section 1: What Is the SSPX — Identity, Self-Understanding, and Overview

## What the SSPX Is in One Paragraph

The Society of Saint Pius X (*Fraternité Sacerdotale Saint-Pie X*, SSPX) is a traditionalist Catholic priestly society founded in 1970 by French Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre at Écône, Switzerland. It exists to form and sanctify priests according to the standards of the pre-conciliar Roman Catholic Church and to provide the sacraments — above all the Traditional Latin Mass — to the faithful who seek them. The Society holds the doctrinal positions of pre–Vatican II Catholicism, objects to specific documents and developments of the Second Vatican Council (1962–1965), and operates outside the ordinary canonical structures of the Catholic Church following the episcopal consecrations of 1988, which were carried out without papal mandate. Its priests are validly ordained and its Masses are valid; the 1988 excommunications were lifted in 2009; its confession and marriage faculties were normalized by Pope Francis (2016–2017). Through the past two decades Rome has not treated the Society as in formal schism — but in May 2026, with the SSPX preparing to consecrate four further bishops on July 1 without papal mandate, the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith publicly warned that those consecrations would constitute "a schismatic act" incurring automatic excommunication. The Society, invoking what it calls a state of necessity, has declined to postpone. The SSPX is, in short, the most significant anomaly in the post-conciliar Catholic Church — a community of more than seven hundred priests, several hundred thousand faithful, and a complete institutional life, situated in a canonical space that has no precise precedent in modern Catholic history.

## Part I: Origins and Founding Vision

The SSPX was born from a specific historical moment: the collision between centuries-old Catholic formation and the sweeping reforms that followed the Second Vatican Council.

Marcel Lefebvre was not a marginal figure. He was an Archbishop, a former Apostolic Delegate for French-speaking Africa, the founding Archbishop of Dakar, and a former Superior General of the Congregation of the Holy Ghost (the Spiritans). He had participated as a Council Father in all four sessions of Vatican II. He was, in other words, a man formed in the deepest currents of pre-conciliar Catholic life who then watched in real time as that life was transformed in ways he judged catastrophic.

What he saw in the years after the Council — the collapse of priestly vocations, the rapid disappearance of Latin, the confusion that followed *Humanae Vitae*, the promulgation of a new Mass he judged theologically deficient, the public dissent of theologians who faced no meaningful discipline — convinced him that the Church was in a genuine crisis of faith. Not a pastoral difficulty or a transitional moment, but a crisis of doctrine: the introduction into the life of the Church of ideas incompatible with what the Church had always taught.

On November 1, 1970, with the approval of Bishop François Charrière of Lausanne, Geneva and Fribourg, he canonically erected the Fraternité Sacerdotale Saint-Pie X at Écône in the Swiss canton of Valais as a *pia unio* — a "pious union," the form of voluntary priestly association recognized in the canon law of the time. The Society's stated purpose was simple: the formation and sanctification of Catholic priests according to traditional standards. Its name — taken from Pope Pius X, who had condemned Modernism in *Pascendi Dominici Gregis* (1907) — signaled its orientation.

The founding vision is most clearly articulated in Lefebvre's Declaration of November 21, 1974, the theological charter of the Society. Its two best-known passages — the one affirming what the SSPX holds to, the other refusing what it will not follow — set out his stance in his own words:

> "We hold fast, with all our heart and with all our soul, to Catholic Rome, Guardian of the Catholic Faith and of the traditions necessary to preserve this faith, to Eternal Rome, Mistress of wisdom and truth."

> "We refuse, on the other hand, and have always refused to follow the Rome of neo-Modernist and neo-Protestant tendencies which were clearly evident in the Second Vatican Council and, after the Council, in all the reforms which derived from it."
>
> — Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre, *Declaration of 21 November 1974*

These two passages contain the SSPX's entire self-understanding in miniature. The Society is not rejecting Rome — it is distinguishing between "Eternal Rome" (the Rome of perennial Catholic Tradition) and the Rome of a specific historical moment (the Rome of post-conciliar reforms it considers deviations). It claims fidelity to the first while resisting the second. Whether this distinction is theologically defensible is the central question of the entire dispute. [→ §5 Vatican II; §6 Doctrinal Positions; §10–§12 The Debate]

## Part II: The SSPX's Self-Understanding

The SSPX does not understand itself as a movement, a sect, a splinter group, or a reform community. It understands itself as *simply Catholic* — Lefebvre's own repeated phrase. On its self-reading:

**It is not a new church.** The SSPX makes no claim to be anything other than part of the Roman Catholic Church. It recognizes the pope as pope, the bishops as bishops (in principle), and the Catholic hierarchy as legitimate. It does not ordain bishops to lead a rival denomination. It does not establish a new creed or a new sacramental system.

**It is not a sect.** A sect is defined by departure from the parent body in doctrine or governance. The SSPX holds every doctrine the Catholic Church has ever definitively taught. Its departure from current Church governance is, in its own analysis, a response to governance that has departed from doctrine — not a departure from doctrine itself.

**It is not a nostalgia movement.** Its commitment to the Traditional Latin Mass is not aesthetic preference for Latin or Gregorian chant. It is a theological claim: that the pre-conciliar Mass more fully and adequately expresses the Catholic doctrine of the Eucharist as a true, propitiatory sacrifice. The doctrinal positions the SSPX holds are argued positions, grounded in specific magisterial sources, not expressions of cultural conservatism. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §6 Doctrinal Positions]

**It is not sedevacantist.** This distinction is essential and frequently confused. Sedevacantists hold that the post-conciliar popes are not valid popes — that the Chair of Peter (*sedes*) is vacant (*vacante*) because the men claiming to occupy it have fallen into heresy. The SSPX emphatically rejects this position. Lefebvre explicitly and repeatedly affirmed that John Paul II was pope, even as he defied specific papal commands. The current SSPX recognizes Leo XIV as pope. The SSPX prays for the pope at every Mass. This recognition of papal authority, combined with resistance to specific exercises of it, is the distinctive — and canonically significant — feature of the SSPX's stance. [→ §6 Position on the Papacy; §7 Canon Law]

**It is in a state of necessity.** The SSPX's canonical justification for its irregular acts — founding without diocesan approval after 1975, ordaining priests against Vatican wishes, consecrating bishops in 1988, and now preparing to consecrate further bishops in 2026 — is the principle of the "state of necessity" (*status necessitatis*) recognized in Canon 1323 §4 of the Code of Canon Law. When the Church is in grave danger, and when legitimate authority either fails to address that danger or actively contributes to it, a minister may act outside ordinary canonical structures to preserve the faith. Lefebvre invoked this principle explicitly in 1988; the SSPX has maintained it ever since; and Superior General Fr. Davide Pagliarani invoked it again in February 2026 in announcing the planned consecrations. [→ §7 Canon Law]

**It is a sign of contradiction.** The SSPX is aware that its existence is itself a theological statement — that a Catholic community can survive, grow, and thrive by holding the pre-conciliar faith in its integrity. The steady growth of the SSPX (vocations, schools, families, converts) is presented by its defenders as evidence that the traditional faith retains its power to attract and form genuine Catholics. The existence of the SSPX is, in this sense, not merely a canonical problem but a pastoral argument.

**It is a complete Catholic ecosystem, not merely a chapel network.** Lefebvre's founding logic was that the Society could not be only a network of Mass centers. The chain is unbroken: the SSPX exists for the Mass; the Mass requires holy priests; holy priests require a seminary that forms them in Catholic doctrine and spirituality; the seminary requires Catholic families who raise virtuous sons; Catholic families require schools, religious houses, and a sacramental life that does not exist in fragments. The Society has therefore deliberately built — alongside its priories and Mass centers — schools, sister congregations (Carmels, the teaching Dominicans of Brignoles and Fanjeaux, the Consoling Sisters of the Sacred Heart in India, contemplative Dominicans, a contemplative congregation in Spain praying specifically for priests, and several others), Benedictine and Franciscan men's monasteries, and a network of roughly seventeen friendly traditional communities that share its formation and orientation. In its own self-understanding, the SSPX is not a chapel network with a seminary attached; it is a complete Catholic ecosystem of which the seminary and the chapels are the visible parts. [→ §10 Case For]

## Part III: What Rome Says the SSPX Is

Rome's official position on the SSPX has evolved significantly over the nearly four decades since the 1988 consecrations, and it is important to distinguish what Rome said in 1988, what it has said since, and what it is saying in May 2026.

**1988 — Maximum severity.** Pope John Paul II's motu proprio *Ecclesia Dei* (July 2, 1988) declared the consecrations a "schismatic act," imposed automatic (*latae sententiae*) excommunications on Lefebvre and the four bishops he had consecrated, and characterized the SSPX's notion of Tradition as "incomplete and contradictory." [→ §8 Papal Documents]

**2007 — Significant softening.** Pope Benedict XVI's motu proprio *Summorum Pontificum* declared that the Traditional Latin Mass had "never been juridically abrogated" — vindicating a central SSPX claim. His accompanying letter to the world's bishops stated that "after [the 1988 consecrations] there was no formal schism."

**2009 — Canonical normalization (partial).** The excommunications of the four surviving SSPX bishops were lifted. Cardinal Castrillón Hoyos, then head of the Pontifical Commission *Ecclesia Dei*, publicly stated that the SSPX is "not in formal schism." Benedict XVI's accompanying letter explained that the remaining barrier to regularization was "doctrinal, not disciplinary."

**2015–2017 — Sacramental normalization.** Pope Francis granted SSPX priests faculties for valid and licit confession — first for the Holy Year of Mercy (by a letter of September 1, 2015), then made permanent in *Misericordia et Misera* (2016). In 2017 the Vatican established a framework by which SSPX marriages can be celebrated validly with proper delegation. These grants do not regularize the Society's institutional status, but they formally recognize the validity of its sacramental ministry. [→ §8 Papal Documents; §9 Sacramental Questions]

**February–May 2026 — Renewed dispute.** On February 2, 2026, at the seminary in Flavigny, France, Superior General Fr. Davide Pagliarani announced that the SSPX would consecrate four new bishops on July 1, 2026, without papal mandate, to ensure the Society's episcopal succession — the two living SSPX bishops, Bernard Fellay and Alfonso de Galarreta, are nearing seventy. On February 12, Pagliarani met with Cardinal Víctor Manuel Fernández, Prefect of the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith (DDF — the Vatican office formerly known as the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith). Fernández offered "specifically theological dialogue" conditional on postponing the consecrations; the SSPX declined. On May 13, 2026, the DDF released a public statement reiterating that the planned consecrations would constitute "a schismatic act" carrying automatic excommunication. The SSPX, again invoking the state of necessity, has not retracted.

**Current position (as of May 2026):** No SSPX cleric is excommunicated. The Society is not formally declared schismatic. Its sacraments are valid; its confessions and marriages are licit under the 2016 and 2017 grants. The relationship with Rome is, however, in its most acute crisis since 1988. The outcome of the planned July 1 consecrations — both the act itself and Rome's response — will determine whether the canonical status quo of the past two decades holds or is overturned.

**In plain terms:** Through May 2026, Rome treats the SSPX as canonically irregular rather than schismatic — operating outside the ordinary canonical structures of the Church, in a way that must be resolved but which does not, as of the current state of dialogue, deprive the faithful of valid sacraments or cut them off from the Catholic Church. After July 1, that situation may change.

## Part IV: What the SSPX Actually Is — Size, Structure, and Global Presence

The SSPX is not a small fringe community. It is a substantial global institution with a complete institutional life.

### Statutory Purpose: The Six Works

The SSPX's governing statutes — internal documents which the Society does not publish — list six works that constitute its purpose, in this order:

1. The formation of holy priests in the Society's seminaries.
2. The continued education and sanctification of priests, including non-SSPX clergy.
3. The formation and sanctification of religious brothers, oblate sisters, catechists, and other auxiliaries who serve the work of the Society.
4. The education and sanctification of children in the Society's schools.
5. The education and sanctification of children and adults — and the administration of the sacraments — in the Society's Mass centers.
6. The care of elderly priests in their final years (any priest, not only SSPX members).

Two features of this list are worth marking. First, *the chapel and Mass-center apostolate is fifth, not first* — the priesthood-formation works precede it in the statutory order. The SSPX is not a Sunday-attendance organization that happens to operate a seminary; it is a priesthood-formation society that operates Mass centers as one of its works. Second, the sixth work is the care of priests beyond the Society's own membership: the SSPX statutorily refuses to confine its priestly charity to its own ranks.

The remainder of this section describes the resources by which these six works are carried out — as of late 2025:

### Clergy
- **733 priests** worldwide (as of November 1, 2025) — making the Society one of the larger priestly bodies in the Catholic world and by far the largest among traditionalist societies.
- **Two living bishops:** Bishop Bernard Fellay (consecrated 1988) and Bishop Alfonso de Galarreta (consecrated 1988). Bishop Bernard Tissier de Mallerais died on October 8, 2024. Bishop Richard Williamson was expelled from the Society in 2012 and died January 29, 2025. No bishop has been consecrated for the SSPX since 1988; the four new bishops planned for July 1, 2026 would be the first.
- Approximately **200 seminarians** across six international seminaries.
- The Superior General is **Fr. Davide Pagliarani**, an Italian priest of the Society — a priest, not a bishop. He was elected Superior General on July 11, 2018, for a twelve-year term, succeeding Bishop Bernard Fellay.

### Seminaries
| Seminary | Location |
|---|---|
| International Seminary of Saint Pius X (founding seminary) | Écône, Switzerland |
| Saint Thomas Aquinas Seminary (English-speaking seminarians) | Dillwyn, Virginia, USA |
| Saint-Curé d'Ars Seminary (first-year house of spiritual formation, for French-speaking seminarians) | Flavigny-sur-Ozerain, France |
| Sacred Heart of Jesus Seminary / *Priesterseminar Herz Jesu* (German-speaking seminarians) | Zaitzkofen, Germany |
| Our Lady Co-Redemptrix Seminary / *Seminario Nuestra Señora Corredentora* (Spanish-speaking seminarians) | La Reja, Argentina (near Buenos Aires) |

### Geographic Presence
- **Americas:** Strong presence across the United States, Canada, Mexico, Brazil, Argentina, and most of Latin America.
- **Europe:** France (the largest national presence), Germany, Switzerland, England, Ireland, Spain, Italy, Poland.
- **Africa:** Multiple countries, particularly in Francophone Africa.
- **Asia:** Growing presence in the Philippines, India, Singapore, Vietnam, and Japan.
- **Australia and Oceania:** Established communities in Australia and New Zealand.

### Priories, Chapels, and Mass Centers
- Approximately **170 priories** and **750+ Mass centers** worldwide, ranging from dedicated chapels to adapted spaces.
- Weekly Mass attendance estimated at several hundred thousand — though precise figures are not published.

### Schools
- Dozens of primary and secondary schools attached to SSPX communities, primarily in Europe and the Americas. These schools form a significant alternative educational system for traditional Catholic families.

### Publications and Media
- **Angelus Press** (Kansas City, Missouri): The Society's English-language publishing house, producing books, pamphlets, and the monthly *The Angelus* magazine.
- ***Fideliter***: French-language SSPX journal.
- ***Cor Unum***: Internal circular from the Superior General to SSPX priests.
- **SSPX.org** and **FSSPX.news**: The Society's primary English-language web presences, containing a substantial library of articles, sermons, and resources.
- An increasing presence on YouTube and podcast platforms, particularly through recorded sermons, conferences, and catechetical material.

### Organizational Structure
The SSPX is governed by a Superior General (currently Fr. Davide Pagliarani) assisted by two Assistants General, and organized into geographic **Districts**, each led by a District Superior. Major districts include the United States, France, Germany, Switzerland, Great Britain, and the various Latin American districts.

## Part V: Where the SSPX Fits in the Catholic Landscape

The SSPX occupies a specific position in the broader landscape of Catholic traditionalism. It is neither the only traditionalist community nor the most separated from Rome. Understanding where it sits helps calibrate what it is.

### Communities more integrated with Rome than the SSPX

**The Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter (FSSP):**
Founded in 1988 by eleven SSPX priests who broke with the Society over the episcopal consecrations and sought canonical regularity with Rome. Fully canonical, initially under the Pontifical Commission *Ecclesia Dei* and now under the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith (DDF). Celebrates exclusively the Traditional Latin Mass. Significantly affected by Pope Francis's motu proprio *Traditionis Custodes* (2021), which restricted the celebration of the Traditional Mass throughout the Church. Often described as the "regularized alternative" to the SSPX.

**The Institute of Christ the King Sovereign Priest (ICKSP):**
Founded in 1990. Fully canonical, known for particularly solemn and careful traditional liturgical practice. Present primarily in Europe and the United States.

**The Apostolic Administration of Saint John Mary Vianney (Campos, Brazil):**
The former Campos diocese that reconciled with Rome in 2002, following the trajectory of Bishop Antônio de Castro Mayer's community. A model for what an SSPX regularization could look like.

**Diocesan Traditional Mass communities:**
Parishes and communities within ordinary dioceses that offer the Traditional Mass, typically under the provisions of *Summorum Pontificum* — now significantly restricted by *Traditionis Custodes*.

### Communities more separated from Rome than the SSPX

**Sedevacantist communities:**
Groups that hold the Chair of Peter has been vacant since one of the post-conciliar popes — most commonly since John XXIII, Paul VI, or John Paul I — because that pope fell into heresy. Major sedevacantist groups include the Congregation of Mary Immaculate Queen (CMRI) and the Society of Saint Pius V (SSPV). The SSPX emphatically rejects sedevacantism as theologically untenable. [→ §6 Position on the Papacy]

**The "Resistance":**
A loose network of clergy and faithful associated with Bishop Richard Williamson, formed after his expulsion from the SSPX in 2012 over disagreement with then–Superior General Bishop Bernard Fellay's negotiations with Rome. Williamson performed episcopal consecrations of his own outside the Society in the years following — most notably Fr. Jean-Michel Faure (2015). These men are *not* SSPX bishops; the Resistance is a separate phenomenon from the SSPX. Considers the SSPX leadership insufficiently resistant to Rome.

**Various independent traditional communities:**
Priests who operate independently of both the SSPX and Rome, celebrating the Traditional Mass without canonical oversight of any kind. A spectrum from serious priests in difficult circumstances to more problematic situations.

### The SSPX's position in this landscape

The SSPX occupies the middle of the traditionalist spectrum — more separated from Rome than the FSSP and ICKSP, more connected than the sedevacantists and the Resistance. Its specific position is defined by:

- **Full acceptance of the papal office** (against sedevacantism).
- **Resistance to specific post-conciliar developments** (against full integration without doctrinal resolution).
- **Valid sacraments and a complete institutional life** (distinguishing it from independent priests with no community).
- **Ongoing engagement with Rome** (distinguishing it from communities that have abandoned dialogue entirely).

This middle position is both the SSPX's strength (it avoids the theological errors of sedevacantism while maintaining doctrinal integrity) and its structural challenge (it creates a canonical anomaly that neither side has resolved).

## Part VI: The Central Questions

The SSPX raises several distinct questions that must be kept carefully separate:

**1. Are SSPX sacraments valid?**
Yes. This is beyond serious dispute. [→ §7 Canon Law; §9 Sacramental Questions]

**2. Is the SSPX schismatic?**
Through May 2026, Rome has not declared the Society in formal schism. The DDF has, however, warned that the planned July 1, 2026 episcopal consecrations would constitute "a schismatic act" carrying automatic excommunication. The status going into July is therefore unsettled in a way it has not been since 1988. [→ §7 Canon Law; §8 Papal Documents]

**3. Is the SSPX canonically regular?**
No. The Society lacks canonical mission and operates outside the ordinary structures of the Church. [→ §7 Canon Law]

**4. Is the SSPX right about Vatican II?**
This is the central disputed question — the one Benedict XVI identified as the real barrier to regularization. It involves genuine theological complexity and is not resolved by pointing to the Society's canonical status. [→ §5 Vatican II; §10–§12 The Debate]

**5. Was the 1988 episcopal consecration justified?**
This is the central canonical question, and it returns with full force in 2026. The SSPX answers yes — invoking the state of necessity. Rome's 1988 position was no. Whether that earlier answer is the same answer Rome will give in 2026 is the open question. [→ §7 Canon Law; §8 Papal Documents; §10–§12 The Debate]

**6. Should a Catholic attend the SSPX?**
This is a pastoral and prudential question that cannot be answered categorically. It depends on what is available, what the individual's spiritual needs are, and how the individual engages with the broader Catholic Church. [→ §9 Sacramental Questions; §15 Forming Your Stance]

**7. Will the SSPX be regularized?**
Unknown. Multiple attempts — the 1988 Protocol, the 2012 doctrinal preamble negotiations — have come close and failed. The pontificate of Leo XIV had revived the possibility of dialogue; the July 2026 consecrations may instead set regularization back. The underlying theological dispute about Vatican II has not been resolved. [→ §2 Timeline; §12 Point-by-Point Debate]

## Part VII: Why This Matters

The SSPX matters far beyond the communities that attend its chapels. It matters as a theological datum — a challenge to the Catholic Church that demands a response at the level of ideas, not merely of canon law.

**It is a standing challenge to the hermeneutic of continuity.** If the SSPX's theological claims are right — that specific Vatican II documents contradict prior definitive teaching — then the Church's claim to teach without contradiction across time is under pressure. If the SSPX's claims are wrong — if the documents can be read in genuine continuity — then that continuity must be demonstrated, not merely asserted.

**It is a living experiment in traditional Catholicism.** The SSPX has maintained pre-conciliar Catholic life for more than five decades outside ordinary Church structures. Its vocations are strong, its communities are growing (especially since *Traditionis Custodes*), and its faithful are, by sociological measures, significantly more observant than average Catholics. Whether this proves anything depends on one's prior theological commitments — but it is a sociological fact that demands explanation.

**It is a measure of the post-conciliar crisis.** The SSPX's growth correlates with the documented post-conciliar collapse of Catholic practice. Whether the Society is a symptom of the crisis, a response to it, or a contributor to it is debated. That it exists in the context of a genuine crisis is not.

**It is an ongoing test of Rome's pastoral and theological seriousness.** The repeated failure to achieve regularization — despite multiple near-agreements — suggests that the theological dispute is real and deep, not merely a political negotiation awaiting the right moment. The question of how to receive Vatican II is not an administrative matter; it is a question about the nature of the Magisterium, the continuity of Tradition, and the proper response to a crisis in the Church. Until that question is addressed honestly at the highest levels, the SSPX will continue to exist as a standing sign of contradiction.

**It is personally consequential for any Catholic seeking the Traditional Mass.** Since *Traditionis Custodes* (2021), the SSPX is in many places the primary or only available provider of the Traditional Latin Mass. For these Catholics, the question "what is the SSPX?" is not academic — it is the question of where they go for Sunday Mass and the sacraments.

## Part VIII: How to Use This Document

**The SSPX Compendium** is organized to build in layers:

**The factual foundation (§2 and §3):** The timeline and key figures give you the chronological and personal context without which nothing else is intelligible.

**The technical foundation (§7 and §8):** The canon law and papal documents sections give you the precise vocabulary and legal framework that every argument depends on.

**The doctrinal substance (§4, §5, §6):** The sections on the Mass, Vatican II, and SSPX doctrinal positions give you the actual theological content of the dispute — what the Society holds, why it holds it, what the prior Magisterium says, and where the genuine tensions lie.

**The practical questions (§9):** The sacramental questions section answers the most practically urgent questions for any Catholic engaging with the SSPX.

**The debate (§10, §11, §12):** The three debate sections present the strongest case for the SSPX, the strongest case against, and the point-by-point confrontation of the best arguments on each side.

**The reference materials (§13, §14):** The source library and glossary provide the research tools needed to go deeper.

**The conclusion (§15):** The final section offers a framework for forming your own stance — the document's ultimate purpose.

### A Note on the Document's Posture

This document is not advocacy. It is not written to convince you that the SSPX is right or that the SSPX is wrong. It is written to give you the materials — historical, canonical, theological, pastoral — to form your own judgment.

That said, it is written with the conviction that this question deserves to be taken seriously. The SSPX is not a fringe curiosity or a Catholic equivalent of Evangelical fundamentalism. It is a community holding identifiable positions for identifiable reasons, with a substantial body of magisterial evidence in its favor on specific points and a genuine canonical problem on others. Taking it seriously means neither dismissing it nor uncritically embracing it — it means engaging it with the rigor the question demands.

## Glossary Preview

Key terms used throughout this document are defined in full in §14 (Glossary). Brief definitions for the terms most essential to reading §1:

| Term | Brief definition |
|---|---|
| Traditional Latin Mass (TLM) | The 1962 Roman Missal as codified by John XXIII; the Mass of the Roman Rite as celebrated before the 1969 reform; also called the Tridentine Mass, the Extraordinary Form, or the *Usus Antiquior* ("older usage") |
| Novus Ordo Missae | The new Order of Mass promulgated by Paul VI in 1969; the Ordinary Form of the Roman Rite as celebrated in the vast majority of Catholic churches today |
| Canonical irregularity | Operating outside the ordinary canonical structures of the Church without being under formal censure or penalty |
| Schism | The withdrawal of submission to the pope or communion with Catholics subject to him (Canon 751); a charge Rome does not currently apply to the SSPX, though it has warned of it in relation to the July 2026 consecrations |
| State of necessity | The canonical principle (Canon 1323 §4) allowing departure from ordinary canonical rules when grave necessity requires it; the SSPX's primary canonical justification |
| *Latae sententiae* | Latin for "of a sentence already passed" — a canonical penalty incurred automatically by the act itself, without requiring a judicial declaration |
| *Pia unio* | Latin for "pious union" — a form of voluntary association of clergy or faithful recognized by canon law; the SSPX's original 1970 canonical form |
| Supplied jurisdiction | The Church's supply of missing canonical power in cases of common error or probable doubt (Canon 144) |
| Sedevacantism | The position that the Chair of Peter is vacant because post-conciliar popes have fallen into heresy; rejected by the SSPX |
| Hermeneutic of continuity | Benedict XVI's framework for reading Vatican II in continuity with prior Tradition rather than as a rupture |
| *Lex orandi, lex credendi* | Latin for "the law of praying is the law of believing" — the principle that liturgical formulations express and shape doctrinal belief |
| Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith (DDF) | The Roman dicastery responsible for doctrinal matters in the Catholic Church; formerly the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith (CDF); currently led by Cardinal Víctor Manuel Fernández |
