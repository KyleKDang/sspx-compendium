## Summary

Episode 5 completes the three-episode case for the state of necessity that began with Fr. Robinson on canonical principles (ep03) and continued with Fr. Sherry on doctrinal application (ep04). Where Robinson laid the manualist framework and Sherry argued the *doctrinal* facts, Wiseman argues the *liturgical* facts: that the changes to the Mass and to the other sacraments since Vatican II amount to a state of grave general necessity by themselves, even prior to the doctrinal case. The argument moves through three principles and one extended demonstration.

(1) The Catholic life is *primarily a sacramental and liturgical life* — St. Thomas's mapping of the seven sacraments onto the stages of human life is invoked to ground the claim that to touch the liturgy is to touch what defines Catholic identity itself. (2) *Lex orandi, lex credendi*: the way the Church prays forms the way the faithful believe. (3) The Athanasian Creed's binding insistence ("Whoever wishes to be saved must above all keep the Catholic faith") is invoked to make the necessity-claim sharp: if the rite forms a non-Catholic mentality, the rite endangers souls in a strict and not merely figurative sense.

The extended demonstration proceeds in three stages. **First**, the New Mass — Wiseman rehearses the three principal defects identified in the *Ottaviani Intervention* (systematic removal of references to propitiatory sacrifice; conflation of ministerial and lay priesthood; tacit and systematic denial of the Real Presence) and quotes verbatim the cover letter's "striking departure" passage and the closing "incalculable error" verdict. The reformers' ecumenical intent is documented from successive popes' own published statements: John Paul II's *Ut Unum Sint* (§§76, 78, 83); Benedict XVI's September 23, 2011 address at the former Augustinian convent in Erfurt; Pope Francis's June 9, 2021 video message to the John 17 Movement retreat at St. Joseph's Seminary, Yonkers; Pope Leo XIV's January 25, 2026 Vespers homily for the Week of Prayer for Christian Unity. *Traditionis Custodes* (2021) is read as the "honest" confirmation that the two rites cannot in fact coexist — Art. 1's "unique expression of the *lex orandi*" formula is read as a sharp closure of Benedict XVI's two-form solution.

**Second**, the other sacraments — Wiseman walks briefly through the parallel pattern in the rites of infant baptism (parts lifted from the 1549 Anglican Book of Common Prayer; no exorcisms; baptismal vows omitted; salvific weight shifted from the sacrament onto the parents); confirmation (vague formula; uncertainty about valid oil); penance (renamed *reconciliation*; face-to-face rooms; severely reduced availability — Wiseman compares an LA-diocese average of 2–3 hours per week against St. Mary's, Kansas's 27 hours); extreme unction (renamed *anointing of the sick* on an Anglican/Lutheran model; confession easily omitted); and marriage (mixed marriages no longer an impediment; annulment statistics out of control; preparation classes delegated to laity).

**Third**, the priestly identity — the Divine Office's transformation into the Liturgy of the Hours is treated as a particularly sharp wound to the priest as *consecrated soul*. Concrete changes are inventoried: the suppression of Prime; the displacement of Matins by a non-night-time "office of readings"; the stretching of the 150-psalm cycle from one week to four; and — most pointedly — the omission of three entire psalms (58, 83, 109) and a further 62 verses on the explicit ground stated at *General Instruction of the Liturgy of the Hours* §131 of "a certain psychological difficulty" with the curses. Wiseman reads this as the clearest signal that the reformers held that there is something in the deposit of faith no longer appropriate to modern man — the very claim Archbishop Lefebvre insisted could never be made of revelation.

The episode's load-bearing contributions for the compendium are: (a) two further verbatim Ottaviani Intervention passages (the "perennial value finds only a minor place / bewilderment" passage and the closing "incalculable error" verdict), now fully web-verified; (b) the *Ut Unum Sint* / Erfurt 2011 / John 17 2021 / Leo XIV 2026 chain of pontifical statements documenting the continuity of the ecumenical project across four pontificates, extending §5's existing Assisi 1986 → Abu Dhabi 2019 trajectory in both directions; (c) the *Traditionis Custodes* Art. 1 / Art. 3 §1 reading as the post-Benedict closure of the two-form solution; (d) the "building is not on fire because *this part* of it is not on fire" extension of Fellay's Katrina and Schneider's firefighter images to the specific objection that local Latin Mass / Eastern-rite availability negates the *general* claim; (e) the Liturgy of the Hours / GILH §131 datum on the omission of three full Psalms and 62 verses — a new and previously unaddressed dimension of the liturgical state of necessity.

### → §5 Vatican II — Part I or Part II (Hermeneutics / *Dignitatis Humanae*) — *Traditionis Custodes* as closure

**ARGUMENT — *Traditionis Custodes* Art. 1 read as the post-Benedict closure of the two-form solution.** Wiseman reads Art. 1 ("the liturgical books promulgated by Saint Paul VI and Saint John Paul II … are the unique expression of the *lex orandi* of the Roman Rite") as the explicit reversal of Benedict XVI's *Summorum Pontificum* (2007) framing of two legitimate forms of one rite. Combined with Art. 3 §1's requirement that bishops "determine that these groups do not deny the validity and the legitimacy of the liturgical reform, dictated by Vatican Council II and the Magisterium of the Supreme Pontiffs," the document is read as an "honest" admission that the two rites cannot coexist because they express incompatible doctrines — the SSPX's longstanding contention. **Value: medium-high.** This is a sharper reading of *Traditionis Custodes* than the compendium currently offers and works either as a paragraph in §5 (as a magisterial-trajectory marker) or as a paragraph in §4 (as confirming the SSPX's reading of the liturgical defects).

### → §10 Case For — Part IV (State of Necessity)

**ARGUMENT — The Liturgical State of Necessity as the third pillar of the state-of-necessity case.** §10 currently has, within Part IV: Canonical Foundation, Five Levels of Spiritual Necessity, Divine Warrant (Sabbath parables + Pharisee reinforcement), Charity and Duty of State, Suspension Not Denial of Authority, Intuitive Case (Katrina + Schneider firefighter + Canon 844 §2 + the lifeboat image), Lefebvre's 1988 Application, and the Continuing Application. What is missing — and what Wiseman supplies — is the *liturgical* application of the same framework. The argument has three parts, all of which Wiseman articulates explicitly:

1. **The Mass.** The three Ottaviani-identified defects — systematic removal of propitiatory-sacrifice references, conflation of ministerial and lay priesthood, tacit denial of the Real Presence — together with the documented ecumenical *intent* of the reform, render the New Mass deficient in the *lex orandi* sense even where it remains valid in the *lex sacramenti* sense. The empirical fruit (the 31% Pew real-presence figure already in §4) is offered as confirmation that the predicted *lex credendi* effect has in fact occurred.

2. **The other sacraments.** The same pattern recurs across each of the seven sacraments — the new rites of baptism, confirmation, penance, anointing of the sick, marriage, and orders all bear the marks of an ecumenical reorientation, and the *practice* of the Church (annulment statistics, sacrament-of-reconciliation hours, mixed-marriage discipline) has shifted in parallel.

3. **The Divine Office.** The transformation of the Roman Breviary into the Liturgy of the Hours — particularly the suppression of three full Psalms (58, 83, 109) and 62 further verses on the explicit ground of "a certain psychological difficulty" (GILH §131) — is read as the clearest signal that the reformers held there is something in the deposit of faith no longer appropriate to modern man. This is in itself a *doctrinal* claim about Scripture that Lefebvre and Wiseman alike judge incompatible with the Catholic understanding of inspiration.

**Value: high.** This is the third leg of the three-episode state-of-necessity case (canonical / doctrinal / liturgical), and it is the leg the compendium does *not* yet have in §10. Strong fit for a new sub-section in §10 Part IV between the existing "Continuing Application" and "Part V (Classical Catholic Theology)."

**ARGUMENT — "The building is not on fire because *this part* of it is not on fire."** Wiseman extends the Fellay-Katrina / Schneider-firefighter image to address the specific objection that local availability of the Latin Mass (or of the Eastern rites) negates the *general* nature of the necessity claim. The form of the answer is the building image: pointing to one room that is not yet on fire does not show that the building is not on fire. The objection conflates *general* with *universal* — the canonical state-of-necessity test requires that the threat be general (touching many souls), not that no soul anywhere is untouched. **Value: medium-high.** Reinforces the Intuitive Case sub-section already in §10 Part IV with a sharper response to a recurring "trad bubble" objection.

**ANALOGY — The "unofficial firemen" formulation.** Wiseman paraphrases Bishop Fellay: "We're not the official firemen, but nobody standing by and watching a building burning with people in it can just say, 'Oh well, it's not me, I can't do anything.'" This already-attested image is reinforced as the SSPX's own self-understanding of its irregular pastoral provision. **Value: medium.** Already substantively covered by the Schneider firefighter / Katrina material in §10 Part IV; can be referenced briefly in the new "Liturgical State of Necessity" sub-section.
