## Summary

Episode 4 is the doctrinal half of the two-episode case for the state of necessity that began with Fr. Robinson in ep03.
Where Robinson laid out the manualist framework and the canonical anchors, Sherry argues the *facts*: that the post-conciliar Church is in fact in a state of grave general doctrinal necessity, and that the case is recognizable to any non-Modernist Catholic without recourse to private revelation or conspiracy theory.
His argument moves in three steps.
(1) Human law is not divine law: necessity dispenses from the former but not the latter, and the proper Catholic disposition is neither legal positivism ("the law is the law") nor antinomianism ("it's just a human law") but the classical understanding that human law must conform to divine law, with the technical category of *equity* providing the conceptual bridge.
(2) The judgment that a state of necessity exists is a *certain* judgment, not a *rash* one — Sherry distinguishes the kind of judgment Christ forbade ("Judge not") from the kind of objective judgment moral life requires, locating the difference in whether one judges the internal forum (forbidden) or objective public acts in matters of certainty (required).
(3) Applied to the present crisis: Lefebvre's 1988 "two signs from God" (Assisi 1986 and the silence of the world's bishops) demonstrated that the pope was teaching error against the first commandment and that the universal episcopate would not correct him — both publicly verifiable, neither requiring conspiracy theory.
From Assisi 1986, Sherry traces a line through Pope Francis's signed declaration at Abu Dhabi (February 4, 2019) that "the pluralism and the diversity of religions … are willed by God," through Lumen Gentium §8's *subsistit in* formulation, through *Dignitatis Humanae*'s teaching of religious liberty as a natural right, through the post-Vatican II suppression of preaching on the four last things, mortal sin, and the necessity of the Catholic Church for salvation.
The Society's response is calibrated: *only what is necessary* — Sherry insists the SSPX is not asserting jurisdiction, not consecrating every priest, not claiming the pope is not the pope, not setting up a parallel hierarchy — but holding firm on the *minimum* sufficient to preserve the faith and the sacraments through the crisis.

The episode's load-bearing contributions for the compendium are: (a) the *judgment-vs-rash-judgment* framework as the methodological precondition for any Catholic case against post-conciliar magisterium; (b) the *equity* category as the precise legal-philosophical bridge between law and necessity; (c) the Abu Dhabi 2019 statement as the most concrete verbatim datum of post-conciliar indifferentism (paired naturally with the existing Assisi 1986 / *subsistit in* / *Dignitatis Humanae* materials); (d) the *minimal-means / reasonable-force* principle as the SSPX's internal check against schismatic drift.

## Items grouped by destination compendium section

### → §6 Doctrinal Positions

**ARGUMENT — Judgment vs. Rash Judgment as the methodological precondition for the SSPX's case against post-conciliar magisterium.** Sherry's central methodological move is to disambiguate the Lord's "Judge not" prohibition (Matt 7:1) from the kind of judgment moral life requires.
The Fathers of the Church, on the SSPX reading, interpret *Nolite judicare* as forbidding (a) rash judgment — judging without sufficient reason — and (b) judgment of the *internal forum* (the state of another's soul, his culpability, his ultimate destiny).
What it does *not* forbid is judgment of objective public acts where the matter is certain.
Sherry's worked example: a brother who shows up to a new wedding while his first wife is still living and the first marriage has not been annulled.
To judge "what you are about to do is adultery" is not rash judgment; it is the judgment a fraternal love is bound to make.

Applied to the pope: when the pope teaches a doctrine that contradicts the perennial teaching of the Church on a matter that is *certain* and *public* (the necessity of the Catholic Church for salvation, the duty of states to acknowledge Christ, the prohibition on positive natural right to false religion), the duty to *recognize* that contradiction is not the prohibited judgment; it is the discharge of an intellectual act without which one cannot remain a moral agent.
The SSPX is not judging the pope's *soul*; it is judging *objective public teachings* in matters of *certainty*.
**Value: very high.** The compendium currently treats the SSPX's resistance position substantively in §6 Position 6 (Papacy) and §10 Part V (Resistance to Erring Superiors), but does not articulate this preliminary epistemic/methodological move — the disambiguation between forbidden and required judgment — that the SSPX needs *before* its substantive resistance argument can even be presented.
Adding it to §6 Position 6 (or as a new sub-heading there) closes a gap.

**ARGUMENT — Modernism's two-part structure (agnosticism + vital immanence) explicitly mapped to the post-conciliar errors of indifferentism and ecumenism.** Sherry traces the architectural line: agnosticism (no certainty in religious truth) → immanence (religion comes from inside us) → indifferentism (all religions equally valid in principle) → ecumenism / inter-religious dialogue as the practical normal form of the Church's posture toward false religion.
This is Pius X's own logic in *Pascendi* (§ "the synthesis of all heresies") but the SSPX's application to specifically post-conciliar developments is the move worth capturing.
**Value: medium-high.** The compendium has §6 Position 9 (On Modernism) and §5's treatment of the doctrinal disputes, but the *direct line* from Pascendi's two roots to the specifically post-conciliar errors is not currently drawn explicitly.

The Abu Dhabi Document is a more recent, more concrete, and more sharply formulated datum: a pope, signing a joint declaration with a non-Catholic religious leader, formally asserting that the diversity of religions is "willed by God in His wisdom."
This is the formulation that consolidated the SSPX's contention that the line from Vatican II's ambiguities through to settled post-conciliar doctrine has reached a point of formal indifferentism.
The footnote interpretations (Schneider's "permissive will of God" explanation; the "Declaration of Truths" countersignature by Burke / Schneider / Peta / Lenga / Pujats stating that "the religion born of faith in Jesus Christ" is the "only religion positively willed by God") add the controversy on which the SSPX rests its case — they are themselves evidence that *mainstream-loyal Catholic prelates* found the wording problematic.
**Recommend** placing this in §5's existing treatment of the ecumenism / interreligious-dialogue trajectory, between Assisi and *Traditionis Custodes*.

### → §10 Case For

**ARGUMENT — Equity as the legal-philosophical category bridging law and necessity.** Sherry introduces *equity* (Aristotelian / Thomistic *epieikeia*) as the technical name for action "not in accordance with the letter of the law, but with the mind of the legislator."
This is the precise philosophical apparatus that distinguishes the SSPX's case from positivism on one side and antinomianism on the other.
The mind of the legislator in canon law is the salvation of souls; where the letter of the law (the prohibition on consecrating bishops without papal mandate) frustrates the mind of the law (the preservation of the priesthood and the sacraments for the salvation of souls), action against the letter is *not* lawlessness but the higher fidelity equity calls for.
**Value: high.** §10 Part IV currently treats state of necessity primarily through analogies (Fellay's Katrina, the firefighter, the off-duty police officer).
Equity gives the case its proper Thomistic legal-philosophical name.
Strong fit for §10 Part IV's Canonical Foundation or Part V (Classical Catholic Theology).

**ARGUMENT — The "reasonable force" / "minimal means" principle as the SSPX's internal check against schismatic drift.** Sherry's closing move: the Society does *only what is necessary*.
If Fr. Pagliarani came tomorrow and said "we are consecrating every priest as a bishop," that would be ridiculous.
If he said "we are asserting territorial jurisdiction in Westminster," that would be schismatic.
The Society's calibration — auxiliary bishops only, no jurisdictional claims, no parallel hierarchy, the explicit instruction to the four new bishops to be consecrated July 1, 2026 that they will lay down their episcopacy before the pope once the crisis ends — is the SSPX's own internal answer to the "parallel church" charge.
The analogy he draws is to *reasonable force* in self-defense law: legitimate self-defense uses the minimum force necessary to repel the unjust attack; excessive force is itself unjust.
**Value: high.** This sharpens §10 Part VIII's existing parallel-church response (already strengthened in ep02 from Fellay) with the specific *minimum-means* discipline as the SSPX's own internal check.
Strong fit for §10 Part VIII's "The SSPX is a parallel church" objection, expanding the existing auxiliary-bishop architectural point.

**ARGUMENT — The "lifeboat" image of the SSPX's interim role.** Sherry explicitly invokes a lifeboat / SOS image: the Society is what one *does* during the crisis, not the permanent form of the Church.
Once the crisis is over, "business as usual" returns; the bishops lay down their offices; Rome resumes its proper governance.
**Value: medium.** Complementary to the existing §10 Part IV "Continuing Application" treatment and to §15 Part VII's "Hold Rome with filial love" closing posture.

**ARGUMENT — The "Pharisee" framing of legal positivism.** Sherry uses Christ's denunciation of the Pharisees (tithing mint and cumin while neglecting the weightier matters of the law; straining the gnat and swallowing the camel) as the dominical critique of the *fetishization* of human law.
This is a Christological grounding for the SSPX's posture that complements the already-applied Sabbath-parables grounding from ep03.
**Value: medium-high.** Could be added as a brief reinforcement of the existing "Divine Warrant — Christ's Sabbath Parables" subsection in §10 Part IV.
