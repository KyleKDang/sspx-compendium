# Findings — Episode 3: Fr. Paul Robinson on the State of Necessity

**Series:** The Episcopal Consecrations
**Episode:** 3 of 9
**Speaker:** Fr. Paul Robinson, SSPX (author of *The Realist Guide to Religion and Science*)
**Host:** Andrew (Angelus Press)
**Run time:** ~56:30
**Source:** `transcripts/episcopal_consecrations/ep03.md`

## Summary

This episode is the conceptual hinge of the entire SSPX consecrations argument. Fr. Robinson lays out the *state of necessity* doctrine in three moves: (1) law is an ordinance of reason for the common good, not an end in itself, and human (ecclesiastical) law admits of exceptions where it obstructs the common good; (2) the state of necessity is a recognized category in classical moral theology with five distinct levels (ordinary, grave, near-extreme, extreme, and grave general/public), and the SSPX claims levels four and five — extreme individual necessity and grave public necessity (doctrinal + liturgical); (3) when in a state of necessity, the superior's *power of binding* is suspended (not denied), the subordinate has a positive duty to act, and the act is not disobedience but a recognition that a particular command exceeds the superior's authority in that emergency. Robinson grounds this in Christ's Sabbath-controversy parables, in moral theology manuals (Prümmer, Génicot, Billuart, St. Alphonsus), in Bishop Schneider's recent (2026) firefighter analogy, in the 1999 *Si Si No No / Angelus* theological study by "Hirpinus," and in Canon 1323 §4° of the 1983 Code, which directly exempts those acting "by reason of necessity" from penalty. He closes by anticipating the next two episodes (doctrinal necessity, liturgical necessity) and by inviting agreement on the *principles* of necessity even from those who will dispute whether the church is in fact in such a state.

The episode is unusually theoretical for the series — its value is precisely as the *framework* against which ep04 and ep05 will be cashed out. Several of the named theological authorities (Prümmer's *Manuale*, the 1999 Angelus series) are essential to strengthen the compendium's §6 and §10 treatment of state of necessity, which currently presents the doctrine in compressed form without the manualist genealogy.

## Items grouped by destination compendium section

### → §6 Doctrinal Positions

**ARGUMENT / FRAMEWORK — The five levels of spiritual necessity (manualist taxonomy).** This is the single most useful framework gift from ep03. Robinson lays out the standard pre-Vatican II moral-theology taxonomy:
1. *Ordinary* spiritual necessity — the situation of any sinner in normal circumstances (e.g., the ordinary need of the sacraments).
2. *Grave* spiritual necessity — a soul threatened in spiritual goods of great importance, faith or morals (e.g., the teenager with a bad companion).
3. *Near-extreme* spiritual necessity — a soul which without another's help could be rescued only with great difficulty (e.g., a soul on its deathbed in mortal sin).
4. *Extreme* spiritual necessity — a soul whose salvation is morally impossible without another's help (Robinson's example: souls with no instruction in the faith at all — the motivation for missionary work).
5. *Grave general / public* spiritual necessity — a group of souls threatened in spiritual goods of great importance; the canonist/theologians' standing example is the public spreading of heresy.

Robinson states that the SSPX claims the church is *currently in states four and five* — extreme individual necessity (because saving one's soul today without assistance is morally near-impossible) and grave public necessity (because heresy is "rampant throughout the church"). He further subdivides the public necessity into *doctrinal* and *liturgical*. **Value: high.** The compendium's §6 currently asserts the SSPX's necessity claim but does not lay out the manualist five-level structure. Adding it gives the doctrine a precise classical pedigree.

**ARGUMENT — Law is an ordinance of reason for the common good; human (ecclesiastical) law admits of exceptions where it obstructs the common good.** Robinson cites the traditional Thomistic definition (an ordinance of reason for the common good, by competent authority, promulgated). He distinguishes divine law (no exceptions) from human law (admits exceptions where the law would obstruct its own purpose). **Value: medium-high.** §6 currently makes this distinction in compressed form; Robinson's articulation is cleaner and gives the SSPX's case its proper Thomistic grounding.

**ARGUMENT — The state of necessity does *not* deny authority; it suspends the power of binding in a particular emergency.** Three guiding principles when one is in a state of necessity: (a) the moral law still binds — necessity does not authorize sin; (b) the superior's power of binding is *suspended* in the matter of necessity (and any attempted command is non-binding), so the subordinate is in moral/physical impossibility of obeying both the superior and God; (c) the subordinate is not disputing the superior's authority *in principle*, only its applicability *in this emergency*. **Value: high.** This is the precise theological move the SSPX needs to dispel the "you're just disobedient" charge — and §6 does not currently articulate it with this care.

**VERBATIM QUOTE — Hirpinus, *The 1988 Consecrations* (September 1999):**

> "The priest or bishop who, constrained by necessity, does not obey the pope is not thereby denying his own subordination to the pope outside the case of necessity, and so he is not refusing authority in its lawful exercise."

and immediately following:

> "Similarly, a wife is not denying the authority of her husband outside of the case of necessity, in which she has the duty to supply for him against his unreasonably opposed will."

**Web-verified verbatim against the sspxasia.com mirror of the original article.** Attribution: Hirpinus, *The 1988 Consecrations* (theological study, Part II), *Si Si No No / The Angelus*, September 1999, ed. Rev. Fr. Kenneth Novak. **Value: high.** This is the cleanest, most quotable formulation of the suspension-not-denial principle anywhere in SSPX literature. The compendium does not currently cite the Hirpinus theological study.

### → §7 Canon Law

**FACT / CANON — Canon 1323 §4° of the 1983 Code provides the explicit canonical exemption from penalty for those acting "by reason of necessity":**

> "No one is liable to a penalty who, when violating a law or precept: … 4° acted under the compulsion of grave fear, even if only relative, or by reason of necessity or grave inconvenience, unless, however, the act is intrinsically evil or tends to be harmful to souls."

**Vatican.va-verified verbatim.** **Value: high.** This is the central canonical text supporting the SSPX's case that the 1988 consecrations (and the 2026 consecrations) do not incur the *latae sententiae* penalty of Canon 1382/1387, because they fall within the necessity exception of Canon 1323 §4°. The compendium's §7 mentions Canon 1323 but does not currently quote it verbatim and does not foreground the §4° subsection.

**FACT / CANON — Canon 1324 §1 (mitigation of penalty for various reduced-imputability conditions, including necessity-adjacent circumstances) and Canon 1345 (judicial discretion to refrain from punishment where the offender acted under necessity, fear, passion, or disturbed mental states).** These are the SSPX's secondary canonical anchors. **Value: medium.** Useful for a more complete canonical treatment in §7.

**ARGUMENT — The structural point that wherever law exists, the legal tradition recognizes states of necessity as exceptions.** Robinson's broad point that necessity-doctrine is not an SSPX innovation — it is found in moral theology manuals "by and large" and is codified in canon law. **Value: medium-high** as a rebuttal to the "wildcat priests inventing doctrine" charge.

### → §10 Case For

**ANALOGY — Bishop Athanasius Schneider's firefighter / old-equipment vs. new-equipment analogy (2026).** Used by Schneider in his early-2026 appeal to Pope Leo XIV to build a bridge between Rome and the SSPX. The fire chief mandates new equipment that does not work; the older firefighters who use the proven old equipment are denounced as disobedient and "systematic," but they are the ones putting out the fire. Schneider concludes: faced with such rigid behavior, either the fire chief is denying the seriousness of the fire, or he in fact desires that the house burn so it may be rebuilt according to a new design. **Web-verified** against OnePeterFive, Diane Montagna's *Substack* publication of Schneider's appeal, and FSSPX News. **Value: very high.** This is the most current (2026) high-profile non-SSPX endorsement of the necessity case, given by a Roman-Catholic bishop in good standing addressing the reigning pope. The compendium's §10 currently has Fellay's Hurricane Katrina / fire-without-firemen analogies (added from ep02); Schneider's parallel from a different ecclesiastical position significantly strengthens Part IV ("State of Necessity") of §10.

**ARGUMENT — The "off-duty police officer" / "doctor on a plane" framing of the priest's duty of state.** Robinson's central charity-and-duty-of-state argument: a priest with sacred orders walking past a soul in grave spiritual danger is like an off-duty police officer walking past a woman being beaten, or a doctor on a plane refusing to respond when the call goes out. The supervening duty arises not from a juridical assignment but from charity together with the priest's *duty of state*. He cites St. Alphonsus de' Liguori for the proposition that "those who possess the power of orders have an additional duty of state that goes beyond the duty of charity to assist the spiritual needs of souls." **Value: high.** This is the SSPX's positive justification for going beyond what could be defended on bare necessity-as-permission alone — it grounds the *obligation* (not merely the *permission*) to act. §10 has the necessity argument but does not currently articulate this charity-plus-duty-of-state framing.

**VERBATIM QUOTE — Lefebvre, June 30, 1988 consecration sermon:**

> "Thus we find ourselves in a case of necessity. We have done all we could, trying to help Rome to understand that they had to come back..."

and:

> "Your applause a while ago was, I think, not a purely temporal manifestation; it was rather a spiritual manifestation, expressing your joy to have at last Catholic bishops and priests who are dedicated to the salvation of your souls..."

and:

> "This Life of Our Lord Jesus Christ is disappearing everywhere in the Conciliar Church. They are following roads which are not Catholic roads: they simply lead to apostasy."

**All three passages web-verified against the Catholic Family News mirror of the 1988 sermon text.** Robinson opens the long quote with "We are in a case of necessity" — this is his paraphrase of the sermon's actual opening "Thus we find ourselves in a case of necessity" (the rest of his quotation is verbatim). For compendium use, the verbatim sermon wording should be used. **Value: high.** §10 currently has the consecrations rationale and necessity-claim but does not anchor it in the actual sermon's necessity formulation. Hooking the SSPX's classical state-of-necessity argument to Lefebvre's *own* sermon-text formulation tightens §10 considerably.

**ARGUMENT — Christ's two Sabbath parables as the divine warrant for state-of-necessity reasoning.** Robinson reads the Pharisees' rebukes of Christ's Sabbath healings — and Christ's own farm parables (the ordinary daily watering of livestock vs. the extraordinary rescue of an ox fallen into a pit) — as Christ's own articulation of necessity-doctrine. The compendium has nothing analogous; this is a good Lefebvre-tradition framing to anchor the case in dominical (Christ-given) precedent. **Value: medium-high.**

**ARGUMENT — The "double yellow line and the deer" analogy.** Robinson's go-to (he says he gave it to Taylor Marshall): if you swerve across a double yellow line to avoid a deer, the law is precisely *obstructing* its own purpose (your safety), so you may break it; but when the police officer pulls you over you still must pull over, treat him respectfully, and answer to him — i.e., the principle of authority is not denied even when its particular command is suspended. **Value: medium-high** as an accessible American analogy reinforcing the suspension-not-denial point. Connects naturally with Fellay's Hurricane Katrina analogy already in §10 from ep02.

**ARGUMENT — The duty to maintain "good relations with Rome" returns once the emergency ends.** Robinson explicitly states: "if in the future, God willing, the church, the state of the church is restored to normal, then of course, if there's a traditional Catholic pope or a pope that fully approves tradition, then the SSPX would be obliged to, you know, have good relations with Rome." **Value: medium.** Useful for the §10 / §15 closing posture that the SSPX's irregular status is contingent, not principled.

### → §15 Forming Your Stance

**FRAMING — Robinson's invitation to agree on principles even where one disagrees on facts.** "I hope that people would agree that if we're in a state of grave general necessity, then the SSPX is right to do what it's doing… If we're going to disagree, we're going to disagree on whether or not there is a state of necessity, not on the principles that apply when there is a state of necessity." **Value: medium-high.** This is a clean methodological frame for §15: the reader's stance turns on the *factual* judgment (is there a state of necessity?) once the *principles* are granted. §15 currently does not foreground this principles-vs-facts division.
