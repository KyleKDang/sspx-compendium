## Summary

Episode 6 is the central polemical episode of the series: a direct, point-by-point answer to the most-circulated charge against the SSPX in 2026 — that consecrating bishops without papal mandate is *intrinsically* schismatic.
Fr. Luke (himself an adult convert from the Episcopal Church via the University of Dallas) treats the charge with great seriousness.
He frames his exposition around an article by Fr. Bressier (a French priest writing on the FSSP's *Claves.org* publication, first publishing on the question in 1987 and most recently in 2026) and a parallel interview by Cardinal Gerhard Müller (former Prefect of the CDF, in the German Catholic press, March/May 2026).

The episode proceeds in three movements.

**(1) The objection structure.** Fr. Bressier's article (which Fr. Luke takes to express the FSSP's mind, since *Claves* published it under their official banner and the FSSP itself published a parallel document on April 11, 2026) develops four objections, all resting on the same single premise:

- Objection 1: Choosing a candidate for the episcopacy is itself an act of jurisdiction.
- Objection 2: The separability of the power of order and the power of jurisdiction "is not total" — episcopal consecration confers an "aptitude to rule" that intrinsically demands its complement in jurisdiction.
- Objection 3: The 1988 and announced 2026 SSPX consecrations are doing the same thing as the Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association under Pius XII — withdrawing from papal jurisdiction.
- Objection 4: The SSPX definition of schism is incomplete; schism also includes attack on *communion between the faithful*, and SSPX directives that faithful avoid Novus Ordo masses (and even the Ecclesia Dei groups' masses) constitute such an attack.

The underlying premise of all four — and Fr. Luke is explicit about this — is the formulation at *Lumen Gentium* §21: "Episcopal consecration, together with the office of sanctifying, also confers the office of teaching and of governing, which, however, of its very nature, can be exercised only in hierarchical communion with the head and the members of the college."
On the *Lumen Gentium* §21 reading, the single act of episcopal consecration simultaneously confers a share in the *power* of governing the Church, and that power is *intrinsically ordered* to actual jurisdiction; therefore consecrating a bishop without papal mandate is to claim what only the pope can give.

**(2) The SSPX answer.** Fr. Luke's response is structural, not piecemeal: the premise itself — that episcopal consecration *intrinsically* confers a share in the hierarchy of governance — is a novelty of *Lumen Gentium* §21, contradicted by the perennial Catholic teaching that the hierarchy of orders and the hierarchy of jurisdiction are two distinct hierarchies, the second of which (jurisdiction) is conferred only by direct papal grant (canonical mission).
The argument is anchored in:

- **Pius XII, *Ad Sinarum Gentem*** (October 7, 1954), §12 — "By virtue of the same Will is established the twofold sacred hierarchy, namely, of orders and jurisdiction."
- **Pius XII, *Ad Apostolorum Principis*** (June 29, 1958), §39 — "bishops who have been neither named nor confirmed by the Apostolic See, but who, on the contrary, have been elected and consecrated in defiance of its express orders, enjoy no powers of teaching or of jurisdiction"; and the citation of *Mystici Corporis* (1943): bishops "enjoying ordinary power of jurisdiction which they receive directly from the same Supreme Pontiff."
- **1917 Code of Canon Law**, Canons 108–109 — the twofold hierarchy is codified; jurisdiction is conferred by "canonical mission" from the supreme pontificate, not by the sacrament of orders.
- **1917 CIC Canon 348** — titular bishops "exercise no power in their dioceses, nor do they take possession of them" — the canonical sanction of the order/jurisdiction distinction.
- **Cardinal St. Robert Bellarmine, *De Ecclesia*** — explicitly denying that the episcopal order cannot exist in anyone without jurisdiction, and noting that "against this interpretation… nearly all the monuments of tradition would protest."
- **Joseph Ratzinger, "The Church of Vatican II"** (1966 essay collection) — Ratzinger himself identifies *Lumen Gentium* §21 as a *change* from the perennial teaching: "An evolution of a scope difficult to foresee is expressed in these affirmations… the rigid line of demarcation that had stood for centuries between the power of order and the power of jurisdiction in the spirit of most Western theologians has become passible."

The 1917 CIC carries the perennial teaching forward in two further ways that bear directly on the schism charge:

- **The penalty for unauthorized episcopal consecration was suspension only** (Canon 2370) — not excommunication, and not the *latae sententiae* excommunication of the present law.
  Excommunication was added by the Holy Office Decree of August 9, 1951, in direct response to the Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association crisis [→ §7, already covered in ep02 application].
  Bishop Schneider, addressing the present 2026 situation, has explicitly noted that "all of traditional canon law up until the 1983 code did not punish illicit episcopal consecrations with excommunication, but with suspension. Now, suspension is not an expression of schism."
- **The crime was categorized under "Crimes against the Administration of Sacraments" — not under "Crimes against the Unity of the Church."** This canonical categorization is itself probative of the Church's mind on the act: an unauthorized episcopal consecration was treated as a disorder in the administration of a sacrament (parallel to, e.g., a priest confirming without delegation), not as an attack on the unity of the Church.

**(3) Application to the four objections.** Each of the four objections fails because the underlying *Lumen Gentium* §21 premise fails:

- *Objection 1* (choosing a candidate is an act of jurisdiction) — at most, this is an act of disobedience to the existing law; it is not an act of *claiming* jurisdiction.
  The Pius XII / 1917 CIC framework treats the act under "administration of sacraments," not "unity of the Church."
- *Objection 2* (separability is not total) — the auxiliary and titular bishops who exist in the Church in good standing today, who have the *order* without the *jurisdiction*, are the lived refutation of the premise.
  The pope freely grants or withholds jurisdiction; the SSPX neither claims it nor demands it for its bishops.
- *Objection 3* (parallel to the Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association) — Pius XII's actual writings on the Chinese situation describe a national church *displacing* the canonical hierarchy by force of state coercion, with deposed Catholic bishops in prison and replacement bishops appointed to their sees.
  The SSPX is not deposing any bishop, not claiming a single see, not asking any Catholic to submit to its bishops' jurisdiction.
  The parallel is, in Fr. Luke's word, "absurd on its face."
- *Objection 4* (attack on communion between the faithful) — this misreads Fr. Pagliarani's 2016 statement about the impossibility of integral Catholic life within the official structures.
  The SSPX is not telling its faithful to cross the street to avoid Novus Ordo Catholics; it is telling them to keep apart from the *milieu* in which novel doctrine is preached and a deficient liturgy is celebrated.
  The analogy Fr. Luke draws is a *medical quarantine*, not a *break of communion*.

The episode closes with two positive affirmations — Lefebvre's 1988 sermon's explicit disavowal of separation from Rome ("Far from us be this miserable thought of separating ourselves from Rome. On the contrary, it is in order to manifest our attachment to Rome that we are performing this ceremony") and Fr. Pagliarani's February 2026 framing that the four new SSPX bishops will be "without jurisdiction… solely to help souls… not with that hierarchical authority that belongs to the hierarchical Church properly so-called."
The closing patristic anchor is from Pius XII's *Ad Sinarum Gentem* §26: "When men demand things contrary to the Divine Will, then it is necessary to put into practice the maxim of St. Peter: 'We must obey God rather than men.'"

The episode's load-bearing contributions for the compendium are: (a) the *Lumen Gentium* §21 verbatim text as the doctrinal pivot at issue — currently absent from §5's treatment of the Council; (b) the order/jurisdiction distinction as the perennial teaching — anchored in *Ad Sinarum Gentem* §12, *Ad Apostolorum Principis* §39 + the *Mystici Corporis* citation, 1917 CIC Canons 108–109; (c) the 1917 CIC's categorization of unauthorized consecration as a *sacramental-disorder* offense (not a *unity-of-Church* offense) — sharpening the existing §7 ep02 treatment; (d) the auxiliary-and-titular-bishop evidence (1917 CIC Canon 348) for the lived practice of order-without-jurisdiction; (e) the Ratzinger 1966 admission that *Lumen Gentium* §21 represented a *change* from the perennial teaching; (f) Müller's 2026 *Communio*-press articulation of the present schism charge — the third 2025–2026 senior-curial statement against the SSPX (alongside Fernández's May 13, 2026 DDF statement and the Müller / Sarah joint appeal of February 25, 2026); (g) the Pagliarani February 2026 statement on the auxiliary-bishop status of the four bishops to be consecrated.

## Items grouped by destination compendium section

### → §5 Vatican II — Part I (Hermeneutics) or new sub-section on *Lumen Gentium* §21

**VERBATIM QUOTE — *Lumen Gentium* §21 — the doctrinal pivot at issue in the schism charge:**

> "Episcopal consecration, together with the office of sanctifying, also confers the office of teaching and of governing, which, however, of its very nature, can be exercised only in hierarchical communion with the head and the members of the college."
>
> — *Lumen Gentium* §21 (November 21, 1964)

**Value: very high.** §5 currently does not treat *Lumen Gentium* §21, despite §21 being one of the central doctrinal pivots the SSPX identifies in the Council.
The 2026 schism charge against the SSPX rests on §21's reading that episcopal consecration *itself* confers a share in the office of governing — a reading the SSPX argues is incompatible with the perennial teaching codified at *Ad Sinarum Gentem* §12 and 1917 CIC Canons 108–109.
The current §5 Part I (Hermeneutics) and Part III (*Unitatis Redintegratio*) leave this gap; adding a brief sub-section either in Part I, or as a parallel treatment to *DH* / *UR* / *NA*, captures the doctrinal substance of the dispute that drives the 2026 schism objection.

**ANALYSIS — Joseph Ratzinger's 1966 admission that *Lumen Gentium* §21 represented a *change* from the perennial teaching.** In the 1966 essay collection *The Church of Vatican II* (the English-language predecessor of what became *Theological Highlights of Vatican II*), the then-Father Ratzinger wrote that "an evolution of a scope difficult to foresee is expressed in these affirmations… the rigid line of demarcation that had stood for centuries between the power of order and the power of jurisdiction in the spirit of most Western theologians has become passible. The strong bond between these two realities, which at bottom are only one, appears to view."
Ratzinger himself, the post-Vatican-II Council's most prominent theological commentator, identified the §21 formulation as a deliberate post-conciliar reformulation, not a recapitulation of prior teaching.
**Value: high.** The Ratzinger admission is the cleanest internal evidence for the SSPX's contention that §21 is a *change* — not a simple restatement — of the perennial teaching.
Strong fit alongside the *Lumen Gentium* §21 verbatim quote.

### → §7 Canon Law

**FACT + CANONICAL-CATEGORY ARGUMENT — Under the 1917 Code, the crime of consecrating a bishop without papal mandate was categorized under "Crimes against the Administration of Sacraments," not under "Crimes against the Unity of the Church."** §7 already has the ep02 treatment of the historical penalty (suspension only in 1917 CIC Canon 2370; *latae sententiae* excommunication introduced by the 1951 Holy Office Decree; carried into 1983 CIC Canon 1382 / 2021 CIC Canon 1387).
What is *not* yet in §7 is the canonical-category point: the 1917 codifiers — Cardinal Gasparri working under St. Pius X and Benedict XV — placed unauthorized episcopal consecration in the *same canonical category* as a priest illicitly conferring confirmation without delegation, not in the same category as schism.
This categorization is itself probative of the pre-1951 Church's mind on the act.
**Value: high.** Strong fit as a new paragraph in §7 immediately after the existing Canon 2370 / 1951 Decree / *Ad Apostolorum Principis* paragraph.

**VERBATIM QUOTE — *Ad Sinarum Gentem* §12 on the twofold hierarchy:**

> "By virtue of the same Will is established the twofold sacred hierarchy, namely, of orders and jurisdiction."
>
> — Pius XII, *Ad Sinarum Gentem* §12 (October 7, 1954)

**VERBATIM QUOTE — *Ad Apostolorum Principis* §39 on bishops without papal mandate:**

> "Bishops who have been neither named nor confirmed by the Apostolic See, but who, on the contrary, have been elected and consecrated in defiance of its express orders, enjoy no powers of teaching or of jurisdiction, since jurisdiction passes to bishops only through the Roman Pontiff."
>
> "As far as his own diocese is concerned, each bishop feeds the flock entrusted to him as a true shepherd and rules it in the name of Christ. Yet in exercising this office they are not altogether independent but are subordinate to the lawful authority of the Roman Pontiff, although enjoying ordinary power of jurisdiction which they receive directly from the same Supreme Pontiff."
>
> — Pius XII, *Ad Apostolorum Principis* §39 (June 29, 1958), citing *Mystici Corporis* (1943)

**Value: very high.** These two passages together codify the perennial teaching on the order/jurisdiction distinction that the SSPX argues *Lumen Gentium* §21 silently overturned.
Strong fit as new paragraphs in §7's existing treatment of the Chinese schism background to the 1951 / *Ad Apostolorum Principis* developments.

**FACT — 1917 CIC Canons 108–109.** Canon 108 §3 codifies the twofold hierarchy ("by reason of orders [the hierarchy] consists of bishops, priests and ministers; by reason of jurisdiction it consists of the supreme pontificate and the subordinate episcopate"); Canon 109 establishes the modes of investiture ("by sacred ordination" for orders; "by canonical mission" for jurisdiction).
Canon 109's "canonical mission" clause was dropped from the 1983 Code, in keeping with the *Lumen Gentium* §21 reformulation.
**Value: high.** The 1917 → 1983 codification change is itself probative evidence of the pivot.
Strong fit as a brief paragraph in §7.

### → §10 Case For — Part VIII (Anticipated Objections)

**ARGUMENT — The auxiliary-and-titular-bishop refutation of the "denatured episcopate" charge.** The SSPX bishops, present and announced, are essentially in the canonical position of auxiliary bishops: validly consecrated, exercising the *power of orders* (Mass, confirmation, ordination), but without ordinary jurisdiction.
This is the *normal* canonical position of many bishops in the Catholic Church in good standing — auxiliary bishops in large dioceses (New York, Chicago, Los Angeles), titular bishops *in partibus infidelium*, and (since 1983 CIC) the entire class of bishops emeritus who, on the *Lumen Gentium* §21 reading, would be "denatured."
The charge that an SSPX bishop without jurisdiction is a "denatured episcopate" (the term used by Fr. Bissig in 1989 and Fr. Bressier in 2026) proves too much: it would equally condemn the entire class of auxiliary, titular, and emeritus bishops in good standing throughout the Catholic Church.
**Value: high.** This sharpens §10 Part VIII's existing "parallel church" response (already strengthened across ep02 and ep04) with the most concrete canonical refutation.
Strong fit as a new sub-section or expansion of the existing parallel-church paragraph.

**ARGUMENT — Schism vs. disobedience: the perennial canonical distinction.** Schism (1917 CIC Canon 1325 / 1983 CIC Canon 751) requires *systematic and habitual refusal of dependence* on the Roman Pontiff.
Transient disobedience — even sinful disobedience — does not constitute schism in the canonical sense.
Fr. Luke's family analogies (the little boy who refuses to clean his room is disobedient but not a runaway; the teenage runaway who declares "who are you to tell me what to do" is in a structurally different position) capture the substance of the canonical distinction.
The SSPX has consistently denied that it rejects in principle the authority of the Roman Pontiff to give jurisdiction; it has consistently affirmed that authority while declining to apply it to the present specific case under the canonical exception of necessity.
**Value: high.** Reinforces §10 Part VIII's existing schism-objection treatment with the schism-vs-disobedience canonical distinction.
Strong fit.

**ANALOGY — The drunk-or-insane father.** Fr. Luke develops a family analogy: a child does not deny his father's authority by refusing to obey a command issued under drunkenness, mental incapacity, or in matters beyond the father's competence (e.g., the father commanding his daughter into prostitution or his son into a marriage against his vocation).
Refusing the command in such cases is not disobedience to authority but recognition of its proper limits.
**Value: medium-high.** This is a strong intuitive analogy that complements (without duplicating) Fellay's Alzheimer's-parents analogy [→ §15 Part VII] and Schneider's firefighter parable [→ §10 Part IV].
Strong fit as a brief paragraph in §10 Part VIII's "disobeys the pope" response.
