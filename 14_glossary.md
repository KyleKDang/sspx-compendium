# Section 14: Glossary of Terms
## The SSPX Compendium

> **How to use this section.** This glossary defines every technical term used throughout the compendium - canonical, theological, liturgical, and historical. Entries are written to be precise without being pedantic; the goal is working fluency, not academic exhaustiveness. Terms are organized alphabetically within four categories: (I) Canonical and Legal Terms, (II) Theological and Doctrinal Terms, (III) Liturgical Terms, and (IV) Historical and Institutional Terms. Cross-references to relevant sections of the compendium are included where the term is analyzed in depth. Where a term has both a technical Catholic meaning and a common misuse, both are noted.

---

## Category I: Canonical and Legal Terms

---

### Anathema
A solemn ecclesiastical condemnation of greater severity than excommunication; typically attached to the canons of Ecumenical Councils to mark propositions as definitively contrary to Catholic faith. Used at the Council of Trent ("let him be anathema") to condemn specific Protestant errors. Distinct from excommunication, which is a medicinal penalty; an anathema is a condemnation of the proposition, not necessarily of the person. [→ §6 Doctrinal Positions; §7 Canon Law]

---

### Apostolic Administration
A canonical structure by which a territory or community is placed under the pastoral care of a bishop appointed directly by the Holy See, rather than under a diocesan ordinary. The *Apostolic Administration of Saint John Mary Vianney* in Campos, Brazil (2002) is the model most relevant to the SSPX - a fully canonical traditional community that reconciled with Rome and was placed under an apostolic administrator. [→ §1 Overview; §3 Key Figures]

---

### Apostolic See
The See of Rome, considered as the seat of the Bishop of Rome - the Pope - and his curial apparatus. "Apostolic See" is used interchangeably with "Holy See" in canonical usage. Penalties "reserved to the Apostolic See" (such as the 1988 excommunications under Canon 1382) can only be lifted by the Pope or by someone acting with explicit papal delegation. [→ §7 Canon Law; §8 Papal Documents]

---

### *Apostolica Sedes* (See: Apostolic See)

---

### Automatic Excommunication (See: *Latae Sententiae*)

---

### Canonical Mission
The formal commissioning of a cleric by competent authority to exercise his orders within the Church's structures. A validly ordained priest has the sacramental power to celebrate Mass and absolve sins, but he requires a canonical mission - given by his bishop through incardination - to exercise these powers lawfully in public ministry. SSPX priests have valid orders but lack canonical mission; this is the precise meaning of their "irregular canonical status." [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### Canonical Irregularity
The condition of operating outside the full canonical structures of the Catholic Church without being formally under a canonical censure or penalty. Distinguished from schism (which involves withdrawal of submission to the Pope) and from excommunication (a specific canonical penalty). The SSPX's current status: not excommunicated, not formally schismatic, but without canonical mission. An unprecedented condition in modern Catholic canon law. [→ §7 Canon Law; §1 Overview]

---

### Canon Law
The body of laws governing the Catholic Church, currently codified in the *Code of Canon Law* promulgated by John Paul II in 1983 (*Codex Iuris Canonici*, CIC 1983). Replaced the 1917 Pio-Benedictine Code. The Eastern Catholic Churches have their own code (*Codex Canonum Ecclesiarum Orientalium*, CCEO, 1990). Book VI of CIC 1983 (on penal sanctions) was revised in 2021 by Pope Francis's *Pascite gregem Dei*, renumbering some canons (notably Canon 1382 became Canon 1387). [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### Censure
A canonical penalty intended to correct and bring about the amendment of the offender, as distinguished from purely punitive penalties. Excommunication, interdict, and suspension are all censures. Because censures are medicinal (aimed at correction), they are lifted when the offender shows amendment. The 1988 excommunications were censures; they were lifted in 2009 as a pastoral gesture toward reconciliation, not because the SSPX retracted the consecrations. [→ §7 Canon Law; §8 Papal Documents]

---

### Communion (Ecclesial)
Full ecclesial communion with the Catholic Church involves three elements: (1) baptism and profession of the Catholic faith; (2) participation in the sacramental life of the Church; and (3) submission to the governance of the Church through its hierarchical structure, including the Bishop of Rome. The SSPX's faithful fulfill (1) and, in most cases, (2) in substance. Their relationship to (3) is the contested question. [→ §7 Canon Law; §11 Case Against]

---

### Declaration (*Declaratio*)
The lowest of the three categories of Vatican II documents (Constitutions, Decrees, and Declarations). A Declaration does not carry the same weight as a dogmatic or pastoral constitution. *Dignitatis Humanae* (religious liberty) and *Nostra Aetate* (non-Christian religions) are Declarations. This matters for assessing the authoritative weight of the contested Vatican II texts. [→ §5 Vatican II]

---

### Delegation (Marriage)
The canonical authorization given to a priest by a local ordinary or pastor, permitting him to validly witness a Catholic marriage in a specific territory. Without delegation, a Catholic marriage witnessed by a priest lacking it is normally invalid. SSPX priests do not automatically possess delegation; Pope Francis's 2017 letter to Cardinal Müller addressed this by directing local ordinaries to grant delegation to SSPX priests for the marriages of their faithful. [→ §7 Canon Law; §9 Sacramental Questions]

---

### *De Fide Divina et Catholica*
Literally, "of divine and Catholic faith." The highest category of Catholic doctrine - propositions that must be believed because they have been proposed by the Church (through solemn definition or universal ordinary Magisterium) as divinely revealed. Denial constitutes heresy in the canonical sense. Vatican II, as a pastoral council without formal definitions, did not produce doctrines of this category; its teachings are of the lower categories of authoritative Church teaching. [→ §5 Vatican II; §6 Doctrinal Positions]

---

### Decree (*Decretum*)
The second of the three categories of Vatican II documents. More authoritative than a Declaration but less than a Constitution. *Unitatis Redintegratio* (ecumenism) and *Ad Gentes* (missionary activity) are Decrees. [→ §5 Vatican II]

---

### Excommunication
A canonical censure that excludes the person from participation in the sacramental life of the Church and from the exercise of ecclesiastical offices and ministries. The most severe of the three canonical censures. In the SSPX context: the four 1988 bishops incurred *latae sententiae* excommunication upon the act of consecration; Lefebvre had been suspended *a divinis* (a lesser penalty) since 1976. All excommunications of the four living bishops were lifted by Benedict XVI in 2009. [→ §7 Canon Law; §8 Papal Documents]

---

### Faculty (Canonical)
The specific authorization to perform a sacramental or jurisdictional act. Distinct from the power of orders (which is received at ordination) and from canonical mission (which is received by incardination). A priest can have valid orders but require a specific faculty to licitly or validly exercise them in certain contexts - for example, the faculty to hear confessions (Canon 966) or to witness marriages (Canon 1111). Pope Francis granted SSPX priests the faculty to hear confessions permanently in 2016. [→ §7 Canon Law; §9 Sacramental Questions]

---

### *Ferendae Sententiae*
A canonical penalty that must be imposed by a competent authority through a judicial or administrative process - it does not exist until it is formally declared. Contrasted with *latae sententiae* (automatic) penalties. Most canonical penalties are *ferendae sententiae*. [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### Incardination
The canonical process by which a cleric is formally attached to a particular church (diocese, religious institute, or similar structure). Every Catholic cleric must be incardinated somewhere. SSPX priests are incardinated into the Society itself, but since the Society lacks canonical recognition, this incardination is irregular. [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### Interdict
A canonical censure that forbids participation in certain liturgical actions in a particular place or for a particular person. Less severe than excommunication; relevant here primarily because it helps distinguish the different categories of canonical penalty. Not the penalty invoked in the SSPX context. [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### Jurisdiction
The authority to govern - to make binding decisions in the forum of conscience (internal forum) or in the external life of the Church (external forum). Distinct from the power of orders (which enables sacramental actions) though related to it. The power of absolution, for instance, requires both valid priestly orders and jurisdictional authority (the faculty). The 2016 papal grant of confession faculties to SSPX priests addressed the jurisdictional dimension. [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### *Latae Sententiae*
Literally "sentence already passed." A canonical penalty incurred automatically by the very act itself, at the moment of its commission, without any judicial declaration or process. The person becomes subject to the penalty the instant the act occurs. The 1988 excommunication of Lefebvre and the four bishops was *latae sententiae* - it applied the moment the consecrations took place, not when John Paul II subsequently declared it. [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### *Lex*
Latin for "law." Often encountered in two phrases central to this document: *lex orandi* (the law of prayer - the Church's liturgical practice) and *lex credendi* (the law of belief - the Church's doctrinal content). The principle *lex orandi, lex credendi* holds that these are inseparably related: how the Church prays expresses and shapes what the Church believes. The SSPX's entire liturgical argument rests on this principle. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §6 Doctrinal Positions]

---

### Magisterium
The teaching authority of the Catholic Church, exercised through the Pope and the bishops in communion with him. Divided into:
- **Solemn (Extraordinary) Magisterium:** *Ex cathedra* papal definitions and conciliar definitions with canons. Infallible. Examples: the definitions of the Immaculate Conception (1854) and the Assumption (1950); the canons of Trent and Vatican I.
- **Ordinary Universal Magisterium:** The consistent teaching of popes and bishops throughout the Church, even without formal definition, when proposed as definitively belonging to the faith. Also infallible.
- **Authoritative Non-Defining Magisterium:** Teaching that demands religious submission of intellect and will but does not claim infallibility for the specific formulation. Vatican II's pastoral teachings fall primarily here.

Understanding which level of Magisterium a given teaching belongs to is essential for evaluating the SSPX dispute. [→ §5 Vatican II; §6 Doctrinal Positions]

---

### Mandate (Papal)
The explicit permission from the Pope required for the valid episcopal consecration of a bishop in the Latin Rite. Canon 1382 (now 1387) attaches *latae sententiae* excommunication to episcopal consecrations performed without this mandate. The absence of papal mandate is the precise canonical violation underlying the 1988 consecrations and all subsequent SSPX consecrations. [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### Ordinary
A term for the bishop of a diocese (the "local ordinary") or the Pope (the "supreme ordinary"). Also used for religious superiors with jurisdiction over their communities. In the context of SSPX marriages: "local ordinary" refers to the diocesan bishop, whose delegation SSPX priests require to validly witness marriages in his territory. [→ §9 Sacramental Questions]

---

### *Pia Unio*
A "pious association" - a canonical category for recognized groups of Catholics with shared religious or charitable purposes. The SSPX was originally erected as a *pia unio* with canonical approval from the Diocese of Lausanne in 1970. Its canonical status was suppressed in 1975 by Bishop Mamie. [→ §2 Timeline; §3 Key Figures]

---

### Personal Prelature
A canonical structure - established by Vatican II and codified in CIC 1983 - consisting of a prelate, clergy, and (optionally) laity, organized for particular pastoral purposes and operating across diocesan boundaries under direct supervision of the Holy See. Opus Dei is the only existing personal prelature. The 2012 negotiations proposed regularizing the SSPX as a personal prelature - a model the Society ultimately refused on doctrinal terms. [→ §2 Timeline; §12 Debate]

---

### Schism
Per Canon 751: "the withdrawal of submission to the Supreme Pontiff or from communion with the members of the Church subject to him." Schism is distinct from heresy (denial of defined doctrine) and apostasy (complete repudiation of the faith). Its key elements are *active withdrawal* and *denial of authority*, not merely disobedience. The current Roman position is that the SSPX is not in formal schism; Cardinal Castrillón Hoyos and Benedict XVI have both stated this explicitly. [→ §7 Canon Law; §12 Debate 1]

---

### *Status Necessitatis* (State of Necessity)
The canonical principle, codified in Canon 1323 §4, that exempts from canonical penalty a person who acted "due to necessity or grave inconvenience" - provided the act is not intrinsically evil and does not tend to the harm of souls. The SSPX invokes this principle to argue the 1988 consecrations did not incur the excommunication of Canon 1382. Whether the conditions for the state of necessity were met in 1988 is the most contested canonical question in the entire debate. [→ §7 Canon Law; §10 Case For; §11 Case Against; §12 Debate 2]

---

### Supplied Jurisdiction (*Supplet Ecclesia*)
The principle of Canon 144: in cases of common error or positive and probable doubt, the Church supplies the jurisdictional authority that would otherwise be missing. Applied to SSPX confessions (before the 2016 papal grant) and SSPX marriages (before the 2017 framework): the Church supplies the missing faculty when the faithful approach SSPX priests in good faith. Canon 144 §2 explicitly applies to confessions (Canon 966) and marriage witnessing (Canon 1111). [→ §7 Canon Law; §9 Sacramental Questions]

---

### Suspension *a divinis*
A canonical censure barring a priest or bishop from exercising his priestly functions - celebrating Mass, administering sacraments, preaching with authority. Does not invalidate orders; a suspended priest remains a validly ordained priest, but his exercise of priestly ministry is illicit. Lefebvre was suspended *a divinis* in 1976 after ordaining thirteen priests against Vatican orders. He publicly defied the suspension. [→ §2 Timeline]

---

### Validity vs. Liceity
The most important distinction in the SSPX canonical discussion. A sacrament is **valid** when it really and truly occurs - when the necessary conditions of matter, form, minister, and intention are met. A sacrament is **licit** when it is performed in conformity with Church law. These are independent: a sacrament can be valid but illicit. All SSPX sacraments are valid; their liceity varies depending on the specific sacrament and the specific canonical grants in force. *This distinction is essential to understanding every canonical claim made about the SSPX.* [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

## Category II: Theological and Doctrinal Terms

---

### *Alter Christus*
Latin: "another Christ." The traditional description of the Catholic priest as one who, by ordination, is configured to Christ as Priest, acting *in persona Christi* at the altar and in the confessional. The SSPX's doctrine of the priesthood insists on this characterization, which they argue has been weakened in post-conciliar theology by treating the priest primarily as a "presider" or community leader. [→ §6 Position 2]

---

### Apostolic Succession
The unbroken chain of episcopal consecrations from the apostles to the present. A bishop validly consecrated by a bishop who was validly consecrated, going back without interruption to the apostolic age, has apostolic succession and can validly ordain priests and consecrate bishops. SSPX bishops and priests have valid apostolic succession; the question of canonical mission is a separate question. [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### *Cantate Domino* (See: Council of Florence in §13)

---

### Deposit of Faith
The totality of divine Revelation - contained in Scripture and Tradition - entrusted by Christ to the apostles and through them to the Church, to be preserved and transmitted in its integrity. The Magisterium does not create doctrine; it guards and authentically interprets the deposit of faith. The SSPX's claim is that certain post-conciliar developments have introduced novelties incompatible with the deposit - not additions to it, but contradictions of it. [→ §6 Position 7; §5 Vatican II]

---

### Development of Doctrine
The legitimate growth in the Church's understanding of the deposit of faith, without contradiction of what has been previously defined. Articulated most fully by Bl. John Henry Newman (*An Essay on the Development of Christian Doctrine*, 1845). Development is genuine and recognized by the SSPX in principle; the dispute is whether specific Vatican II teachings represent development (deepening of prior teaching) or contradiction (denial of prior teaching). [→ §5 Vatican II; §12 Debate 3]

---

### *Extra Ecclesiam Nulla Salus*
Latin: "Outside the Church there is no salvation." The traditional Catholic doctrine that the Catholic Church is the necessary means of salvation. Defined at the Council of Florence (1442). Qualified - but not abandoned - by Pius IX's *Quanto Conficiamur Moerore* (1863): those who are invincibly ignorant may be saved through extraordinary grace, but salvation always comes from Christ through the Church. The SSPX holds this doctrine in its traditional formulation; they argue post-conciliar ecumenical frameworks have effectively abandoned it. [→ §6 Position 8; §5 *Nostra Aetate*]

---

### Heresy
The obstinate denial or obstinate doubt, after reception of baptism, of a truth that must be believed by divine and Catholic faith (Canon 751). Distinguished from schism (refusal of submission to authority) and apostasy (total repudiation of Christianity). The SSPX does not accuse the post-conciliar popes of heresy in the strict canonical sense; their charge is something less - the toleration and promulgation of theological errors and ambiguities, not formal denial of defined doctrine. [→ §7 Canon Law]

---

### Hermeneutic of Continuity
Pope Benedict XVI's framework for receiving Vatican II (articulated in his 2005 Christmas Address to the Roman Curia): the Council's teachings must be read in continuity with the prior Magisterium, not as a rupture from it. Contrasted with the "hermeneutic of rupture," which treats the Council as a new beginning that supersedes what came before. The SSPX accepts this principle in theory but argues that its application to certain contested passages is impossible without abandoning the prior definitive teaching. [→ §5 Vatican II; §12 Debate 4]

---

### Hypostatic Union
The Catholic dogma that Jesus Christ is one divine Person in two natures - human and divine - without confusion, change, division, or separation (Council of Chalcedon, 451). Not directly disputed in the SSPX context but cited here because: (a) it illustrates the kind of defined, irreversible doctrine the SSPX invokes when arguing prior teaching cannot be reversed; (b) all parties in the SSPX debate fully accept it, which clarifies that the dispute is not about the central dogmas of Christianity. [→ §6 Doctrinal Positions]

---

### Infallibility (Papal)
The charism by which the Pope, when speaking *ex cathedra* - in his capacity as supreme teacher of the universal Church, defining a doctrine of faith or morals to be held by the whole Church - is preserved from error by the Holy Spirit. Defined at Vatican I (*Pastor Aeternus*, 1870). The SSPX accepts this dogma; they are not anti-papal. The dispute is about whether specific post-conciliar papal teachings qualify as *ex cathedra* definitions (they do not) and whether non-infallible teaching can contradict prior definitive teaching (the SSPX says yes in certain cases; Rome says no). [→ §6 Position 6]

---

### *In Persona Christi*
Latin: "in the person of Christ." The theological formula describing the priest's sacramental role at the altar and in the confessional - acting not in his own person but as a representation of Christ himself. Essential to the SSPX's doctrine of the priesthood and the sacrificial character of the Mass. [→ §6 Position 1; §6 Position 2]

---

### *Lamentabili Sane* (1907)
The syllabus of sixty-five condemned Modernist propositions issued by the Holy Office under Pius X, accompanying *Pascendi Dominici Gregis*. Condemned propositions include claims that dogmas evolve into different meanings over time, that the Church's teaching authority must adapt itself to historical progress, and that modern Catholicism cannot be reconciled with true science. The SSPX treats this document as defining the heresy they argue characterizes certain post-conciliar trends. [→ §13 Source Library; §6 Position 9]

---

### *Lex Orandi, Lex Credendi*
Latin: "the law of prayer is the law of belief." The ancient principle that the Church's liturgical practice both expresses and shapes her doctrinal faith. How the Church prays is not incidental to what the Church believes; liturgical forms carry theological weight. The SSPX's entire liturgical argument rests on this principle: if the new Mass expresses the doctrine of the Eucharist less clearly, it will over time weaken Catholic Eucharistic faith. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §6 Position 1]

---

### Modernism
The theological system condemned by Pope Pius X in *Pascendi Dominici Gregis* (1907): characterized by treating doctrine as historically conditioned rather than objectively revealed, reducing revelation to religious experience, and allowing dogmas to evolve into genuinely different content over time. Sometimes called the "synthesis of all heresies" (Pius X's phrase). The SSPX argues that Modernism, while condemned and driven underground by Pius X, reemerged in the post-conciliar period and shapes certain post-conciliar theological trends. [→ §6 Position 9]

---

### Ordinary Magisterium (See: Magisterium)

---

### *Propitiatory Sacrifice*
A sacrifice offered to God in reparation for sin, which wins his mercy and placates his justice. The Council of Trent defined the Mass as a "true and proper" propitiatory sacrifice, offered not merely for praise and thanksgiving but for the remission of sins. The SSPX argues that the Novus Ordo's revised Offertory prayers and its overall framework express the propitiatory character of the Mass less clearly than the traditional rite. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §6 Position 1]

---

### *Quod Ubique, Quod Semper, Quod Ab Omnibus*
Latin: "what has been believed everywhere, always, by all." The *Vincentian Canon* - the standard for identifying authentic Catholic Tradition from St. Vincent of Lérins's *Commonitorium* (434 AD). The SSPX invokes this as the test by which prior definitive teaching is identified and by which novelties are measured. [→ §6 Position 7; §13 Source Library]

---

### Real Presence
The Catholic doctrine that Christ is truly, really, and substantially present - body, blood, soul, and divinity - in the Eucharist after the words of consecration, under the appearances (accidents) of bread and wine. Defined at the Council of Trent (Session XIII, 1551). All parties in the SSPX dispute affirm this doctrine; the dispute is about whether the Novus Ordo's liturgical formulations express it with sufficient clarity. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §6 Position 1]

---

### Religious Submission of Intellect and Will (*Obsequium Religiosum*)
The response owed by Catholics to authoritative but non-infallible Church teaching. Per Canon 752: "Although not an assent of faith, a religious submission of intellect and will must be given to a doctrine which the Supreme Pontiff or the college of bishops declares concerning faith or morals when they exercise the authentic magisterium." Distinguished from the assent of divine and Catholic faith owed to defined dogmas. The mainstream critics of the SSPX invoke this canon to argue the Society is required to submit to Vatican II teachings even where it cannot perfectly reconcile them with prior teaching. [→ §5 Vatican II; §12 Debate 3]

---

### Sacred Tradition (See: Tradition)

---

### Sedevacantism
The position that the Chair of Peter (*sedes Petri*) is currently vacant (*vacante*) because the present or recent claimants to the papacy have fallen into heresy and therefore lost their office. Most sedevacantists place the vacancy at some point during the pontificate of John XXIII, Paul VI, or later. The SSPX explicitly and emphatically rejects sedevacantism; they recognize the current pope as the valid Pope. This is one of the most important distinctions in the Catholic traditionalist landscape: the SSPX is not sedevacantist. [→ §1 Overview; §6 Position 6]

---

### Social Kingship of Christ
The doctrine that Christ's Kingship extends not only over individual souls but over all human societies - nations, governments, and civil institutions - which are therefore obligated to acknowledge him and to order their laws according to divine law. Defined and elaborated by Pope Pius XI in *Quas Primas* (1925). The SSPX considers this doctrine implicitly denied by *Dignitatis Humanae*'s framework. [→ §6 Position 3]

---

### *Subsistit In*
Latin: "subsists in." The formula used in *Lumen Gentium* §8 to describe the relationship between the Church of Christ and the Catholic Church: the Church of Christ "*subsistit in*" the Catholic Church, rather than the pre-conciliar formula "*is*" the Catholic Church. The SSPX argued this language could be read as a branch theory - implying the Church of Christ extends beyond the Catholic Church. The CDF's 2007 *Responses* clarified that "subsists in" means the Catholic Church *is* the Church of Christ, substantially addressing this textual concern. [→ §5 Vatican II]

---

### Transubstantiation
The doctrine that at the words of consecration, the entire substance of the bread is changed into the substance of Christ's body, and the entire substance of the wine is changed into the substance of Christ's blood, while the accidents (appearances) of bread and wine remain. Defined at the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) and elaborated at Trent (Session XIII, 1551). Affirmed by all parties in the SSPX dispute. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §6 Position 1]

---

### Tradition (Sacred)
The deposit of divine Revelation as transmitted through the living life of the Church - distinct from but co-equal with Sacred Scripture as a source of Revelation. Includes the liturgical tradition, the Fathers of the Church, the universal teaching of the bishops, and the *sensus fidelium* (the sense of the faithful). The SSPX's entire framework depends on a specific understanding of Tradition: that it is unchangeable in substance, however it may develop in expression, and that no subsequent teaching can contradict it. The 1988 *Ecclesia Dei* accused the SSPX of an "incomplete and contradictory" notion of Tradition. [→ §6 Position 7; §8 Papal Documents]

---

### Vincentian Canon (See: *Quod Ubique, Quod Semper, Quod Ab Omnibus*)

## Category III: Liturgical Terms

---

### *Ad Orientem*
Latin: "to the east." The traditional posture of celebration in which both priest and people face the same direction - toward the liturgical east (the altar), symbolizing the shared orientation of priest and people toward God. Contrasted with *versus populum* (facing the people), which became standard after the 1969 reform. The SSPX celebrates exclusively *ad orientem*. Cardinal Ratzinger in *The Spirit of the Liturgy* (2000) argued for the restoration of *ad orientem* celebration. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### Canon of the Mass (Roman Canon)
The central Eucharistic Prayer of the Roman Rite - one of the most ancient liturgical texts in Christianity, dating in its basic form to at least the 4th century. Also called *Eucharistic Prayer I* in the Novus Ordo. The only Eucharistic Prayer in the 1962 Missal; one of four (later seven) in the Novus Ordo, and the least frequently used in practice. The SSPX considers the marginalization of the Roman Canon in favor of newer, shorter Eucharistic Prayers a doctrinal loss. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### Extraordinary Form
The name given by Pope Benedict XVI in *Summorum Pontificum* (2007) to the Traditional Latin Mass (1962 Missal) - the "extraordinary expression" of the *lex orandi* of the Roman Rite, as distinguished from the Ordinary Form (the Novus Ordo). This nomenclature was accepted by mainstream traditionalists and rejected by Francis's *Traditionis Custodes* (2021), which returned to describing the Novus Ordo as "the unique expression" of the Roman Rite. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §8 Papal Documents]

---

### GIRM (General Instruction of the Roman Missal)
The introductory document to the Roman Missal that governs how the Mass is to be celebrated. First issued in 1969 with the Novus Ordo; the original §7 definition of the Mass was criticized by the Ottaviani Intervention and revised by Paul VI in 1970. The GIRM is a practical implementation document, not a doctrinal definition, but its formulations have doctrinal weight. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### *Mysterium Fidei*
Latin: "mystery of faith." In the Traditional Latin Mass (and in the original 1969 Novus Ordo), these words appear *within* the words of consecration of the chalice: "...this is the chalice of my blood, of the new and eternal covenant, the mystery of faith, which shall be shed for you and for many for the remission of sins." In the Novus Ordo as revised, *Mysterium Fidei* was removed from the consecration formula and became a separate acclamation ("The mystery of faith!") after the consecration. The SSPX considers this change a doctrinal weakening. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### Novus Ordo Missae
Latin: "New Order of Mass." The revised Roman Rite of the Mass promulgated by Pope Paul VI in 1969 through the apostolic constitution *Missale Romanum*; the Ordinary Form of the Roman Rite as celebrated in the vast majority of Catholic churches today. Sometimes called the Mass of Paul VI, the post-conciliar Mass, or the Ordinary Form. The SSPX holds it is valid when properly celebrated but theologically deficient in its formulations. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### Offertory
The section of the Mass in which the bread and wine are presented and offered to God before the Eucharistic Prayer and consecration. The traditional Offertory prayers (Suscipe sancte Pater; Offerimus tibi Domine) are explicitly sacrificial in language; the Novus Ordo replaced them with prayers modeled on Jewish *berakhah* blessings. This change is the liturgical change the Ottaviani Intervention treats most extensively, and one of the SSPX's specific textual concerns. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### Ordinary Form
The name for the Novus Ordo Missae as established by *Summorum Pontificum* (2007) - the "ordinary expression" of the *lex orandi* of the Roman Rite, as distinguished from the Extraordinary Form (the 1962 Missal). [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §8 Papal Documents]

---

### *Pro Multis* / "For Many"
Latin: "for many." The traditional translation of Christ's words over the chalice at the Last Supper (Matthew 26:28; Mark 14:24). In the Latin Novus Ordo, "*pro multis*" was preserved; however, many vernacular translations rendered it as "for all" rather than "for many" - a translation the SSPX (among others) criticized as obscuring the Catholic theology of applied redemption. Pope Benedict XVI issued a directive in 2006 requiring "for many" in all vernacular translations; this was implemented in the English-speaking world with the revised Roman Missal of 2011. The resolution of this dispute represents one of the clearest SSPX-adjacent vindications by the mainstream Church. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### *Sacrosanctum Concilium*
The Second Vatican Council's Constitution on the Sacred Liturgy (December 4, 1963); the foundational document authorizing liturgical reform. The SSPX argues that the 1969 reform went far beyond what *Sacrosanctum Concilium* itself mandated - particularly noting that the Constitution explicitly called for the preservation of Latin (§36, §54) and Gregorian chant (§116), both of which largely disappeared from post-conciliar parish practice. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo; §5 Vatican II]

---

### Traditional Latin Mass (TLM)
The Roman Rite of Mass as it developed organically from the early Church and was codified by Pope Pius V in the *Missale Romanum* of 1570; celebrated in essentially the same form through the pontificate of John XXIII (1962 Missal). Also called the Tridentine Mass, the Old Mass, the Extraordinary Form (since 2007), the Usus Antiquior, or the Mass of All Times. The SSPX celebrates this Mass exclusively; it is the center of the Society's liturgical life and theological self-understanding. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### Usus Antiquior
Latin: "the older use." A neutral term for the Traditional Latin Mass, favored by those who wish to refer to it without political implication. Used in Benedict XVI's documents and in mainstream traditional Catholic discourse. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### *Versus Populum*
Latin: "toward the people." The posture of celebration in which the priest faces the congregation across the altar. Became standard practice after the 1969 reform, though not strictly required by the text of the new Missal. The SSPX argues this orientation expresses a "meal" theology rather than a "sacrifice" theology; Cardinal Ratzinger agreed this change was theologically significant. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

## Category IV: Historical and Institutional Terms

---

### Angelus Press
The SSPX's English-language publishing house, based in Kansas City, Missouri. Publishes *The Angelus* magazine, Lefebvre's books, the works of Michael Davies and other traditionalist authors, and a wide range of traditional Catholic religious and devotional materials. The primary commercial source for SSPX-related English-language books. [→ §13 Source Library]

---

### Assisi Gatherings (1986, 2002, 2011)
Interreligious prayer gatherings convened by John Paul II and (in 2011) Benedict XVI at Assisi, bringing together religious leaders from dozens of traditions to pray for peace. Each group prayed according to its own tradition, in close proximity to Catholic sacred spaces. Archbishop Lefebvre identified the 1986 Assisi event as a turning point in his decision to proceed with the 1988 consecrations - citing it as a visible manifestation of false ecumenism. [→ §2 Timeline; §6 Position 5]

---

### *Consilium*
The *Consilium ad exsequendam Constitutionem de sacra Liturgia* - the commission established by Paul VI in 1964 to implement *Sacrosanctum Concilium*. Under the direction of Archbishop Annibale Bugnini, the Consilium produced the Novus Ordo Missae (1969). Its proceedings were largely private; six Protestant observers participated in a consultative capacity. The Consilium's methods and composition are subjects of traditionalist criticism. [→ §4 TLM vs Novus Ordo]

---

### *Cor Unum*
The internal circular distributed by the SSPX Superior General to Society priests; not publicly available but occasionally quoted in traditional Catholic media. The most direct expression of the Society's internal governance communications. [→ §13 Source Library]

### *Ecclesia Dei* Commission (Pontifical Commission *Ecclesia Dei*)
The commission established by John Paul II in 1988 to care for Catholics attached to the traditional liturgy and to facilitate the return of SSPX communities to full communion. Absorbed into the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith (now the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith) in 2019 by Pope Francis. The commission was the primary Vatican contact point for the SSPX from 1988 to 2019. [→ §2 Timeline; §8 Papal Documents]

---

### *Fideliter*
The French-language SSPX journal; the primary periodical for the Society's French-speaking communities. Contains articles on theology, pastoral matters, and SSPX institutional life. [→ §13 Source Library]

---

### FSSP (Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter)
Founded in 1988 by eleven SSPX priests who broke from the Society over the episcopal consecrations; fully canonical, celebrating exclusively the Traditional Latin Mass. The most prominent alternative to the SSPX for Catholics attached to the traditional liturgy; significantly affected by *Traditionis Custodes* (2021). The FSSP's existence and growth is the mainstream critic's primary empirical counter-argument to the SSPX's claim that its institutional response was necessary. [→ §1 Overview; §3 Key Figures; §11 Case Against; §12 Debate]

---

### ICKSP (Institute of Christ the King Sovereign Priest)
A fully canonical traditional Catholic institute, founded in 1990 in Gabon and now headquartered in Florence, Italy; known for especially solemn traditional liturgical celebration. Like the FSSP, demonstrates that traditional Catholicism can be preserved within canonical communion. [→ §1 Overview]

---

### Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification (1999)
A joint declaration by the Catholic Church and the Lutheran World Federation stating that they had reached "a consensus in basic truths of the doctrine of justification" - the doctrine that had been at the center of the 16th-century Reformation controversy. The SSPX and other traditionalists criticized this declaration as obscuring genuine remaining theological differences between Catholic and Protestant soteriology. [→ §5 *Unitatis Redintegratio*; §6 Position 5]

## Quick Reference: The Most Commonly Confused Terms

| Term A | Term B | How to distinguish them |
|---|---|---|
| Valid | Licit | *Valid* = really happens; *licit* = legally done. SSPX sacraments are valid but some are technically illicit. |
| Schism | Heresy | *Schism* = refusal of submission to authority; *heresy* = denial of defined doctrine. SSPX is accused of the former, not the latter. |
| Schism | Canonical irregularity | *Schism* = formal break; *canonical irregularity* = operating outside structures without formal penalty. The SSPX is irregular, not formally schismatic. |
| *Latae sententiae* | *Ferendae sententiae* | *Latae* = automatic penalty; *ferendae* = imposed by declaration. The 1988 excommunication was *latae sententiae*. |
| Excommunication | Suspension | *Excommunication* = exclusion from sacramental life and ministry; *suspension* = barred from priestly functions but not from sacramental life as a faithful. Lefebvre was suspended in 1976; the bishops were excommunicated in 1988. |
| Ordinary Form | Extraordinary Form | *Ordinary Form* = Novus Ordo (1969 Missal); *Extraordinary Form* = Traditional Latin Mass (1962 Missal). Language established by *Summorum Pontificum* (2007), modified by *Traditionis Custodes* (2021). |
| State of necessity | Disobedience | *State of necessity* = canonical exemption from penalty due to grave need; *disobedience* = violation of a legitimate command. The SSPX argues the consecrations were the former; critics say they were the latter. |
| Sedevacantism | SSPX position | *Sedevacantism* = the papal chair is currently vacant; *SSPX position* = the pope is validly the pope; his specific commands may be resisted in limited cases. These are fundamentally different positions. |
| Magisterium | Tradition | *Magisterium* = the Church's living teaching authority; *Tradition* = the deposit of Revelation transmitted through the life of the Church. The Magisterium serves and transmits Tradition; it does not create it. |
| Propitiatory sacrifice | Memorial | *Propitiatory sacrifice* = the Mass truly offers Christ to the Father for the remission of sins; *memorial* = the Mass commemorates the Last Supper without itself being an act of sacrifice. The former is Catholic teaching; the latter is (roughly) the Protestant position Trent condemned. |
