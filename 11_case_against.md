# Section 11: The Case Against the SSPX
## The SSPX Compendium

> **How to use this section.** This section is the mirror image of §10. It presents the strongest possible case against the SSPX's position: what a thoughtful, well-formed mainstream Catholic critic would say, with the best evidence and reasoning marshaled honestly. As with §10, this is steelmanning: presenting the opposing view at its most powerful, not its weakest. After this section, §12 brings the two cases into direct dialectical encounter, claim by claim. Read §10 first; this section assumes you have engaged the case for. Reading §11 without §10 first will give you a one-sided picture; reading §10 without §11 will do the same in the opposite direction. The point of these three sections together is to give you the full evidentiary terrain on which any honest stance must be built.

---

## A Note on the Format

This section is written from within the position of the SSPX's most serious critics. The voice here is not the voice of internet polemicists, sedevacantist enemies, or anti-traditional progressives. It is the voice of a serious mainstream Catholic theologian: someone who loves the Catholic Church, accepts Vatican II as a legitimate exercise of the ordinary Magisterium, recognizes the genuine problems in the post-conciliar period, but holds that the SSPX's institutional response constitutes a grave error that has done substantial harm to the Catholic Church and to many of those who attend its chapels.

The critic being represented here is the kind of critic the SSPX itself takes most seriously: Cardinal Müller in some of his statements, Cardinal Sarah at his most thoughtful, the broader pre-conciliar Roman theological tradition speaking through its post-conciliar inheritors. This is not the case made by those who hate tradition. It is the case made by those who love tradition but believe the SSPX has betrayed it in a specific and serious way.

Counterarguments to each point are real and substantial. Many of them appear in §10. Here we let the critic speak.

## Part I: The Foundational Objection

### The Core Counter-Claim

The Catholic Church is not merely a deposit of doctrine; it is also a living, visible, hierarchical society, the Mystical Body of Christ on earth, ordered by Christ Himself under the supreme pastoral authority of the Bishop of Rome. The Catholic faith is not transmitted by ideas alone but by the visible communion of the Church through her sacraments, her hierarchy, and her unity around the See of Peter.

The Second Vatican Council was a legitimate ecumenical council of the Catholic Church. Its documents, whatever their pastoral or dogmatic weight, were promulgated by the Roman Pontiff in union with the bishops of the universal Church, the highest organ of teaching authority short of solemn definition. They are owed religious submission of will and intellect (*religiosum voluntatis et intellectus obsequium*) per Canon 752 of the Code of Canon Law and the consistent teaching of the Church on the Magisterium.

A Catholic who finds himself in apparent disagreement with a Council teaching has, properly, three options:

1. Submit his judgment to the Magisterium, presuming the apparent contradiction is his own failure of understanding.
2. Continue to study the matter in fidelity to the Church, presuming that what the Magisterium teaches can be reconciled with what the Magisterium has previously taught, even if he cannot yet see the reconciliation himself.
3. Express his concerns through legitimate channels (theological dialogue, respectful correspondence with bishops and Rome, scholarly work) while remaining in full communion and obedience.

What he may not do is the fourth option: conclude unilaterally that the Council's teaching contradicts prior teaching, declare that he therefore knows better than the universal Church about how to receive the Council, and proceed to act on that judgment by establishing structures parallel to the legitimate hierarchy.

This is what the Society of Saint Pius X has done. Its founder consecrated bishops without papal mandate in direct defiance of an explicit prohibition. Its current bishops continue this practice. Its priests operate without canonical mission. Its faithful are formed in a posture of suspicion toward the actual visible Catholic Church under the actual visible Pope. Whatever the SSPX's claims to be "simply Catholic," its actual practice has been to set its own judgment, exercised through Archbishop Lefebvre and his successors, above the judgment of the universal Catholic Church.

This is not the classical Catholic doctrine of resistance to gravely erring superiors. It is something different: a sustained, institutional, multi-generational refusal to submit to the Catholic Church in her current pastoral and doctrinal life, justified by appeal to the SSPX's own reading of a Council whose authentic interpretation belongs to the Magisterium, not to a single bishop or his Society.

This is the foundational objection. Everything else follows.

## Part II: The Argument From the Nature of the Magisterium

### What the Catholic Church Actually Teaches About Authority

The Catholic Church teaches that Christ founded a visible Church on the foundation of Peter, that He gave to Peter and his successors the keys of the Kingdom, and that the Roman Pontiff possesses supreme, ordinary, immediate, and universal jurisdiction over the entire Church. This is not a piece of post-conciliar theology. It was solemnly defined at the First Vatican Council in *Pastor Aeternus* (1870):

> "Wherefore we teach and declare that, by divine ordinance, the Roman church possesses a pre-eminence of ordinary power over every other church, and that this jurisdictional power of the Roman pontiff is both episcopal and immediate. Both clergy and faithful, of whatever rite and dignity, both singly and collectively, are bound to submit to this power by the duty of hierarchical subordination and true obedience, and this not only in matters concerning faith and morals, but also in those which regard the discipline and government of the church throughout the world."
>
> — *Pastor Aeternus* (First Vatican Council, 1870), ch. 3

The same Council declared:

> "So, then, if anyone says that the Roman pontiff has merely an office of supervision and guidance, and not the full and supreme power of jurisdiction over the whole church, and this not only in matters of faith and morals, but also in those which concern the discipline and government of the church dispersed throughout the whole world; or that he has only the principal part, but not the absolute fullness, of this supreme power; or that this power of his is not ordinary and immediate both over all and each of the churches and over all and each of the pastors and faithful: let him be anathema."
>
> — *Pastor Aeternus* (First Vatican Council, 1870), ch. 3, canon

This teaching is at the same level of authority as the dogmas of the Trinity and the Incarnation. It is *de fide divina et catholica*. To deny it is heresy.

Now, *Pastor Aeternus* does not authorize the pope to teach error or to command Catholics to sin. But it does teach that the pope's authority is supreme, universal, and immediate, and that all the faithful owe him "true obedience" in matters pertaining not only to faith and morals but also to "the discipline and government of the church."

When the pope, in the legitimate exercise of his office, prohibits a bishop from consecrating other bishops, as John Paul II did in 1988, that prohibition falls squarely within "the discipline and government of the church." It is precisely the kind of papal command that Vatican I says is owed "true obedience" by all the faithful, "singly and collectively."

To disobey that command, no matter how strong the disobedient party's reasons appear to themselves, is to act against the very nature of the Catholic Church as defined by *Pastor Aeternus*.

### The Limits of Classical Resistance Theology

The SSPX appeals to Bellarmine, Cajetan, and Suárez to justify its resistance. This appeal is selective.

Bellarmine, Cajetan, and Suárez, like the entire pre-Vatican-I tradition they represent, wrote *before* the dogmatic definition of papal primacy at Vatican I. Their reflections on resistance were developed in a theological context where the precise scope of papal jurisdiction was not yet defined. After 1870, the framework changes.

More importantly, none of these theologians authorized the kind of action Lefebvre took. Bellarmine speaks of *resistance*: refusing to obey, impeding the execution of a harmful will. He does not authorize positive acts that themselves require papal authority (such as the consecration of bishops). Resistance is essentially passive: refusing to do what the pope wrongly commands. The 1988 consecrations were not passive. They were a positive act, performed in direct defiance of an explicit papal prohibition, that produced bishops standing outside the Church's normal canonical structures.

Bellarmine also explicitly grounds the right of resistance in defense against immediate harm to the faith. The 1988 consecrations were not such a defense. They were a long-term institutional decision to perpetuate a structure outside the visible Church. This is not what the classical theologians authorized.

Furthermore, the classical theologians teach resistance to specific commands one cannot in good conscience obey. They do not teach the creation of parallel institutions. A subject may refuse to celebrate the new Mass if he believes it gravely sinful. He may not consecrate bishops to establish a parallel rite he prefers. The first is resistance; the second is institution-building, which requires authority the subject does not possess.

### The Limits of the State of Necessity

The state of necessity (Canon 1323 §4) is a real canonical principle, but its application is constrained.

First, "necessity" in canon law means *strict* necessity: a situation in which no other course of action is available. In 1988, this condition was not met. Rome had offered, in the May 5 Protocol, to consecrate a bishop for the SSPX. Lefebvre signed the agreement. He then retracted his signature within 24 hours, not because Rome had reneged, but because he did not trust Rome to follow through. This is not strict necessity; it is preemptive action based on suspicion.

Second, the necessity must be such that the act in question is the *only* available means of preventing the harm. In 1988, even if Lefebvre had not consecrated bishops, the priests he had already ordained would have continued ministering for decades. The traditional Mass would have continued to be celebrated. The SSPX would have continued to exist. Episcopal succession could have been secured through negotiation with Rome, even if delayed. The consecrations were a choice for a particular form of preservation, not the only possible preservation.

Third, the act must not be "intrinsically evil" or "tend to the harm of souls." The SSPX argues consecration without mandate is not intrinsically evil. This is true at the level of the bare act. But the consecrations *tended to the harm of souls* in a specific sense: they created a continuing structure of irregular ministry that has, for nearly four decades, drawn Catholics into a posture of suspicion toward the visible Catholic Church. This is itself a form of harm to souls: the harm of being formed in attitudes incompatible with full Catholic communion.

Fourth, the state of necessity cannot be invoked *unilaterally* by the subject of the law. If any subject could decide for himself that necessity justifies disobedience, no canonical law would bind anyone. The principle requires a genuinely objective necessity, not a subjective judgment. The judgment that a state of necessity exists must be defensible to the broader Catholic community, not merely to the disobedient party.

In 1988, the broader Catholic community, including thoughtful traditionalists who shared most of Lefebvre's concerns, did not agree that the conditions for the state of necessity were met. The bishops who broke from the SSPX over the consecrations and formed the FSSP (Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter) were not less attached to tradition than Lefebvre. They simply judged that the consecrations were not justified. Their judgment has been substantially vindicated by the subsequent history: the FSSP and similar communities have preserved traditional Catholicism within full canonical communion, demonstrating that the SSPX's institutional response was not necessary.

## Part III: The Argument From the FSSP and the Alternative Path

### The Counterfactual That Actually Happened

In 1988, twelve SSPX priests broke from the Society over the episcopal consecrations and approached Rome seeking canonical regularization. Cardinal Ratzinger, with the approval of John Paul II, established the *Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter* (FSSP) to receive them, a fully canonical traditional priestly fraternity with the explicit charism of celebrating the Traditional Latin Mass and the pre-conciliar sacraments.

Over the subsequent decades, multiple traditional communities have emerged within full canonical communion:

- The Priestly Fraternity of Saint Peter (FSSP)
- The Institute of Christ the King Sovereign Priest (ICKSP)
- The Personal Apostolic Administration of Saint John Mary Vianney (Campos, Brazil)
- The Institute of the Good Shepherd (IBP)
- Various traditional communities of nuns, monks, and friars
- Diocesan personal parishes dedicated to the Traditional Mass
- The Old Mass communities established under *Summorum Pontificum* (2007)

These communities, taken together, demonstrate that traditional Catholicism does not require the SSPX's institutional posture. The Traditional Mass has been preserved. Traditional priestly formation has continued. Traditional families have been formed and sustained. Vocations have been attracted. All of this has happened *within* full Catholic communion: without episcopal consecrations against papal will, without priests deprived of canonical mission, without the cultivation of suspicion toward Rome.

This is the empirical refutation of the SSPX's central practical claim: that without the 1988 consecrations, traditional Catholicism would have died out. It did not die out. It survived in canonical communion. The SSPX's institutional response was not necessary because the alternative was demonstrated to be possible.

### The FSSP and the Vindication

The FSSP today has hundreds of priests, multiple seminaries, parishes across the world, and vocations comparable to or exceeding those of the SSPX. Its priests celebrate exclusively the Traditional Mass. Its formation is doctrinally serious. Its families are observant. The pastoral fruit the SSPX claims as evidence in its favor (vocations, formation, observance) is present in the FSSP and other canonically regular traditional communities in measure at least comparable to the SSPX itself.

This empirical fact has serious consequences for the SSPX's defense. The SSPX argues that its irregular status was necessary to produce the spiritual fruit it has produced. The existence of the FSSP and similar communities, producing similar fruit *within* canonical regularity, suggests that the SSPX's irregularity was not necessary for the fruit. The fruit could have been, and has been, produced through legitimate channels.

What the SSPX has, that the FSSP does not have, is a distinctive *posture* toward the post-conciliar Church and toward Vatican II. The SSPX has cultivated a stance of theological resistance to specific Vatican II teachings. The FSSP has not. The FSSP accepts Vatican II while maintaining the traditional liturgy and formation.

If the SSPX's distinctive contribution is not preserving tradition (which the FSSP has demonstrated can be done in canonical communion) but maintaining a particular theological posture toward Vatican II, then the question becomes: is that theological posture itself worth the cost of the canonical irregularity? Is the rejection of specific Council teachings, at the level of institutional self-definition, so important that it justifies forty years of irregular ministry, the formation of generations of Catholics in suspicion toward the visible Church, and the public scandal of an irregular structure within Catholicism?

The mainstream Catholic critic answers: no. The traditional liturgy and formation could be preserved through canonical communities. The theological dispute over Vatican II can be pursued through scholarly and ecclesial dialogue without requiring institutional separation. The SSPX's specific choice to make its theological posture the basis of an irregular institution was not necessary for the preservation of tradition. It was a choice, and it was the wrong choice.

## Part IV: The Argument From the Pope-and-Bishops as Constituent of the Church

### The Visible Church

The Catholic Church is not an idea or a set of doctrines. It is the Mystical Body of Christ, a visible society constituted by sacramental communion, professed faith, and submission to legitimate authority. Pope Pius XII taught this with great clarity in *Mystici Corporis Christi* (1943):

> "Actually only those are to be included as members of the Church who have been baptized and profess the true faith, and who have not been so unfortunate as to separate themselves from the unity of the Body, or been excluded by legitimate authority for grave faults committed."
>
> — *Mystici Corporis Christi* (1943), §22

Three things make one a member of the Catholic Church: baptism, profession of the true faith, and submission to legitimate authority (visible communion with the bishops and Pope).

The SSPX claims the first two: their members are baptized and profess what they take to be the true faith. The question of the third, submission to legitimate authority, is precisely what the SSPX's institutional structure compromises. The Society's bishops were consecrated against the explicit will of the Pope, who possesses the supreme jurisdiction defined at Vatican I. Its priests minister without the canonical mission that comes from communion with the diocesan bishop. Its faithful are formed in a posture of treating the visible Catholic hierarchy as suspect on grounds that the SSPX itself has decided.

This is not a small canonical detail. It goes to the visible structure of the Church as Christ founded it.

When *Lumen Gentium* §8 teaches that the Church of Christ "subsists in the Catholic Church, which is governed by the successor of Peter and by the Bishops in communion with him" (a teaching the SSPX accepts), it identifies the visible Catholic Church under the Pope as the locus where the Church of Christ subsists. To withdraw from full communion with the Bishop of Rome (even in a partial, irregular way) is to withdraw from the visible structure where the Church subsists.

The SSPX argues it has not withdrawn: it recognizes the Pope as Pope, prays for him at every Mass, and so on. But communion with the Pope is not merely a matter of recognition. It is also a matter of submission to his jurisdiction, especially in matters of the discipline and government of the Church. When the Pope explicitly prohibits an act and the act is performed anyway, communion with the Pope is *materially* broken in that act, however much it is *formally* affirmed in word.

### The Problem of Cherry-Picking the Magisterium

The SSPX's framework requires the Catholic to accept the prior definitive Magisterium while resisting the current Magisterium where the SSPX judges that there is contradiction. This framework presupposes that the individual Catholic, or at minimum the SSPX bishops as a small interpretive community, can reliably determine when the current Magisterium contradicts the prior, and act on that determination.

But this is to make the individual Catholic (or the SSPX) the supreme interpreter of the Magisterium. It is precisely the Protestant move, exalting private judgment above the Church's living teaching authority, dressed up in traditionalist clothing.

The Catholic position is different. The Magisterium *itself* is the proper interpreter of the Magisterium. The Church's authentic interpretation of her own prior teaching is what the current Magisterium provides. Where there appears to be tension, the resolution must come from the Magisterium, not from a private theologian, a private bishop, or a society of priests.

This is not blind obedience. It is recognition of where authority lies. A Catholic may hold his own theological views, may publish them, may discuss them with brother Catholics, may submit them to the Magisterium for evaluation. What he may not do is *act on his own judgment* as if it were the judgment of the Church, particularly when his action constitutes a public, institutional contradiction of the current papal magisterium.

The SSPX has, for nearly four decades, done precisely this. Its leadership has determined that specific Council teachings contradict prior teaching, that this contradiction justifies canonical irregularity, and that the irregularity must continue until Rome accepts the SSPX's interpretation of the Council. This makes the SSPX, in effect, an alternative magisterium, claiming to know better than the visible Catholic Church how the Catholic faith is to be received and transmitted.

This is incompatible with Catholic ecclesiology, however carefully it is framed.

## Part V: The Argument From What the SSPX Actually Forms in Its Faithful

### The Spiritual Pattern

The SSPX claims spiritual fruit: vocations, observant families, formed Catholics. This claim has substance, as §10 noted. But it is also incomplete. The full picture of what the SSPX forms in its faithful must include the patterns that are less flattering.

A significant proportion of Catholics formed by long-term SSPX engagement develop a particular set of theological and pastoral instincts:

- A reflexive suspicion of any post-conciliar Vatican document, regardless of its content.
- An assumption that the current Magisterium is presumptively wrong on contested questions, requiring affirmative proof to be accepted.
- A tendency to identify "Catholicism" with pre-conciliar forms, such that post-conciliar Catholic life is treated as a deviation rather than an integral (if imperfect) expression of the Church.
- A culture of grievance: the conviction that the SSPX is heroic, that the post-conciliar Church is corrupted, that the broader Catholic world fails to appreciate the SSPX's witness.
- Difficulty engaging respectfully with the wider Catholic community, including with serious traditional Catholics in the FSSP, ICKSP, and diocesan TLM communities.
- A tendency toward sectarian sensibility, treating the SSPX as the "real Church" within the broader (compromised) Church.

These are not universal features of SSPX life. Many SSPX faithful are exemplary in their charity, their humility, and their engagement with the broader Catholic community. But the patterns described above are well-documented in pastoral experience, including by traditional Catholic priests who have ministered both within and outside the SSPX.

The question for the SSPX is: what is the cost of these formation patterns? If a significant portion of those formed by the SSPX develop a sectarian, grievance-oriented posture toward the visible Catholic Church, this is itself a serious pastoral harm. It is a harm not merely to the individuals (who suffer the spiritual costs of a sectarian disposition) but to the broader Church (who must engage these Catholics, and who suffer their public criticism and their isolation).

### The Williamson Phenomenon

The 2009–2012 Williamson controversy is not an aberration; it is symptomatic of a deeper pattern. Bishop Williamson, one of the four 1988 bishops, became publicly notorious for Holocaust denial and a range of conspiracy theories. He was eventually expelled from the SSPX. But the underlying issue, that an SSPX bishop could hold and publicly proclaim such views for years without significant intervention from his Society, points to a culture of insufficient intellectual discipline.

When a community defines itself in opposition to the broader Church and develops a strong sense of being the faithful remnant against the corrupted mainstream, it becomes vulnerable to the cultivation of broader counter-cultural and conspiratorial habits of thought. The Williamson phenomenon is the most prominent example, but lesser versions (anti-Vatican-II conspiracism, sympathy for political movements incompatible with Catholic social teaching, hostility toward Jewish-Catholic reconciliation that goes beyond legitimate critique of post-conciliar formulations) appear with some regularity in SSPX-adjacent media and discourse.

The SSPX leadership has, in recent years, taken steps to address this. Fr. Pagliarani has been notably more disciplined in his public theology than some predecessors. Williamson's expulsion was a step in the right direction. But the structural condition that produced the Williamson phenomenon (a community defined by opposition, formed in suspicion, operating outside normal canonical accountability) has not been fully addressed.

### The Counter-Argument and the Response

The SSPX may respond: but every Catholic community has its weaknesses; many post-conciliar Catholics have developed equally problematic patterns (heterodoxy, indifferentism, moral laxity). This is true. The post-conciliar mainstream has its own pathologies. But the existence of pathologies elsewhere does not excuse the pathologies of the SSPX. And the specific pathologies the SSPX produces (sectarian disposition toward the visible Church, conspiratorial habits of thought, formation in opposition) are *distinctive* to the SSPX situation and would be substantially mitigated if the Society's faithful were formed within full canonical communion.

The FSSP, the ICKSP, and the diocesan TLM communities form Catholics in traditional liturgy and traditional doctrine *without* the sectarian pattern. The reason is structural: they are in full canonical communion, their priests are accountable to bishops in normal canonical relationships, their faithful are part of the broader Catholic community even as they cultivate the traditional patrimony.

The SSPX's structural irregularity is not incidental to its formation patterns. It produces them. This is a cost the case-for-the-SSPX must acknowledge, and which §10 did not.

## Part VI: The Argument From What the Real Threats to Catholicism Have Required

### The Question of Solidarity

The post-conciliar period has not been principally a struggle between traditionalists and modernists about Vatican II. It has been a struggle for the soul of the Catholic Church against forces far more destructive than disputed conciliar passages: militant secularism, sexual revolution, abortion industry, persecution of religious freedom, growing hostility to Christian witness in the public square.

These struggles have required, and continue to require, the unity of all Catholic forces. The pro-life movement, the defense of marriage, the protection of religious liberty, the formation of Catholic schools and universities, the witness against the moral catastrophes of our age: all of these have demanded Catholic solidarity across liturgical and theological preferences.

In these struggles, where has the SSPX been?

The honest answer: largely absent at the institutional level. The SSPX has not been a significant force in the pro-life movement (compared, for instance, to mainstream Catholic dioceses, the FSSP, the broader pro-life Catholic infrastructure). It has not been a major presence in defending Catholic schools and universities against secular pressures. It has not been a leading voice in the public defense of religious liberty against governmental encroachments. It has been, by and large, an inward-facing community focused on its own preservation rather than the broader Catholic engagement with the modern world.

This is not entirely the SSPX's fault. Its canonical irregularity limits the platforms it can occupy. But the irregularity is a choice, one that has had institutional consequences, including limited engagement with the great Catholic struggles of our age.

The mainstream Catholic critic argues: the energy and zeal the SSPX has spent on internal preservation, on theological disputes about Vatican II, and on the cultivation of its institutional identity could have been spent, and might have been more spiritually fruitful, on engagement with the actual threats facing the Catholic Church and the Catholic faithful. The SSPX's focus on its specific disputes, however genuine those disputes are, has come at the cost of broader Catholic witness.

### The Public Image of Catholicism

The existence of the SSPX as a public anomaly (a Catholic community in irregular relationship with Rome, with its own bishops consecrated against papal will, operating in a canonical gray zone) contributes to the broader public confusion about what the Catholic Church is and what she teaches. To non-Catholic observers, and to many Catholics, the SSPX situation suggests that the Catholic Church is internally divided, that the question of who is and is not in communion with Rome is unsettled, that Catholicism itself may be fragmented in ways that fundamentally compromise her claim to be the one true Church.

This is a public-witness harm. The Catholic claim is that the Church is one: visibly one, hierarchically one, sacramentally one under Peter. The SSPX's existence as a recognized-but-irregular structure undermines this claim, regardless of the SSPX's intentions. To the world, Catholicism appears more fractured because of the SSPX's existence. This is a real cost, paid by the broader Catholic community, that the SSPX's defenders rarely acknowledge.

## Part VII: The Argument From the Actual Dispute

### The Real Theological Stakes

The mainstream Catholic critic does not need to argue that Vatican II is perfect or that every post-conciliar development has been wise. The critic can acknowledge the post-conciliar crisis, the liturgical excesses, the catechetical failures, the genuine tensions between specific Council passages and prior magisterial teaching. None of these acknowledgments require accepting the SSPX's institutional response.

The dispute, properly framed, is not about whether the post-conciliar period has problems. Everyone acknowledges that. The dispute is about whether those problems justify what the SSPX has done.

The mainstream critic's argument: the problems are real, but the SSPX's response is disproportionate and counterproductive. Real problems require real responses, but real responses must be proportionate to the problems and must work within the Catholic Church rather than around her.

The SSPX has responded to real problems with an institutional structure that, in the critic's analysis, is:

- **Disproportionate**: the consecration of bishops against papal will to address problems that could have been addressed through legitimate channels (theological dialogue, canonical traditional communities, scholarly engagement).
- **Counterproductive**: producing a community whose distinctive features (sectarian disposition, suspicion of legitimate authority, formation in opposition) are themselves spiritually problematic.
- **Foreclosing**: making it harder, not easier, to address the real problems by removing the SSPX's distinctive theological perspective from the legitimate magisterial conversation.

A theological dispute about the proper interpretation of *Dignitatis Humanae* is one thing. The dispute is real, it deserves engagement, it should be addressed by competent magisterial authorities. The SSPX's specific contributions to this dispute have genuine value at the level of theological argument.

But a theological dispute does not justify an institutional irregularity that has persisted for nearly four decades. The Catholic way of handling theological dispute is through patient scholarship, respectful dialogue, submission of one's views for evaluation, and trust in the long-term work of the Holy Spirit through legitimate Church structures. The SSPX has substituted for this Catholic way a different method: institutional separation, public assertion that the SSPX is right and Rome is wrong, refusal to accept canonical regularization on Roman terms, perpetual claim of necessity.

This is not the Catholic way. Whatever the merits of the SSPX's specific theological positions, the way they have pursued those positions, through institutional irregularity rather than through legitimate Catholic dialogue, has been wrong.

## Part VIII: The Argument From What Rome Has Actually Asked

### The 2012 Refusal in Perspective

The SSPX's strongest moment was 2012. Rome offered, through the Doctrinal Preamble, canonical regularization. Bishop Fellay accepted in principle. The Society could have become a personal prelature, with its bishops, its priests, its faithful, its Mass, fully in communion with Rome.

The SSPX refused. Not because Rome demanded anything beyond what is required of every Catholic: acceptance of Vatican II as a legitimate exercise of the Magisterium, with the recognition that its teachings must be interpreted in continuity with prior teaching but cannot be rejected as if they were not authentic Church teaching.

The terms Rome offered were what Rome could not, in fact, offer less of. The Catholic Church cannot regularize a Society that, as a matter of institutional self-definition, refuses to receive the teaching of an ecumenical council. To do so would be to compromise the very authority of the council, the principle of submission to legitimate Magisterium, and the visible unity of the Church.

The SSPX refused these minimal terms. The SSPX leadership judged that even this minimal acceptance of Vatican II would compromise the Society's mission.

But this is precisely the problem. If the Society's mission cannot accommodate the minimal acceptance of Vatican II that Rome requires for canonical regularity, then the Society's mission is not, after all, simply "Catholic." It is Catholic with a specific institutional position toward Vatican II that the Catholic Church herself does not recognize as a legitimate position for a fully Catholic community to hold.

The SSPX is, on this analysis, defining its identity in a way that places it outside what the Catholic Church can accommodate within full communion. This is not Rome's stubbornness. It is the SSPX's choice, a choice to make a particular theological posture toward Vatican II essential to its identity in a way that no other Catholic community has done.

### Why Rome Cannot Yield Further

The mainstream critic argues: Rome has been remarkably patient. The excommunications were lifted in 2009, a major concession that the SSPX has not reciprocated with any institutional acceptance of Vatican II. The sacramental faculties have been normalized, addressing the practical pastoral problem without requiring SSPX submission. *Summorum Pontificum* freed the Traditional Mass, addressing the central liturgical concern.

Rome has done what Rome can do. What Rome cannot do is regularize a Society that, as a matter of identity, refuses to accept the legitimate teaching of an ecumenical council. To do so would be to undermine the council itself, the principle of magisterial authority, and the visible unity of the Church.

The barrier to regularization is therefore not on Rome's side. It is on the SSPX's. As long as the Society defines itself by opposition to specific Council teachings, it cannot be regularized, not because Rome refuses but because the SSPX has made regularization incompatible with its self-understanding.

If the SSPX truly sought reunion with Rome, the path would be open: acceptance of Vatican II as authentic Church teaching, while continuing to engage its specific passages theologically within the legitimate framework of Catholic scholarship and ecclesial dialogue. Many serious theologians do exactly this. They accept the Council's authority while raising substantive questions about its interpretation. Cardinal Müller is an example. Various pre-conciliar-formed theologians in Rome itself are examples. The path is real and walkable.

The SSPX has not taken this path. The reason cannot be that the path does not exist. The reason must be that the Society has chosen a different path, and that choice has consequences for the canonical situation it now occupies.

## Part IX: The Cumulative Counter-Case

The case against the SSPX is not a single argument but a cumulative case. Taken together, the arguments converge on a serious critique:

- **The Magisterium argument** establishes that the SSPX's framework misrepresents how Catholic authority actually works. The Magisterium itself is the proper interpreter of the Magisterium. The SSPX has made itself an alternative magisterium.
- **The FSSP argument** establishes that the SSPX's institutional response was not necessary. Traditional Catholicism could be, and has been, preserved within canonical communion.
- **The visible Church argument** establishes that the SSPX's institutional posture undermines the visible communion that constitutes the Catholic Church. Recognition of the pope in word, combined with persistent disobedience in act, is not communion in the Catholic sense.
- **The formation argument** establishes that the SSPX has produced not only spiritual fruit but spiritual harm: a culture of suspicion, sectarianism, and grievance that is a serious pastoral problem.
- **The solidarity argument** establishes that the SSPX has, by its institutional self-isolation, largely absented itself from the great Catholic struggles of our age, weakening rather than strengthening the Catholic witness against the modern world.
- **The dispute argument** establishes that even the genuine theological concerns of the SSPX could have been pursued through legitimate Catholic channels rather than through institutional irregularity.
- **The 2012 argument** establishes that the barrier to regularization is on the SSPX's side, not Rome's. Rome has done what it can. The SSPX has chosen its institutional identity over the path to reconciliation.

The cumulative case is therefore not a desperate condemnation but a serious Catholic critique. The SSPX is not a heretical sect. Its members are not damned. Its sacraments are valid. But its institutional response to the post-conciliar situation has been wrong: disproportionate, counterproductive, and ultimately incompatible with the Catholic Church's visible unity. It has produced real spiritual fruit at the cost of real spiritual harm, preserved tradition while fragmenting the Church, and resisted error by separating from the visible Catholic Church under Peter.

### The Critic's Personal Note

The mainstream critic does not write with satisfaction. The critic loves the Traditional Mass. The critic acknowledges the post-conciliar crisis. The critic has read Lefebvre's writings and recognizes the genuine pastoral seriousness in them. The critic does not dismiss the SSPX's concerns as frivolous.

The critic writes with sadness, sadness that a community of genuine Catholics, holding much that is true and beautiful in the Catholic tradition, has been led by its particular reading of Vatican II into an institutional posture that the Catholic Church cannot, in good conscience, accept. The critic prays for the SSPX's regularization. The critic believes such regularization is possible. The critic believes it would require, from the SSPX, what every Catholic must accept: that the Catholic Church, not the individual believer, is the proper interpreter of her own teaching.

Until that acceptance is forthcoming, the SSPX will remain what it has been for nearly four decades: a sign of contradiction and a wound, a genuine Catholic community in an irregular state, a preserver of much that is true and a source of much that is problematic.

The Catholic critic prays for the day when the wound is healed. That day requires the SSPX to come home, not to abandon its concerns but to pursue them within the Catholic Church that Christ founded, in full communion with the visible Successor of Peter who, however imperfectly, holds the office Christ gave to Peter and to his successors forever.

This is the case against the SSPX. It is serious. It deserves engagement. The SSPX's responses to it are also serious. Those responses are taken up in §12.
