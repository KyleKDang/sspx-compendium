# The Case for the Society of Saint Pius X

The Society of Saint Pius X is the largest community of traditional Catholic priests in the world. Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre founded it in 1970 to form priests and to keep the Roman Mass, the sacraments, and Catholic doctrine as they stood before the Second Vatican Council. Today it has more than 730 priests, six seminaries, and hundreds of Mass centers around the world. For more than fifty years it has carried on this work outside the ordinary structures of the Church, in a dispute with Rome that has never been resolved.

The matters in dispute are specific: the validity of the Society's sacraments, its position under canon law, the nature of its resistance to certain papal decisions, and the merit of its objections to the Second Vatican Council. This essay addresses each and makes the case that the Society's position is serious and defensible, grounded in Catholic doctrine, canon law, and the Church's own history.

## A precedent from the Church's own history

The Society's defenders often appeal to the Arian crisis of the fourth century. The comparison is worth taking seriously, because it establishes something the rest of the argument depends on.

After the Council of Nicaea defined the divinity of Christ in 325, the Arian heresy that denied it was not quickly defeated. For several decades it spread through the hierarchy, until a large share of the world's bishops had either accepted it or signed compromise formulas to keep the peace. St. Jerome summarized those years in one sentence: "The whole world groaned and was astonished to find itself Arian." St. Athanasius of Alexandria, who held to the Nicene faith, was driven from his diocese repeatedly. Around the year 357, Pope Liberius, worn down by imperial pressure and exile, subscribed to an ambiguous formula that fell short of the Nicene faith and agreed to the condemnation of Athanasius.

Athanasius did not conclude from this that Liberius had ceased to be pope, and he did not start a separate church. He kept teaching the faith and outlasted the crisis. He is now a canonized saint and a Doctor of the Church.

The Church has always been careful to note that Liberius never defined heresy. His failure was a personal act under coercion, not a solemn use of the teaching office, which is why it leaves the doctrine of papal infallibility intact. That careful distinction is exactly what makes the episode useful. It shows that the Church has long accepted three things that sound surprising when stated plainly. A validly reigning pope can fail gravely under pressure without ceasing to be pope. A Catholic can be condemned by churchmen, including a pope acting wrongly, and still be the one holding the faith. And resisting such a failure is not the same act as leaving the Church.

The SSPX applies this precedent to its own situation. Archbishop Marcel Lefebvre, who founded the Society in 1970, returned to the example of Athanasius often, and the Society's own publications draw the parallel directly. The comparison does not prove the SSPX is correct in the way Athanasius was correct. It proves something narrower: the position the Society claims to occupy is a recognized one, with precedent in the Church's own history. Whether the Society has a serious case for occupying it is the subject of the rest of this essay.

## The crisis behind the Society's position

Every part of the Society's case rests on one factual claim: that the Catholic Church has been in a serious crisis since the Second Vatican Council (1962 to 1965). If that claim were false, the Society would have little ground to stand on. So it is worth establishing that the claim is well documented and is not unique to the SSPX.

The measurable figures, for the United States, are these. Weekly Mass attendance among Catholics was near 75 percent in the late 1950s; by 2019 it was about 21 percent (Gallup). The number of priests fell from roughly 49,000 in 1965 to roughly 35,000 six decades later, while their average age rose steeply. Women in religious life fell from roughly 180,000 to roughly 35,000. In 2019 the Pew Research Center found that only 31 percent of self-identified Catholics believed that the bread and wine of the Eucharist become the Body and Blood of Christ.

The diagnosis is not confined to traditionalists. Paul VI, who promulgated the post-conciliar reforms, said in 1972 that "the smoke of Satan has entered the temple of God." Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, before he became Benedict XVI, wrote in his memoir that the crisis in the Church was "to a large extent due to the disintegration of the liturgy."

The SSPX does not claim the Council caused all of this by itself. Other factors plainly contributed, including the broad collapse of religious practice across the West. The Society's claim is narrower. The decline is real, it coincides closely with the post-conciliar period, and it cannot be addressed honestly by treating that period as a success. A Catholic who finds the decline alarming is not being disloyal in doing so. The SSPX is simply the part of the Church that refused to treat it as normal.

## The Mass

The most visible feature of the SSPX is that it celebrates the traditional Latin Mass, the Roman Mass as it stood before the 1969 reform. This is often described as an attachment to Latin and ceremony. The Society's reasons are doctrinal, and they follow from teaching the whole Church holds.

The Catholic Church teaches that the Mass is a sacrifice, the same sacrifice Christ offered on the Cross, made present again on the altar in an unbloody manner. It is not primarily a communal meal. The Council of Trent defined this and attached an anathema, the Church's strongest censure, to its denial. The wording, from Session XXII in 1562, is:

> "If anyone says that the sacrifice of the Mass is one only of praise and thanksgiving; or that it is a mere commemoration of the sacrifice consummated on the cross, but not a propitiatory one... let him be anathema."

Behind the Society's concern is an old principle, *lex orandi, lex credendi*, meaning that the way the Church prays shapes what the Church believes. If the prayers of the Mass change, the faith of those who pray them tends to change with them over time. The SSPX argues that the 1969 revision did exactly this. The explicitly sacrificial prayers of the Offertory were replaced, the priest was increasingly described as a "presider," and the language was generally softened. The doctrine was never formally denied, but it was, in the Society's view, expressed less clearly, and a less clear expression produces a weaker faith in practice. The 2019 figure on belief in the Real Presence is the kind of outcome the Society predicts.

This criticism did not begin with the SSPX. In 1969, two months before the new Mass took effect, a critical study was sent to Paul VI signed by Cardinal Alfredo Ottaviani, the former head of the Holy Office, and Cardinal Antonio Bacci. It argued that the new rite "represents, both as a whole and in its details, a striking departure from the Catholic theology of the Holy Mass as it was formulated in Session XXII of the Council of Trent." These were senior figures of the Roman Curia, with no connection to Lefebvre.

Parts of the criticism have since been confirmed by Rome itself. For years, Catholics attached to the older Mass were told it had been abolished. In 2007, Benedict XVI stated in *Summorum Pontificum* that it had "never been juridically abrogated," that is, never actually abolished in law. He added that "what earlier generations held as sacred, remains sacred and great for us too." When post-conciliar translations rendered the words of consecration as Christ's blood poured out "for all," the SSPX argued that the Latin and the Gospels say "for many," and in 2006 Rome ordered the translation corrected to "for many."

Lefebvre called the older rite simply "the Mass of all time." His point was that it had never been a special concession or a relic to be tolerated. It was the Church's ordinary inheritance, the Mass of countless canonized saints, and by Rome's own admission it was never lawfully abolished. Continuing to celebrate it is therefore not, in itself, an act of rebellion.

## The doctrinal question

The dispute is not finally about the Mass. Underneath it lies a doctrinal question, and Benedict XVI identified it as such. In his 2009 letter to the world's bishops he wrote that the problems with the SSPX "are essentially doctrinal in nature."

The principle at issue is simple to state: the Catholic faith cannot contradict itself. What the Church has solemnly and universally taught as true cannot later become false. If it could, it was not the truth to begin with, and the Church's teaching could never be trusted.

The traditional test for what belongs to that unchangeable body of teaching comes from St. Vincent of Lérins in the fifth century. Authentic Catholic doctrine is what has been believed "everywhere, always, and by all." A teaching that meets that test, held across the whole Church and down the centuries by the agreement of popes and councils, belongs to the deposit of faith and cannot afterward be reversed.

The clearest case the SSPX raises concerns religious liberty and the place of the Church in public life. For more than a century before the Council, the popes taught consistently that civil society owes recognition to the true religion and that there is no genuine right to spread religious error. Pius IX, in the Syllabus of Errors (1864), condemned the proposition that "every man is free to embrace and profess that religion which, guided by the light of reason, he shall consider true." Leo XIII, in *Immortale Dei* (1885), taught that the state has real duties toward the true religion and may not treat all religions as equal before the law. Pius XI instituted the Feast of Christ the King in 1925 to teach that "the law of Christ ought to prevail in human society."

The Council's declaration *Dignitatis Humanae* (1965) then taught that there is a right to religious freedom founded "in the very dignity of the human person," to be protected as a civil right. The SSPX holds that this cannot be reconciled, at the level of plain meaning, with the earlier teaching.

The Society's response is not to discard the Council, and it is not the claim that a Catholic may believe whatever he prefers. Its position is that when a more recent teaching of lower authority appears to contradict an older teaching of higher authority, a Catholic holds to the one that carries greater weight while the apparent contradiction is worked out. That is not private judgment. Private judgment means a Catholic preferring his own opinion to the Church's. The SSPX is doing close to the reverse: it refuses to let the teaching of one period overturn the consistent teaching of many.

This is also why the label "cafeteria Catholic" does not fit. A cafeteria Catholic sets aside demanding doctrines in favor of convenient ones. The SSPX holds to the more demanding and less convenient teaching when an easier formulation is on offer. One can argue that the Society is mistaken about whether the two teachings actually conflict, and that is a real argument. But Benedict XVI's own description of the dispute as doctrinal concedes that it is a serious one, not mere stubbornness.

## The charge of schism

The most common objection is that the SSPX is in schism. It deserves a direct answer, and the answer is found in the Church's own law.

The Code of Canon Law defines schism precisely, in Canon 751:

> "Schism is the refusal of submission to the Supreme Pontiff or of communion with the members of the Church subject to him."

Schism is the rejection of the pope's authority as such, or the refusal of communion with Catholics under him. It is not disobedience to a particular instruction, and it is not criticism of how a pope governs. Classical Catholic theology draws the same line. St. Thomas Aquinas treats schism as a sin against the unity of the Church, distinct from simple disobedience. A subject who refuses a specific command has done something different in kind from a subject who denies that the authority exists at all.

Measured against that definition, the SSPX does not fit it. It affirms that the pope is the pope; it recognized John Paul II, Benedict XVI, and Francis, and it recognizes Leo XIV now. It prays for the reigning pope by name at every Mass. It rejects sedevacantism, the claim that the See of Peter is vacant, and has removed clergy who held that view. It professes the doctrines the Catholic Church has defined. What it does is refuse specific directives it judges harmful to the faith, while continuing to affirm the authority that issues them.

That posture has a name in classical theology, and it is not schism. St. Robert Bellarmine, a Doctor of the Church, addressed it directly in his treatise on the papacy, *De Romano Pontifice* (Book II, Chapter 29):

> "Just as it is licit to resist the Pontiff who attacks the body, so also is it licit to resist him who attacks souls or destroys the civil order or, above all, tries to destroy the Church. I say that it is licit to resist him by not doing what he orders and by impeding the execution of his will."

Bellarmine then sets the boundary: "It is not licit, however, to judge him, to punish him, or to depose him." Resist a harmful command, but do not declare the office vacant or attempt to remove the man who holds it. That is the same line the SSPX draws. Cajetan and Suárez, two of the most respected theologians of the post-Reformation period, taught a similar distinction.

The precedent the Society returns to most often is in Scripture. In the Letter to the Galatians, St. Paul records that he confronted St. Peter, the first pope, in public:

> "But when Cephas came to Antioch, I opposed him to his face, because he stood condemned." (Galatians 2:11)

Paul never questioned Peter's authority or his office. He judged that Peter's conduct at Antioch endangered the truth of the Gospel, and he resisted it openly. The episode is recorded in the inspired text of Scripture without disapproval. The SSPX describes its own stance with the term "filial resistance," meaning the resistance of a son rather than the opposition of an enemy. A son who corrects his father has not stopped being his son.

Rome's own conduct, for long stretches, has matched this reading. Cardinal Darío Castrillón Hoyos, who as head of the Pontifical Commission *Ecclesia Dei* was the Vatican official responsible for the SSPX, said publicly that the Society's situation was "not a formal schism." Through the 1990s, the 2000s, and the 2010s, Rome did not declare the Society schismatic.

The present moment should be stated plainly rather than skipped over. In February 2026 the SSPX announced that it would consecrate four new bishops on July 1, 2026, and in May 2026 the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith warned that doing so would be "a schismatic act." That warning is real and current. But it is the same charge, resting on the same canonical question, that was raised in 1988, and the SSPX's answer is the same answer it gave then. Understanding that answer means understanding what happened in 1988.

## The 1988 consecrations

By 1988 Lefebvre was 82 years old. A bishop is the only minister who can ordain priests, and the Society needed bishops if it was to keep ordaining priests after Lefebvre's death. Without them, the priests he had formed would eventually have no one to ordain their successors, and within a generation the traditional Mass and sacraments would become unavailable to the faithful who depended on the Society.

A protocol of agreement with Rome was in fact signed in May 1988. It broke down within days, over the terms for consecrating a bishop for the Society. Rome would consecrate one, but on a timetable and of a candidate that Rome alone would control. On the basis of how the previous two decades of negotiations had gone, Lefebvre concluded that the promise would not be kept and that the Society would be slowly absorbed. Whether that judgment was correct is debatable. It was, at least, not unreasonable given the history.

On June 30, 1988, he consecrated four bishops without the papal mandate that canon law requires. He did not present the act as ordinary. He called it "Operation Survival," and said that signing the agreement he no longer trusted would have been "Operation Suicide" for the traditional movement. In the consecration sermon he stated directly that "we find ourselves in a case of necessity."

That phrase points to a category in the Church's own law. Canon 1323 of the Code of Canon Law lists those who are exempt from a canonical penalty, including:

> "...a person who acted... out of necessity or grave inconvenience, unless the act is intrinsically evil or tends to the harm of souls."

The Church's own legislation exempts from penalty a person who acts out of necessity, on two conditions: the act must not be intrinsically evil, and it must not tend to the harm of souls. The SSPX's argument is therefore not a loophole the Society invented. It is the Church's own provision.

Apply the two conditions. Consecrating a bishop is not intrinsically evil; it is an ordinary and holy act that the Church performs constantly. And the Society argues that the consecrations did not harm souls but served them, by keeping the sacraments available to hundreds of thousands of Catholics. If both conditions hold, then Canon 1323 means the penalty does not apply.

Canon lawyers have disagreed, in good faith, about whether the necessity in 1988 was grave enough to satisfy the canon. That disagreement is genuine, and this essay does not pretend it away. But the shape of the act is worth noticing. Lefebvre did not set up a rival pope and did not declare the See of Rome vacant. He consecrated bishops for one stated and limited purpose, the continuation of priestly ordinations, and then left the act to the judgment of Rome and of history. The consecrations announced for 2026 rest on the same reasoning. There are two living SSPX bishops, both near seventy, and the problem of succession has returned in the same form.

## The sacraments

For most Catholics, the practical questions matter more than the canonical theory. There are four, and the answers are reassuring, because they rest on Rome's own decisions rather than on the Society's claims.

Is an SSPX Mass a valid Mass? Yes, and this has never been seriously disputed, including by Rome. A valid Mass requires a validly ordained priest, valid matter, the words of consecration, and the intention to do what the Church does. SSPX priests are validly ordained in an unbroken apostolic line. At an SSPX Mass, Christ becomes truly present, and the Eucharist received there is truly his Body and Blood.

Does attending an SSPX Mass fulfill the Sunday obligation? Yes. Canon 1248 of the Code of Canon Law states that the obligation "is satisfied by assistance at a Mass which is celebrated anywhere in a Catholic rite." The traditional Latin Mass is a Catholic rite, as Benedict XVI confirmed. Canon lawyers across the spectrum agree that attendance satisfies the obligation.

Are confessions at an SSPX chapel valid? Yes. Pope Francis granted SSPX priests the faculty to absolve sins validly and licitly, first for the Jubilee Year in 2015 and then permanently in 2016. He wrote that he did so "lest anyone ever be without the Sacrament of Reconciliation through the Church's pardon" (*Misericordia et Misera*, 2016). A confession made to an SSPX priest is valid, and the absolution is real.

Can a marriage be celebrated at an SSPX chapel? Yes, with one practical step. Since a 2017 instruction from Pope Francis, the local bishop is to grant SSPX priests the delegation needed to witness marriages. Where that delegation is in place, and the Society arranges it routinely, the marriage is valid and licit. The only advice is to confirm the delegation with the chapel in advance.

Baptism, confirmation, and the anointing of the sick administered by SSPX clergy are likewise valid. The practical conclusion is that a Catholic who turns to the SSPX is not cut off from the grace of the sacraments. Rome's own decisions, not the Society's assurances, are what establish this.

## What Rome has done

The relationship between Rome and the SSPX over nearly four decades is not well described as a steady Vatican facing a stubborn sect. For long stretches it is better described as Rome moving toward positions the Society already held.

In 1988 the document *Ecclesia Dei* called the consecrations a "schismatic act" and described the SSPX's idea of Tradition as "incomplete and contradictory." That was the strongest charge Rome made.

In 2007, *Summorum Pontificum* stated that the older Mass had never been juridically abrogated, confirming the Society's central liturgical claim. In 2009 the excommunications of the four bishops were lifted. In the letter to the bishops that accompanied that decision, Benedict XVI explained that the remaining obstacle was doctrinal rather than disciplinary, meaning a genuine theological disagreement rather than a matter of punishment. In 2016 Francis made the SSPX's confession faculties permanent, and in 2017 he provided the framework for its marriages.

The other side of the record should be stated too. The same 2009 letter said that the SSPX has no canonical status and that its clergy "do not legitimately exercise any ministry in the Church." The Society's institutional irregularity is real, and the Society itself does not deny it. And in May 2026, with the new consecrations approaching, Rome again used the language of schism.

Even the hard language, though, concedes something. Rome describes the central dispute as doctrinal. That means Rome itself does not treat the SSPX as a group that simply will not obey, but as one holding a theological position serious enough to require a theological answer. For most of the past two decades Rome has, in practice, treated the Society as roughly what it claims to be: a Catholic body in an irregular situation, with a genuine grievance that has not yet been resolved.

## The question of fruits

Christ proposed a test that applies here: "By their fruits you shall know them" (Matthew 7:16).

While diocesan seminaries emptied, the SSPX's six seminaries continued to receive vocations. The Society now has more than 730 priests and serves the faithful at over 750 Mass centers worldwide. Its congregations tend to be young, with large families and with children who can explain their faith. After 2021, when access to the traditional Mass within the dioceses was restricted, the Society grew again, because in many places it had become the only available source of that Mass.

This does not by itself prove that the SSPX is doctrinally correct. Fruitfulness is evidence, not proof. But it is evidence, and it tells against the picture of the Society as a barren or rebellious fringe. A community that forms observant and well-catechized Catholics, at a time when much of the Church struggles to do so, has at least earned the right to be taken seriously.

## Conclusion

The case for the SSPX can be summarized plainly. It celebrates a Mass that Rome admits was never abolished. It holds doctrines the Church taught for centuries and has never repealed. It recognizes the pope, prays for him by name, and rejects the claim that the office is vacant. It resists specific directives on grounds that Bellarmine, Cajetan, Suárez, and the example of St. Paul support. Its sacraments are valid, its confessions licit by the pope's own grant, and its Masses sufficient for the Sunday obligation. It grounded its founding act in a provision of the Church's own canon law. And over nearly forty years Rome has lifted its penalties, confirmed its central liturgical claim, secured its sacraments, and described the remaining dispute as doctrinal.

Lefebvre stated the Society's intention in one sentence: "We are not founding a new religion or a new church. We are simply Catholics, doing what Catholics have always done."

None of this requires the conclusion that a particular Catholic should attend an SSPX chapel. The Society's canonical situation is genuinely irregular, the current tension with Rome is real, and the right course for any individual depends on his own circumstances. A serious Catholic can study all of this and still conclude that the Society chose the wrong path.

What the evidence does not support is the short summary, schismatic and disobedient and excommunicated, offered as a full account of the Society. The penalties were lifted in 2009. The Vatican official responsible for the Society said it was not in formal schism. Its disobedience is the refusal of particular commands, not the denial of the authority that issues them. The SSPX may be mistaken, but if it is mistaken, it is mistaken in the way a serious Catholic position can be mistaken. That is the difference between a question worth arguing and a label worth repeating.
